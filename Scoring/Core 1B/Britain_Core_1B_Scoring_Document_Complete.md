**Britain Core 1B Scoring Document**

### 1. Rule & Methodology (Locked)

**Core 1B Definition**  
Measures the observable degree to which voluntary economic actions (contracts, property rights, production, trade, investment, consumption) are protected from or restricted by state or collective coercion.

**Taxation Treatment**  
Taxation is scored separately using the standard method:

**Taxation Score = (1 − Government Tax Income / GDP) × 100**

**Final Scoring Formula**  
\[
A = \frac{\text{Regulation Score} + \text{Taxation Score}}{2}
\]
\[
L = \min(\text{Regulation Score}, \text{Taxation Score})
\]
\[
d = \frac{|\text{Regulation Score} - \text{Taxation Score}|}{100}
\]
\[
F = (1 - d) \cdot A + d \cdot L
\]

Final score \( F \) is rounded up to the nearest whole number.

### 4. Policies Active by Era

#### Day Zero: Post-War Consensus (Pre-1979)

**Neutered Base Policy Descriptions**

**Nationalised Industries**  
Large sections of the UK economy (including coal, steel, railways, shipbuilding, aerospace, electricity, gas, and parts of the automotive industry) were under direct state ownership and operational control. The government set prices, directed investment, and influenced output and employment levels in these sectors.

**Trade Union Power, Closed Shops & Secondary Action**  
Trade unions enjoyed strong legal protections, including the widespread operation of closed shops (requiring workers to join a union) and the right to take secondary industrial action. Unions had significant statutory rights to recognition and collective bargaining that constrained employer discretion over hiring, firing, and workplace rules.

**Exchange Controls & Capital Controls**  
Strict legal restrictions existed on the movement of capital, foreign currency transactions, and overseas investment by UK residents and companies. Most international financial transfers required administrative approval from the Bank of England or Treasury.

**High Marginal Income Tax Rates**  
The income tax system featured very high marginal rates, with the top rate reaching 83% on earned income plus an additional investment income surcharge. This created substantial fiscal extraction and strong disincentives for additional work, saving, and entrepreneurial activity.

**Rent Controls & Security of Tenure**  
The Rent Act framework imposed statutory rent controls and provided tenants with strong security of tenure, significantly limiting landlords’ ability to set market rents or regain possession of their properties except in narrowly defined circumstances.

**Incomes Policies & Price Controls**  
Successive governments imposed statutory or quasi-statutory limits on wage and price increases as part of anti-inflation policy. These policies were enforced through administrative mechanisms and applied across large parts of the economy.

**Town & Country Planning Act + Compulsory Purchase**  
Development of land was subject to a comprehensive planning permission system administered by local authorities. The state also held extensive compulsory purchase powers to acquire land for public projects, overriding private property rights.

**Agricultural Support & Production Steering**  
Agriculture operated under a system of price supports, production grants, and other state interventions designed to influence output levels, farm incomes, and land use. These measures created conditional incentives tied to specific production decisions.

**Health and Safety at Work Act 1974**  
Employers were placed under general statutory duties to ensure, so far as reasonably practicable, the health, safety, and welfare of employees and others affected by their activities. The regime was enforced through criminal sanctions and created significant compliance obligations and operational restrictions.

**Unfair Dismissal & Employment Protection**  
Employees gained the statutory right not to be unfairly dismissed, with remedies available through industrial tribunals. Employers were required to follow fair procedures, and dismissal decisions were subject to external review on substantive and procedural grounds.

**Competition Policy**  
Merger control and monopoly investigations were conducted under the Fair Trading Act 1973 framework. The system was relatively discretionary, with significant scope for political and administrative influence over outcomes.

**Banking & Financial Regulation**  
Banks and building societies operated under a system of prudential supervision and conduct regulation. The regime imposed constraints on lending practices, capital requirements, and the range of activities financial institutions could undertake.

**Public Procurement & National Preference**  
Government purchasing followed formal tendering processes but frequently incorporated preferences for domestic suppliers and nationalised industries. Non-price criteria played a significant role in contract awards.

**IP / Patent & Copyright Protection**  
Legal rights existed to protect inventions, designs, and creative works, with mechanisms for enforcement against infringement. Owners could commercially exploit these rights through licensing and assignment with relatively limited state interference.

**Corporation Tax & Business Taxation**  
Companies were subject to corporation tax with various allowances, reliefs, and anti-avoidance rules. The interaction between corporate and personal taxation created incentives and distortions affecting business form, investment, and profit distribution decisions.

**Lived-Experience Alignment (Late 1970s)**  
Firms operated under significant constraints on pricing, investment, labour utilisation, and international capital movements. Nationalised industries dominated key sectors, often with chronic under-investment and political interference in commercial decisions. Private employers faced powerful unions with closed shops and the ability to take secondary action, limiting flexibility in hiring, firing, and workplace organisation. Strict exchange controls restricted international expansion and capital raising. High marginal tax rates and incomes policies distorted incentives for work, profit retention, and investment. Rent controls and planning restrictions limited property market dynamism. While some protections (such as unfair dismissal rules and health and safety duties) provided stability, the overall environment was one of constrained managerial discretion and high compliance costs.

### 3. Individual Policy Scores – Audit Notes

Individual policy scores were reviewed under the strict Negative-Only rule. Most were retained where they accurately reflected the coercion created by that specific policy. 

Notable adjustments:
- Policies previously given strong positive weighting for being “liberalising” (e.g. certain privatisation elements, exchange control abolition, major tax cuts) had their contribution to the era holistic score moderated. They still reduce overall drag but do not receive positive scoring.
- Extreme restriction policies in Era 5 (business closures, lockdowns, furlough prohibition on working) remain at very low levels, consistent with their high coercion.

**Routing & Exclusion Summary — Britain Core 1B**

### 1. Routing Principles

All policies and elements in this document have been assessed according to **Scope Purity** and **dominant mechanical effect**.

- **Core 1B (Voluntary Economic Action)**: Elements whose primary mechanical effect is on contracts, property rights, production, trade, investment, pricing, or labour allocation between economic actors.
- **Core 1A (Personal Autonomy)**: Elements whose dominant effect is on individual personal security, bodily autonomy, daily personal decision-making, or individual-level protections that do not primarily affect economic allocation or voluntary exchange.
- **Core 2A (National Sovereignty)**: Elements whose dominant concern is national authority over borders, external economic influence, or protection from foreign coercion.

Only the **dominant** mechanical effect determines routing. Secondary or incidental effects are not used to override the primary classification.

### 2. Routing Decisions by Category

#### Routed to Core 1A (Personal Autonomy)
- Certain individual-level impacts of employment protection rules (e.g. personal remedies for unfair dismissal in some contexts).
- Personal security and daily decision-making aspects of workplace COVID restrictions (e.g. mandatory work-from-home rules affecting individual movement and association).
- Individual-level conditionality in welfare or furlough schemes where the primary effect is on personal behaviour rather than broad economic allocation.

These elements were routed to Core 1A only where the dominant mechanical impact was on the individual rather than on voluntary economic exchange or production decisions.

#### Routed to Core 2A (National Sovereignty)
- Certain aspects of Brexit-related regulatory divergence and customs arrangements (secondary sovereignty dimension).
- Capital controls and exchange controls where the primary concern was national authority over external economic flows.

These were assessed as having a secondary Core 2A dimension but were retained in Core 1B where the dominant observable effect on businesses and individuals remained economic (investment planning, trade friction, compliance costs).

#### Retained in Core 1B (Voluntary Economic Action)
The vast majority of elements across all eras remain in Core 1B because their dominant mechanical effect is on:
- Labour market flexibility and contractual freedom between employers and employees
- Investment and production decisions
- Pricing and resource allocation
- Fiscal extraction and business formation
- Regulatory steering of economic activity

Examples include:
- Nationalised industries and privatisation
- Trade union power and closed shops
- Planning and compulsory purchase
- Tax policy (personal and corporate)
- Energy and industrial policy steering
- Most employment regulation (when the primary effect is on workforce allocation and flexibility)
- Financial regulation and competition policy

### 3. Exclusion from Scoring

No elements were placed fully **Outside of Scope** without clear justification. The following types of content were excluded from Core 1B scoring:

- Purely rhetorical or symbolic statements with no mechanical effect on voluntary economic action.
- Purely administrative or procedural changes with negligible observable impact on economic decisions.
- Temporary crisis measures whose time-limited nature significantly reduced their long-term mechanical weight (these were given reduced weighting rather than full exclusion).
