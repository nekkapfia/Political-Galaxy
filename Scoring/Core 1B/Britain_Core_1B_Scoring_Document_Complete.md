### 3. Policies Active by Era

## Day Zero: Post-War Consensus     | Pre-1979      

**Regulation Score: 42**  
**Taxation Score: 66**  
**Final Score: 53**

The post-war system featured extensive direct state ownership, price and wage controls, compulsory mechanisms, and significant restrictions on voluntary economic decision-making across large parts of the economy. Markets continued to operate in many areas, but the state played a leading role in key sectors with broad and persistent interventions.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Nationalised Industries – State ownership of major production assets: **34**  
- Nationalised Industries – State control over pricing and output: **32**  
- Trade Union Power – Legal protection of closed shops: **38**  
- Trade Union Power – Right to secondary industrial action: **37**  
- Exchange Controls & Capital Controls: **42**  
- Rent Controls & Security of Tenure: **48**  
- Incomes Policies – Limits on wage increases: **43**  
- Incomes Policies – Limits on price increases: **42**  
- Town & Country Planning + Compulsory Purchase: **51**  
- Health and Safety at Work Act 1974: **55**  
- Unfair Dismissal & Employment Protection: **53**  
- IP / Patent & Copyright Protection: **70**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (Late 1970s)**  
Firms faced significant constraints on pricing, investment, labour utilisation, and international capital movements. Nationalised industries dominated key sectors with political interference in commercial decisions. Private employers operated under powerful unions with closed shops and secondary action rights. Strict exchange controls and planning restrictions limited flexibility. The overall environment featured constrained managerial discretion and high compliance costs.

## Era 1: Thatcher Liberalisation    | 1979–1990  

**Regulation Score: 62**  
**Taxation Score: 68**  
**Final Score: 64**

This era saw substantial liberalisation through privatisation, the abolition of exchange controls, and trade union reforms. These changes materially increased voluntary economic decision-making, particularly in investment, labour allocation, and cross-border activity. However, the large welfare state and core employment protections remained in place.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Privatisation – Asset transfer & removal of state control: **69**  
- Trade Union Reform – Closed shops: **65**  
- Trade Union Reform – Secondary action limits: **66**  
- Abolition of Exchange Controls: **75**  
- Right to Buy: **68**  
- Continuation of core employment protections (H&S and Unfair Dismissal): **54**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (1979–1990)**  
Firms experienced a progressive increase in managerial discretion over pricing, investment, hiring, and international expansion. Nationalised industries moved toward commercial decision-making. Employers gained greater flexibility as closed shops were dismantled and secondary action was restricted. The abolition of exchange controls allowed freer capital movement.

## Era 2: Market Consolidation       | 1991–2007

**Regulation Score: 59**  
**Taxation Score: 65**  
**Final Score: 61**

This period represented consolidation after the major reforms of Era 1. Continued privatisation provided further liberalisation, but the introduction of the National Minimum Wage and modest expansion of employment rights added new constraints on labour market flexibility.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Continuation of Privatisation: **70**  
- National Minimum Wage (1999): **60**  
- Light-Touch Financial Regulation: **69**  
- Modest Expansion of Employment Rights: **62**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (1991–2007)**  
Firms operated in a broadly market-oriented environment with continued benefits from earlier privatisation. The National Minimum Wage created a new compliance floor in low-wage sectors. Light-touch financial regulation supported expansion until the mid-2000s. New employment rights added procedural requirements but did not fundamentally reverse labour market flexibility.

## Era 3: Financial Crisis & Austerity| 2008–2016  

**Regulation Score: 56**  
**Taxation Score: 65**  
**Final Score: 59**

The financial crisis led to increased state involvement in the banking sector through bailouts and higher capital requirements. Austerity reduced public spending, while welfare reforms introduced greater conditionality. The net effect was a modest decline in economic autonomy compared to Era 2.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Bank Bailouts and State Involvement: **59**  
- Higher Capital Requirements: **62**  
- Austerity – Public Spending Reductions: **63**  
- Welfare Reform and Conditionality: **60**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (2008–2016)**  
Firms faced a more constrained financial environment due to higher capital requirements and increased regulatory scrutiny. Austerity reduced public sector demand. Employment law changes added procedural complexity. Overall, businesses experienced a more regulated environment than in Era 2, particularly in finance.

## Era 4: Brexit Uncertainty        | 2017–early 2020 

**Regulation Score: 56**  
**Taxation Score: 64**  
**Final Score: 59**

Prolonged uncertainty over future trading arrangements and regulatory standards increased friction on investment and supply chain decisions. Continued net zero policy maintained regulatory pressure on certain sectors. The overall effect was a modest increase in constraints on voluntary economic planning.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Brexit-Related Regulatory Divergence Preparations: **63**  
- Brexit-Related Customs and Trade Arrangements: **61**  
- Continued Net Zero Policy: **60**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (2017–early 2020)**  
Businesses faced prolonged uncertainty over future trading arrangements and customs procedures. Many companies delayed investment or restructured supply chains. Net zero policy continued to influence investment decisions in energy-intensive sectors.

## Era 5: COVID Restrictions        | 2020–early 2022  

**Regulation Score: 24**  
**Taxation Score: 62**  
**Final Score: 40**

This period featured extensive state control over production, labour allocation, and consumption. Mandatory business closures, national lockdowns, and the furlough prohibition on working directly removed voluntary economic activity across large sections of the economy for a sustained period.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Mandatory Business Closures: **14**  
- Repeated National Lockdowns and Movement Restrictions: **15**  
- Workplace Enforcement – Mandatory Work from Home: **17**  
- Furlough Scheme – Prohibition on Working: **16**  
- Furlough Scheme – State Wage Subsidy: **53**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (2020–early 2022)**  
Large parts of the economy were legally prohibited from trading. Repeated lockdowns and movement restrictions severely limited normal voluntary economic activity. The rule preventing furloughed employees from working represented direct state prohibition of voluntary labour allocation.

## Era 6: Post-COVID & Energy | 2022–2024     

**Regulation Score: 53**  
**Taxation Score: 63**  
**Final Score: 57**

The wind-down of COVID restrictions improved autonomy, but new energy price caps and continued net zero policy maintained state direction in key sectors. The net result was a partial recovery from Era 5 but still below pre-pandemic levels of economic autonomy.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Wind-down of COVID Support Schemes: **66**  
- Energy Crisis Interventions (Price Caps): **59**  
- Continued Net Zero Policy: **60**  
- Planning System Reforms: **65**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (2022–2024)**  
Firms navigated the removal of COVID restrictions alongside new government-imposed price controls in energy. Net zero policy continued to shape investment decisions. Planning reforms offered some acceleration but retained significant state oversight.

## Era 7: Current Government Reforms | 2024/2025 – present  

**Regulation Score: 51**  
**Taxation Score: 62**  
**Final Score: 55**

New workers’ rights expansions, strengthened collective bargaining, and green industrial strategy have increased state influence over labour markets and industrial direction. Planning reforms provide some liberalisation, but the overall direction is toward greater state involvement in key areas.

**Key Component Scores (Selected)**  
- Workers’ Rights Reforms (Unfair Dismissal, Flexible Working, Collective Bargaining): **58**  
- Planning System Overhaul: **64**  
- Green Industrial Strategy & Net Zero Acceleration: **57**

**Lived-Experience Alignment (2024/2025 – Present)**  
Businesses face new procedural requirements in employment practices and increased state direction in strategic sectors through green industrial policy. Planning reforms offer faster pathways in some areas while retaining significant state influence over land use.

#### 5. Routing & Exclusion Summary — Britain Core 1B

**Routing Principles**
All elements were assessed according to **Scope Purity** and **dominant mechanical effect**. Only the primary effect determines routing.

**Routing Decisions**

**Routed to Core 1A (Personal Autonomy)**  
- Individual-level impacts of employment protections (e.g. personal remedies for unfair dismissal)  
- Personal security and daily decision-making aspects of COVID workplace restrictions (e.g. mandatory work-from-home rules)  
- Individual-level welfare/furlough conditionality focused on personal behaviour

**Routed to Core 2A (National Sovereignty)**  
- Certain aspects of Brexit regulatory divergence and customs arrangements  
- Historical capital/exchange controls where national authority over external flows was the dominant concern

**Retained in Core 1B (Voluntary Economic Action)**  
The vast majority of policies across all eras remained in Core 1B because their dominant effect was on contracts, property rights, production, trade, investment, pricing, or labour allocation.

**Note on Taxation**  
All pure taxation measures (rates, thresholds, reliefs, enforcement) were removed from Regulation scoring and handled exclusively via the separate Taxation Score.

**Exclusions**
- Purely rhetorical or symbolic statements with no mechanical effect  
- Purely administrative/procedural changes with negligible observable impact  
- Temporary crisis measures whose long-term mechanical weight was significantly reduced (given reduced weighting)

**Summary of Routing Approach**

| Destination | When Applied | Examples |
|-------------|--------------|----------|
| **Core 1B** | Dominant effect on voluntary economic action | Nationalised industries, privatisation, trade union power, planning, energy/industrial policy, most employment regulation, financial regulation |
| **Core 1A** | Dominant effect on individual personal autonomy/security | Personal remedies for unfair dismissal, COVID work-from-home rules, individual welfare conditionality |
| **Core 2A** | Dominant concern is national sovereignty/external authority | Selected Brexit customs arrangements, historical capital controls |
All routing decisions were made on the basis of observable mechanical effect rather than policy framing or intent.
