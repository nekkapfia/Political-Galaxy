# Guide: How to Produce a Cultural 2A Timeline Document for a Country

This guide explains how to create a high-quality document analysing shifts in the allocation of outcomes, rights, responsibilities, and status according to individual merit versus group membership. It is designed to produce consistent, well-structured, and analytically rigorous results.

## 1. Preparation and Scope Definition

**Define the time period**
- Choose a clear starting point that marks a significant baseline (e.g. 1945 for Britain, or a major constitutional/legal change for other countries).
- Decide whether the document will run to the present day.

**Define the analytical focus clearly**
- The document analyses **observable mechanisms** of allocation.
- The central question is: To what extent are outcomes, rights, responsibilities, and status allocated on the basis of **individual actions, merit, character, and choices**, versus on the basis of **group membership**?

**Strict scope boundaries**
Do **not** analyse:
- Explanations for why disparities exist or what should be done about them (Cultural 4B)
- How the nation defines its core identity or “us” (Cultural 3A)
- Immigration, borders, or national sovereignty (Core 2A)
- Moral or ideological justifications (Cultural 1A)

## 2. Research and Data Collection

Gather evidence focused on **allocation mechanisms**, not justifications. Prioritise:

- Major legislation affecting equality, discrimination, and group-based policies
- Government policy documents and institutional guidelines
- Public sector practices (hiring, promotion, admissions, monitoring, diversity targets)
- Significant court rulings that change allocation rules
- Party manifestos and policy platforms (used mainly for the Political Parties section)

Focus on **what was actually done** in law and institutional practice rather than what was said.

## 3. Segmenting History into Eras (High Granularity First)

**Critical rule:** Start by creating **more eras than you initially think are necessary**. It is far easier to merge similar eras later than to split an era that has become too broad.

**Good reasons to create a new era:**
- Introduction or significant expansion of group-based allocation mechanisms (e.g. new equality legislation, widespread positive action, self-ID rules)
- Clear reduction or pushback against group-based allocation
- Major court rulings that meaningfully change allocation practices
- Periods of sustained political or institutional contestation over allocation principles

Aim for relatively short, coherent periods rather than long eras that contain multiple distinct phases.

## 4. Scoring Each Era

Use the official **Cultural 2A bands (0–100)**.

**Scoring approach:**
- Focus on the **routine and structural** role of group membership in allocation across core domains (law, employment, education, and institutional practice).
- Assess whether individual merit remains the default principle or is regularly balanced against or subordinated to group membership.
- Be consistent. Give greater weight to mechanisms that have broad, recurring effects rather than isolated or symbolic policies.
- Justify the score with reference to specific, observable mechanisms rather than general trends or rhetoric.

## 5. Writing the Era Sections

Use a consistent structure for every era. Maintain similar depth and analytical quality across all eras — later eras should not be noticeably thinner than earlier ones.

### Recommended Structure

**Era X: Year–Year – Era Name**  
**(Final Score: XX)**

**Brief Overview** (3–5 sentences)  
Summarise the overall character of allocation during this period and place it in context.

**Allocation Mechanisms**  
Describe the key observable mechanisms. Use bullet points and clearly distinguish between individualist and group-based approaches. Include specific examples from law and institutional practice.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of XX**  
Explain the score with direct reference to the Cultural 2A bands. Be specific about the extent, consistency, and impact of group-based allocation mechanisms versus individual merit-based allocation.

**Writing Standards:**
- Keep analytical depth consistent across all eras.
- Focus on observable allocation mechanisms rather than political justifications.
- Vary phrasing between eras to avoid repetitive language.
- Be precise and evidence-based.

## 6. Refining the Eras

After completing a first draft:

- Review the full set of eras and ask:
  - Can any eras be merged without losing important nuance?
  - Does any era contain distinct phases that would be better separated?
  - Are there periods where content could be better redistributed between adjacent eras?
- Merge eras that show very similar allocation patterns.
- Keep eras that represent meaningful shifts in the balance between individual and group-based allocation.
- Aim for the smallest number of eras that still preserves the important turning points and changes in direction.

## 7. Writing the Political Parties Section

Include a Political Parties section at the end of the document.

**Structure:**
- Short overall summary of the current state of allocation.
- Summary table with party names and Final Scores.
- Individual party entries using this format:
  - Brief Overview
  - Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis (with bullets)
  - Why This Produces a Final Score of XX

Base this section on current party platforms and positions, focusing on what each party proposes regarding the allocation of rights, opportunities, and status.

## 8. Final Assembly and Quality Control

**Include these elements in the final document:**
- Clear title
- Era Summary Table (Era, Era Name, Period, Title, Score, One-line Summary)
- Basic Analysis / Overview of the Timeline (should be analytical, not just descriptive)
- Era sections in chronological order
- Political Parties section

**Quality Standards:**
- All eras should be written to a similar depth and analytical standard.
- The Overview section should identify the overall trajectory and main drivers of change.
- Repetitive phrasing across eras should be minimised.
- Later eras must receive the same level of analytical attention as earlier eras.
- Scoring justifications must be tied to observable allocation mechanisms.

## 9. Recommended Number of Eras

| Time Span       | Recommended Number of Eras | Guidance |
|-----------------|----------------------------|----------|
| 30–50 years     | 6–10                       | Start with more, then merge |
| 70–80+ years    | 10–14                      | Start with 12–15, then refine |

## Summary Workflow

1. Define the time period and starting point.
2. Research observable allocation mechanisms (law and institutional practice).
3. Create an initial high-granularity set of eras.
4. Score each era using the Cultural 2A bands.
5. Write the era sections to a consistent standard.
6. Review and refine the eras (merge where appropriate).
7. Write the Political Parties section.
8. Write the Overview section (make it analytical).
9. Review the full document against the quality standards above.
10. Edit for repetition, consistency of depth, and analytical clarity.

This process is designed to produce a rigorous, consistent, and analytically strong Cultural 2A timeline document.
