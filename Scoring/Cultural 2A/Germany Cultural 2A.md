## Basic Analysis / Overview of the Timeline

Germany’s allocation of outcomes, rights, responsibilities, and status since 1998 has followed a distinct trajectory compared to Britain. While Britain developed group-based allocation mechanisms relatively early, Germany maintained a more formal and individualist approach for longer, with a sharper legislative shift occurring in the mid-2000s.

From 1998 to 2005, under the Schröder Red-Green government, allocation remained largely based on individual merit and formal equality, with only limited institutional use of group-based mechanisms. The decisive turning point came with the General Equal Treatment Act (AGG) in 2006, which introduced comprehensive anti-discrimination law and established group membership as a relevant factor in legal protections for the first time on a broad scale.

The following decade saw gradual institutional embedding of diversity policies, followed by more concrete group-based interventions, most notably the introduction of corporate gender quotas in 2015. From 2017 onwards, Germany entered a phase of increasing political contestation, particularly around gender self-identification and the proper scope of diversity policies. This contestation has been more limited in scope and intensity than in Britain, but it has nevertheless slowed the further expansion of group-based allocation.

Overall, Germany moved from a predominantly individualist and formal-legal system of allocation in the late 1990s and early 2000s, through a period of legislative and institutional expansion in the mid-2000s to mid-2010s, and into a more contested phase from the late 2010s onward, in which the balance between individual merit and group membership has become a subject of growing political debate.

## Era 1: 1998–2005 – Schröder Red-Green Government – Limited Group-Based Allocation
**(Final Score: 71)**

**Brief Overview**  
The Red-Green coalition under Gerhard Schröder pursued a modernisation agenda focused on economic reform and social policy, but allocation of opportunities and protections remained largely based on individual merit and formal equality, with only limited group-based mechanisms.

**Allocation Mechanisms**  
During this period, German law and institutional practice continued to emphasise formal equal treatment. There were no comprehensive anti-discrimination laws covering multiple grounds simultaneously, and positive action or diversity targets were not widely institutionalised in either the public or private sector. Allocation in employment, education, and public services was primarily based on individual qualifications and performance. While some targeted support measures existed for women and people with disabilities, these remained relatively narrow and did not represent broad group-based allocation. Group membership was not routinely used as a determining factor in access to opportunities.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of 71**  
This era scores in the low-to-mid 70s because allocation remained predominantly individualist. Group membership played only a marginal role in formal allocation mechanisms across law and institutions. While informal discrimination existed in practice, there were few institutionalised policies that systematically allocated opportunities or protections on the basis of group identity. Individual merit and formal qualifications remained the dominant principles. This produces a solid low-to-mid 70s score.

## Era 2: 2005–2009 – Grand Coalition – Introduction of the AGG
**(Final Score: 58)**

**Brief Overview**  
The Grand Coalition government passed the General Equal Treatment Act (Allgemeines Gleichbehandlungsgesetz – AGG) in 2006, Germany’s first comprehensive anti-discrimination law. This represented a significant expansion of group-based legal protections.

**Allocation Mechanisms**  
The AGG prohibited discrimination on grounds of race, ethnic origin, gender, religion or belief, disability, age, and sexual orientation in employment and much of civil law. It created new legal remedies and established the Federal Anti-Discrimination Agency. While the law was primarily prohibitive rather than positively allocative, it introduced the principle that group membership could legitimately influence legal protections and remedies on a broad scale. Some public institutions began developing initial diversity policies in response, though these remained relatively limited. Individual merit remained the formal default in most employment decisions, but group membership gained formal legal relevance for the first time across multiple protected characteristics.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of 58**  
This era shows a clear decline because the AGG created the first significant legal framework in which group membership could systematically affect the allocation of legal protections. Although the law did not introduce widespread quotas or positive action, it established group identity as a relevant category in allocation processes. Individual merit remained important in practice, but the new legal architecture made group-based considerations structurally relevant for the first time. This produces a high 50s score.

## Era 3: 2009–2013 – Merkel II – Gradual Institutional Embedding
**(Final Score: 55)**

**Brief Overview**  
During Angela Merkel’s second term, diversity and equal treatment policies became more embedded in public institutions, although there was no major new legislative expansion of group-based allocation.

**Allocation Mechanisms**  
The AGG remained in force without significant amendment. Some public sector organisations began expanding diversity monitoring and equal opportunities policies, but these developments were gradual rather than transformative. There was no significant increase in quotas or strong positive action measures during this period. Individual merit continued to be the dominant principle in most employment and educational decisions. Group membership was increasingly monitored and discussed in institutional contexts, but it did not yet play a major determining role in access to opportunities across most sectors.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of 55**  
This era shows a modest further decline as group-based considerations became more institutionally normalised, even in the absence of major new legislation. The gradual spread of diversity policies and monitoring made group membership a more routine factor in institutional thinking. While individual merit remained the primary allocation principle in most areas, the growing institutional infrastructure around equal treatment created conditions more favourable to group-based approaches. This produces a mid-50s score.

## Era 4: 2013–2017 – Merkel III – Expansion of Corporate Gender Quotas
**(Final Score: 49)**

**Brief Overview**  
This period saw the introduction of mandatory gender quotas for supervisory boards of large companies, representing Germany’s most concrete move toward positive group-based allocation in the corporate sector.

**Allocation Mechanisms**  
In 2015, Germany introduced a 30% gender quota for supervisory boards of listed companies subject to co-determination. This was a clear mechanism allocating positions partly on the basis of group membership (gender) rather than solely on individual merit. At the same time, diversity policies continued to develop gradually in the public sector. Individual merit remained the dominant principle in most areas of employment and education, but the corporate quota law marked a notable expansion of group-based allocation in one high-status domain.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of 49**  
This era scores in the high 40s because the introduction of mandatory gender quotas represented a clear step toward allocating important positions partly on the basis of group membership. While the quota was limited in scope, it established a precedent for group-based allocation in the private sector and signalled greater institutional acceptance of group membership as a relevant allocation factor. Individual merit remained the primary principle in most areas, but the quota created a significant exception in a visible and high-status domain. This produces a high 40s score.

## Era 5: 2017–2021 – Merkel IV – Heightened Contestation and Gender Debates
**(Final Score: 51)**

**Brief Overview**  
This period was characterised by increasing political debate over gender self-identification and the appropriate scope of diversity policies, while the core group-based legal mechanisms remained largely unchanged.

**Allocation Mechanisms**  
The government resisted full self-ID reforms for legal gender recognition. There was growing public and political debate about the balance between individual rights, biological sex, and gender identity, particularly in areas such as sports, prisons, and single-sex facilities. Some public institutions began reviewing or moderating certain diversity practices. At the same time, the corporate gender quota and the AGG remained in force. Individual merit and biological criteria gained greater prominence in political discourse, while group-based mechanisms continued to operate without major reform.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of 51**  
This era shows a slight improvement over the previous one. Growing political resistance to further expansion of self-ID and increased emphasis on biological sex in policy debates represented a partial reassertion of individual and biological criteria in contested areas. However, because the core group-based legal structures (including the corporate gender quota) were not significantly reformed, group membership continued to play a meaningful role in allocation. The result is a period of heightened contestation without major structural rollback, producing a low-to-mid 50s score.

---

## Era 6: 2021–Present – Scholz Traffic Light Coalition – Further Progressive Expansion
**(Final Score: 44)**

**Brief Overview**  
The Traffic Light coalition has pursued a more progressive approach to gender and diversity policy than its predecessor, including reforms to gender recognition law, while maintaining and in some areas expanding group-based allocation mechanisms.

**Allocation Mechanisms**  
The government passed reforms to the gender recognition law that moved Germany closer to a self-identification model. Diversity and inclusion policies have been strengthened in public institutions, and there has been continued support for gender quotas and other group-based measures. Group membership (particularly gender identity) has gained greater weight in determining legal status and access to certain provisions. Individual merit remains formally important, but it faces stronger competition from group-based considerations in a wider range of domains than during the previous government.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of 44**  
This era scores in the low-to-mid 40s because group-based allocation mechanisms have been further expanded, most notably through gender recognition reforms that increase the role of self-identified group membership in legal status and access. While individual merit continues to operate as a principle, the direction of policy has strengthened the institutional weight of group identity in allocation decisions across several areas. This produces a low-to-mid 40s score.

# Political Parties

Germany currently shows a mixed pattern on Cultural 2A. The legal framework established by the AGG and subsequent policies contains significant group-based allocation mechanisms, particularly in employment and corporate governance. Political contestation over these mechanisms has increased since the late 2010s, especially around gender identity. The parties differ substantially in how far they wish to extend or constrain group-based allocation.

## AfD – Final Score: 71

The AfD takes the strongest position against group-based allocation mechanisms among major parties.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

- Rejects most forms of positive action, diversity targets, and group-based preferences.
- Strongly opposes self-ID and emphasises biological sex as the relevant category.
- Advocates for allocation based on individual merit and performance.
- Views many existing equality and diversity policies as discriminatory against the majority population.
- Supports formal individual equality rather than group-based equity approaches.

The AfD consistently argues for allocation based on individual merit and rejects group membership as a legitimate basis for differential treatment in most contexts. This produces a solid low-to-mid 70s score.

## CDU/CSU – Final Score: 58

The CDU/CSU maintains the existing group-based legal framework while showing greater caution toward further expansion, particularly regarding gender self-identification.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

- Supports the continuation of the AGG and existing anti-discrimination law.
- Has accepted corporate gender quotas but resisted their significant expansion.
- Opposed full self-ID reforms and emphasised biological sex in areas such as sports and prisons.
- Generally favours individual merit as the primary principle in employment and education, while accepting some group-based protections.
- Shows increasing internal debate over the appropriate scope of diversity policies.

The CDU/CSU operates within the group-based legal architecture established since 2006 but has become more resistant to further expansion of self-identified group membership as a basis for allocation. It continues to support formal equality protections while placing greater emphasis on individual merit and biological criteria in contested areas. This produces a mid-to-high 50s score.

## Die Linke – Final Score: 35

Die Linke supports expansive group-based allocation mechanisms, often framed through an intersectional and class-conscious lens.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

- Strongly supports positive action and group-based measures to address structural disadvantage.
- Favours self-identification and expansive protections for marginalised groups.
- Views individual merit as insufficient without accompanying group-based interventions.
- Supports broadening and strengthening mechanisms that allocate on the basis of group identity.
- Often combines class-based and identity-based group allocation approaches.

Die Linke consistently prioritises group-based allocation over individual merit across multiple domains. Its positions systematically favour mechanisms that allocate rights and opportunities according to group membership. This produces a low-to-mid 30s score.

## FDP – Final Score: 62

The FDP places greater emphasis on individual merit and formal equality while accepting some group-based protections.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

- Supports the core anti-discrimination framework of the AGG.
- Generally opposes strong positive action and quotas beyond limited cases.
- Has been critical of expansive self-ID policies and emphasises individual rights.
- Favours individual merit and performance as the primary basis for allocation in employment and education.
- Supports formal equality of opportunity more than group-based equity measures.

The FDP maintains a more individualist orientation than most other parties while accepting the existing legal minimum of group-based protections. It consistently argues for allocation based on individual merit rather than group membership in most domains. This produces a low-to-mid 60s score.

## Greens – Final Score: 39

The Greens advocate for a strong expansion of group-based allocation mechanisms, particularly regarding gender identity and intersectional approaches.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

- Strongly support self-identification in gender recognition and related areas.
- Favour expansive diversity and positive action policies across institutions.
- View group membership (especially gender and other marginalised identities) as a key factor that should influence allocation.
- Oppose restrictions based on biological sex in most contexts.
- Support broadening protected characteristics and strengthening institutional mechanisms for group-based allocation.

The Greens consistently prioritise group identity over individual or biological criteria in allocation decisions. Their positions systematically favour mechanisms that allocate rights, access, and opportunities on the basis of self-identified group membership. This produces a low 30s score.

## SPD – Final Score: 48

The SPD maintains strong support for the existing group-based equality framework and has generally favoured its expansion.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

- Strongly supports the AGG and public sector equality duties.
- Backed the introduction of corporate gender quotas.
- Supported reforms moving toward greater self-identification in gender recognition.
- Views group-based measures as necessary to address structural disadvantage.
- Continues to treat group membership as a relevant factor in allocation policy.

The SPD operates comfortably within a framework that incorporates group membership as a significant factor in allocation. It has consistently supported legislative and institutional measures that allocate opportunities and protections partly on the basis of group identity. Individual merit is recognised but is regularly balanced against group equity considerations. This produces a high 40s score.
