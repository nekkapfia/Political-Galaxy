# Guide: How to Produce a Cultural 4A Timeline Document for a Country

This guide explains how to create a high-quality document analysing shifts in the degree of **social determinism** within a country over time. It measures the extent to which circumstances of birth (class, family background, inherited status, and early resources) limit or determine life outcomes, versus the degree to which individuals can improve their status and that of their descendants through their own actions.

## 1. Preparation and Scope Definition

**Define the time period**
- Choose a clear starting point that marks a significant baseline (e.g. 1945 for Britain, or a major constitutional, independence, or regime change for other countries).
- Decide whether the document will run to the present day.

**Define the analytical focus clearly**
- The document analyses the **observable degree to which birth circumstances** shape life outcomes and social mobility.
- The central question is: To what extent are social standing, education access and quality, occupational opportunity, and status transmission determined or constrained by birth circumstances, rather than by individual effort, competence, and merit-based mechanisms?

**Strict scope boundaries**
Do **not** analyse:
- Emotional or moral attitudes toward national history or identity (Cultural 2B)
- Definitions of the national in-group (Cultural 3A)
- Immigration, borders, or national sovereignty (Core 2A)
- Moral or ideological justifications for inequality or mobility (Cultural 1A)

Focus narrowly on **empirical patterns** of birth-based constraint versus mobility.

## 2. Research and Data Collection

Gather evidence focused on **observable patterns** of social determinism. Prioritise:

- Education systems and selection mechanisms (e.g. grammar schools, comprehensives, private schooling, tracking/streaming)
- Access to and quality of further and higher education
- Employment and promotion practices
- Inheritance, housing, and wealth transmission patterns
- Statistical measures of intergenerational mobility and social fluidity
- Institutional decision-making and elite recruitment patterns
- Government policy and legislation affecting opportunity structures
- Significant political and institutional discourse on merit, selection, and equality of opportunity

Focus on **what was actually done and what the observable outcomes were**, rather than stated intentions or moral framing.

## 3. Segmenting History into Eras (High Granularity First)

**Critical rule:** Start by creating **more eras than you initially think are necessary**. It is far easier to merge similar eras later than to split an era that has become too broad.

**Good reasons to create a new era:**
- A clear, sustained shift in the overall level of birth-based constraint on mobility
- Major policy changes that significantly alter education selection, opportunity structures, or status transmission
- Periods of sustained political contestation over merit, selection, or equality of opportunity
- Significant changes in statistical mobility patterns or elite composition

Aim for relatively short, coherent periods rather than long eras that contain multiple distinct phases.

## 4. Scoring Each Era

Use the official **Cultural 4A bands (0–100)**.

**Scoring approach:**
- Assess the **dominant level of birth-based constraint** on life outcomes and social mobility.
- Consider both formal structures (laws, institutions, selection systems) and practical outcomes (statistical mobility, quality gradients, network effects).
- Be consistent. Give greater weight to sustained patterns rather than isolated events or rhetoric.
- Justify the score with reference to specific, observable mechanisms and outcomes.

## 5. Writing the Era Sections

Use a consistent structure for every era. Maintain similar depth and analytical quality across all eras.

### Recommended Structure

**Heading format (use exactly this style):**  
## **StartYear–EndYear: Era Name – Final Score: XX**

**Intro paragraph** (3 to 5 sentences)

**Subheading:**  
### Birth Constraint and Mobility – Expanded Analysis

- Bullet point 1
- Bullet point 2
- Bullet point 3
- Bullet point 4 (optional)

**Analysis paragraphs** (1–2 paragraphs)

**Key Shift sentence** at the end of every era (this is mandatory).

**Rules for Era Sections:**
- Keep eras reasonably concise but with sufficient analytical depth.
- Every single era **must** have a **Key Shift** sentence at the end.
- Focus on observable mechanisms and outcomes rather than justifications or intentions.
- Use 3–5 bullets maximum.

## 6. Refining the Eras

After completing a first draft:

- Review the full set of eras and ask:
  - Can any eras be merged without losing important nuance?
  - Does any era contain distinct phases that would be better separated?
  - Are there periods where content could be better redistributed between adjacent eras?
- Merge eras that show very similar levels of social determinism.
- Keep eras that represent meaningful shifts in the degree of birth-based constraint.
- Aim for the smallest number of eras that still preserves the important turning points.

## 7. Writing the Political Parties Section

Include a Political Parties section at the end of the document.  
**Parties should always be arranged alphabetically.**

**Structure:**
- Short overall summary of the current state of social determinism.
- Summary table with party names and Final Scores.
- Individual party entries with this format:
  - Brief Overview
  - **Impact on Social Determinism – Expanded Analysis** (with bullets)
  - Why This Produces a Final Score of XX

Base this section on current party platforms, rhetoric, and record (especially where parties have held power or significant influence). Focus on how each party’s policies affect birth-based constraints on mobility, selection mechanisms, and opportunity structures.

## 8. Final Assembly and Quality Control

**Include these elements in the final document:**
- Opening summary paragraph (no main title above it)
- Era Summary Table
- All individual era sections (in chronological order)
- # Political Parties heading
- Political Parties intro paragraph (with bridging reference to the eras)
- Table of parties
- All individual party sections (alphabetically)

**Quality Standards:**
- All eras should be written to a similar depth and analytical standard.
- The Overview section should identify the overall trajectory and main drivers of change in the level of social determinism.
- Repetitive phrasing across eras should be minimised.
- Later eras must receive the same level of analytical attention as earlier eras.
- Scoring justifications must be tied to observable mechanisms and outcomes.
- Clear bridging language should connect the historical eras section with the Political Parties section.

## 9. Recommended Number of Eras

| Time Span       | Recommended Number of Eras | Guidance |
|-----------------|----------------------------|----------|
| 30–50 years     | 6–10                       | Start with more, then merge |
| 70–80+ years    | 9–14                       | Start with 10–12, then refine |

## Summary Workflow

1. Define the time period and starting point.
2. Research observable patterns of birth-based constraint and mobility.
3. Create an initial high-granularity set of eras.
4. Score each era using the Cultural 4A bands.
5. Write the era sections to a consistent standard (including Key Shift sentences).
6. Review and refine the eras (merge where appropriate).
7. Write the Political Parties section.
8. Write the opening summary paragraph (make it analytical).
9. Review the full document against the quality standards above.
10. Edit for repetition, consistency of depth, and analytical clarity.

This process is designed to produce a rigorous, consistent, and analytically strong Cultural 4A timeline document.
