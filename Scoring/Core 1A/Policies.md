# Core 1A Scored Policies Reference

**Version:** 2.0 (9 June 2026) — Major usability release. 
- Added prominent Quick Scoring Workflow and Triage Tips at the top of relevant sections.
- Reorganized Outside of Scope into exactly 5 parallel categories mirroring the main 5 scoring categories for rapid lookup when scoring new multi-element policies.
- Added compact Master Index / Common Policy Type Quick Reference.
- Tightened repetitive explanatory text across Outside of Scope buckets (shared rules referenced once).
- Cleaned change log to high-level structural summary only.
- Minor phrasing standardization and formatting consistency improvements.
All entries preserve Element Rule, Negative-Only Weighting, Scope Purity (Core 1A only), Direct Restriction Only, and other locked universal rules. Existing scored precedents retained in full.

**Quick Scoring Workflow (for new policies)**:
1. Break the policy into discrete mechanical elements (apply Element Rule).
2. For each element, match its Neutered mechanical description to one of the 5 main themes.
3. Immediately check the corresponding parallel **Outside of Scope - [Theme] Related** bucket.
4. If it matches an exclusion criterion → record in that bucket with the provided reasoning and routing (no Core 1A score assigned).
5. If not excluded → locate the closest precedent in the main category and inherit the documented Core 1A Score/band + rationale (adjust only with explicit, rule-based justification).
6. For multi-element policies: split across categories/buckets as needed using the Element Rule; the overall policy score is dominated by the most invasive elements.

**Master Index / Common Policy Type Quick Reference** (use for rapid lookup when a new policy appears):
- Information Control / platform moderation / content duties / data collection → Main Information Control **or** Outside of Scope - Information Control Related (if symbolic, administrative, or purely institutional without direct individual compulsion).
- Speech / expression / incitement / defamation / glorification / ideological rhetoric → Main Speech & Expression Restrictions **or** Outside of Scope - Speech & Expression Related (if purely rhetorical/symbolic without enforceable restrictions).
- Movement / assembly / association / curfews / lockdowns / borders → Main Movement Restrictions **or** Outside of Scope - Movement Related (immigration/citizenship, political structure, external/combatant matters, pure economic labour allocation).
- Physical / bodily / medical / reproductive / structural effects on individuals → Main Physical Restrictions **or** Outside of Scope - Physical Related (punishment/enforcement distinct from restriction, structural determinism, long-term inherited/group positioning, demographic engineering, creation of positive rights/entitlements).
- Surveillance / monitoring / reporting / institutional oversight → Main Surveillance & Monitoring **or** Outside of Scope - Surveillance Related (if purely administrative or consequential self-censorship without operational barriers).
- Any element primarily about membership/borders/sovereignty, external matters, pure economic reorganisation, punishment (vs the underlying restriction), or structural/group positioning → Check the matching parallel Outside of Scope bucket first.

**Purpose:** Pure reference document for the mechanical policy *types* scored under Core 1A (Personal Autonomy). Contains only the neutered base policy descriptions and the rationale for each score. No ideologies, historical regimes/countries, politicians, entities, overview philosophy, discarded policies, or mapping notes. This is the single source of truth for consistent policy scoring.

Policies are organised by **type of restriction** rather than score band. Within each category, policies are listed in **descending score order**.

**Element Rule**: Policies are broken into elements. Each element is placed in the most appropriate category. If a policy contains elements belonging to different categories, it appears in each relevant category showing only the elements that belong there.

### National Security Law (Hong Kong, 2020)
**Neutered Base Policy**: Broad legislation creating offences of secession, subversion, terrorism, and collusion with foreign forces, with vague definitions applicable to political speech, publications, slogans, and organisational activities.

**Elements**:
- Targeting of specific slogans and publications deemed to promote independence or subversion → **97–98**
- Restrictions on publishing or possessing materials advocating secession, subversion, or collusion with foreign forces → **97–98**

**Rationale**: Core elements function as system self-defence. The targeting of slogans, publications, and materials promoting independence or challenging state authority places these elements in the 97–98 band.

### Official Secrets Act 1989
**Neutered Base Policy**: Extremely broad criminal offences for unauthorised disclosure of official SECRET information relevant to national security by crown servants and journalists.

**Core 1A Score: 97**

**Rationale**: Pure system-protection mechanism with no general speech-restriction intent. It sits in the explicit 97–98 sub-range for laws whose purpose is to allow the system to protect itself from destruction.

### "Manifestly illegal" or broadly defined harmful content standards for platform liability and proactive moderation duties
**Neutered Base Policy**: Legal requirements or regulatory standards obliging online platforms to proactively identify, remove, or restrict content deemed "manifestly illegal," "harmful," or contrary to specified values, often with expedited procedures, limited appeal, and liability for non-compliance.

**Core 1A Score: 28–34**

**Rationale**: Creates institutionalised proactive censorship through corporate proxy with low-threshold and expedited enforcement mechanics. Fits early ideological enforcement / police-state monitoring band. Improves cross-country reference coverage for EU-derived and similar platform liability regimes.

### Ideological control in media and online platforms
**Neutered Base Policy**: Requirements for platforms and media to proactively moderate content, implement self-censorship systems, and face liability for "harmful" material.

**Elements**:
- Requirements for platforms and media to proactively moderate content → **28–36**
- Implementation of self-censorship systems and platform liability for "harmful" material → **28–36**

**Rationale**: State-mandated proactive censorship through corporate proxy. Creates institutionalised self-censorship across public discourse.

### Systematic expansion of surveillance infrastructure (information aspect)
**Neutered Base Policy**: State-driven rollout of facial recognition cameras, mass data collection systems, and integration of surveillance data across government and commercial platforms.

**Elements**:
- Mass data collection systems and integration of surveillance data → **28–36**

**Rationale**: Creates a pervasive monitoring environment that restricts expression and association through the knowledge of constant observation.

### Online Safety Act 2023
**Neutered Base Policy**: Imposes duties on online platforms to mitigate illegal and "legal but harmful" content, including age verification, safety-by-design requirements, and Ofcom enforcement powers.

**Core 1A Score: 24**

**Rationale**: State-mandated restriction/sanctioning of expression deemed harmful. Broader scope ("legal but harmful") and proactive platform duties.

### Racial Hatred Offences (Public Order Act 1986 Part 3)
**Neutered Base Policy**: It is an offence to use threatening, abusive or insulting words or behaviour, or display written material which is threatening, abusive or insulting, if the person intends to stir up racial hatred or racial hatred is likely to be stirred up thereby.

**Core 1A Score: 65** (top end of 60–69 band)

**Rationale**: Betterment of society. Targets speech and expression that risks stirring up racial hatred.

### Dedicated "glorification / apology for terrorism" or "incitement to terrorism" provisions with vague or low-threshold standards
**Neutered Base Policy**: Criminal or administrative offences targeting expression that glorifies, apologises for, or indirectly supports designated terrorist acts or groups, often with broad or subjective definitions and aggressive enforcement.

**Core 1A Score: 55–60**

**Rationale**: Direct and broad restriction on expression with significant vagueness allowing downward drift into ideological enforcement. Distinct mechanical type from general hate speech or incitement to violence. Improves granularity for security-related expression restrictions across multiple jurisdictions.

### Aggravated defamation or content offences based on protected characteristics, including removal or limitation of truth defence in specific cases
**Neutered Base Policy**: Defamation or insult laws with enhanced penalties or reduced defences when targeting individuals or groups based on protected characteristics, sometimes eliminating or restricting truth as a defence.

**Core 1A Score: 55–60**

**Rationale**: Content-based restriction with identity-based aggravation. The limitation or removal of truth defence in specific cases increases the restrictive mechanical effect on voluntary expression.

### Religious Hatred Offences (Racial and Religious Hatred Act 2006)
**Neutered Base Policy**: It is an offence to use threatening words or behaviour, or display any written material which is threatening, if the person intends thereby to stir up religious hatred.

**Core 1A Score: 32** (28–36 band)

**Rationale**: Ideological enforcement and police state. Religion treated as proto-ideology → ideological suppression.

### "Incitement to discrimination" as a distinct offence category (separate from incitement to hatred or violence)
**Neutered Base Policy**: Laws creating specific offences for expression that incites discrimination on protected grounds, often with lower thresholds and broader application than traditional incitement to violence.

**Core 1A Score: 34–38**

**Rationale**: Distinct mechanical type from incitement to hatred or violence. Lower threshold and broader application create early ideological enforcement effects. Improves precision for future scoring of identity-based speech offences.

### Blair-era usage of Racial and Religious Hatred Act 2006
**Core 1A Score: 24** (mid/top 19–27 band)

**Rationale**: Due to how it was used in practice (suppression of legitimate public discussion), rather than its literal statutory content.

### Obscene Publications Act 1959
**Neutered Base Policy**: Criminalises publication of material likely to deprave and corrupt.

**Core 1A Score: 63** (Betterment of society)

**Rationale**: Obscene publications are still limited even under progressive ideology, explicitly targeting the showing of pornography to very young children. The crossing into banning for adults brings it into betterment of society.

### Administrative house arrest, internal exile, or non-criminal administrative detention powers under emergency or national security frameworks
**Neutered Base Policy**: Executive or administrative powers to impose house arrest, movement restrictions, electronic tagging, or association limits on individuals without criminal conviction, often on grounds of suspected threat to public order or national security.

**Core 1A Score: 82–88**

## Outside of Scope / Cross-Slider Exclusions

**Purpose of this section**: This section records policies or individual policy *elements* that were reviewed during Core 1A analysis but fall outside the defined scope of Core 1A (systemic restrictions on the individual’s personal autonomy — voluntary actions, speech, choices, thoughts, beliefs, and physiological processes). 

These entries are deliberately excluded from Core 1A scoring to maintain **Scope Purity**. They are routed to the appropriate other slider or handled under the **Punishment vs Restriction** rule, Chilling Effects rule, Negative-Only Weighting, or other locked rules (e.g., Direct Restriction Only, Core 1B vs Core 1A boundary). This section serves as a transparent boundary log to prevent scope creep and support consistent application across all country and entity scoring.

**How to Use When Scoring New Policies**: Identify which of the 5 main Core 1A categories a new policy most closely resembles. Check the corresponding "Outside of Scope - [Theme] Related" sub-section below for similar excluded items. If it matches an exclusion criterion, record the relevant element(s) here instead of scoring in the main section. This parallel structure makes boundary decisions fast and consistent.

**Triage Tip for Each Bucket**: Look for these indicators that an element belongs in the Outside of Scope version rather than the main scoring category: purely symbolic/rhetorical effect, no direct enforceable restriction on voluntary action, primary focus on membership/sovereignty/economic structure/punishment/enforcement/structural positioning/positive entitlements rather than ongoing personal restrictions, or consequence (e.g., self-censorship) rather than the rule itself.

Entries below are organised into **5 parallel categories** mirroring the main scoring structure for maximum usability.

### 1. Outside of Scope - Information Control Related

**Quick Reference Table**

| Item | Quick Summary | Reason Outside of Scope |
|------|---------------|-------------------------|
| Purely Symbolic or Declaratory Legislation | Official statements or symbolic prohibitions/affirmations without penalties or behavioural obligations. | Does not meet Direct Restriction Only rule; purely expressive/symbolic with no enforceable limit on voluntary action. |
| Pure Regime Ideology or Justification Rhetoric (Without Creating Specific Legal Restrictions) | General ideological statements or framing (e.g., “defending republican values”) without new enforceable mechanisms. | Pure rhetoric/framing rather than rules that restrict voluntary conduct. |
| Purely Administrative or Financial Reporting Requirements (Without Operational or Behavioural Impact) | Requirements to submit reports/financial information with only minor administrative consequences. | No meaningful restriction on voluntary activity or operational barriers created. |
| Institutional Ownership or Structural Control of Media, Education, or Cultural Institutions (Without Direct Content or Conduct Restrictions) | State ownership or appointment of oversight bodies where primary effect is institutional control. | Primary effect is institutional architecture rather than direct compulsion on individual speech/behaviour (downstream content restrictions scored in main category). |
| Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon | Widespread self-censorship or behavioural changes in response to perceived expectations or climates (even without formal rules). | Consequence of restrictions or social environment, not a restriction itself (Chilling Effects rule). |

**Example elements reviewed and excluded** (detailed):
- Purely Symbolic or Declaratory Legislation: Official statements, declarations condemning ideologies/practices, or symbolic prohibitions without penalties, obligations, or behavioural restrictions.
- Pure Regime Ideology or Justification Rhetoric (Without Creating Specific Legal Restrictions): General statements about “defending republican values”, “national cohesion”, Marxist framing, or “defence of the Republic” without new enforceable mechanisms.
- Purely Administrative or Financial Reporting Requirements (Without Operational or Behavioural Impact): Requirements to submit reports or financial information where non-compliance has only minor administrative consequences and does not restrict activity or create stigma with practical effects.
- Institutional Ownership or Structural Control of Media, Education, or Cultural Institutions (Without Direct Content or Conduct Restrictions): State ownership, nationalisation, or appointment of leadership/oversight bodies where the primary effect is institutional control rather than compulsion on individual speech or behaviour.
- Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon: Widespread self-censorship or behavioural modification in response to perceived social/institutional expectations or climates, even without formal legal restrictions.

**Cross-References**: France party scoring (rhetoric vs concrete mechanisms in NFP, RN, LR, Attal platforms); general symbolic declarations in various documents.

### 2. Outside of Scope - Speech & Expression Restrictions Related

**Quick Reference Table**

| Item | Quick Summary | Reason Outside of Scope |
|------|---------------|-------------------------|
| Pure Regime Ideology or Justification Rhetoric (Without Creating Specific Legal Restrictions) | General statements about values, cohesion, or ideological framing without new enforceable mechanisms. | Pure rhetoric or framing rather than enforceable restrictions on voluntary expression. |
| Purely Symbolic or Declaratory Legislation | Official declarations condemning/affirming ideologies or practices without penalties or obligations. | Does not create direct, enforceable limits on voluntary speech or expression. |
| Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon | Behavioural changes or self-censorship due to perceived social/institutional expectations. | Consequence of environment or restrictions, not the restriction itself (Chilling Effects rule). |

Items that resemble speech or expression controls but are purely rhetorical, symbolic, or do not impose enforceable restrictions on voluntary expression.

**Core Rules Applied**: Direct Restriction Only; Pure Regime Ideology or Justification Rhetoric; Purely Symbolic or Declaratory Legislation; Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon (overlap with above for expression-related effects).

**Example elements reviewed and excluded**:
- Pure Regime Ideology or Justification Rhetoric (Without Creating Specific Legal Restrictions) — as above, when framed around expression or values.
- Purely Symbolic or Declaratory Legislation — as above, when it involves condemnation or affirmation related to speech/ideologies.
- Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon — as above, when it affects expression or public discourse.

**Detailed Reasoning for Exclusion**: These do not create enforceable restrictions on voluntary expression. They are framing, rhetoric, or consequences rather than rules limiting speech. Already applied in party scoring when separating rhetoric from concrete policy.

**Cross-References**: France party scoring (NFP, RN, Attal, LR — rhetoric vs new laws); general ideological justifications across documents.

### 3. Outside of Scope - Movement Restrictions (including Assembly & Association) Related

**Quick Reference Table**

| Item | Quick Summary | Reason Outside of Scope |
|------|---------------|-------------------------|
| Immigration and Citizenship | Policies on entry, residency, naturalisation, deportation, borders, visas, asylum, and differential rights based on immigration/citizenship status. | Primarily regulates membership of the political community and territory (Core 2A – National Sovereignty), not restrictions on individuals already present. |
| Political Structure Elements | Core constitutional rules on government structure, federalism, separation of powers, electoral systems, or regime type. | Concerns architecture of political power and institutional design rather than direct personal autonomy restrictions. |
| External / Territorial and Core 2B Boundary Matters | Policies concerning individuals not under the domestic legal order in the normal sense (e.g., Guantanamo-style enemy combatant detention, extraterritorial actions). | Individuals not “living under” the domestic system; falls under Core 2B / external sovereignty and laws of armed conflict. |
| Laws of Armed Conflict / Treatment of Enemy Combatants (Core 2B Boundary) | Battlefield operations, laws of war applied to enemy forces, targeting/detention of active combatants. | Distinct from civilian/domestic personal autonomy; governed by international humanitarian law / external sovereignty frameworks. |
| Pure Economic Reorganisation and Labour Allocation Mechanisms (Core 1B Boundary) | State-directed economic planning, nationalisation, centralised labour allocation, or production quotas analysed as pure economic tools. | Primarily economic organisation and workforce distribution (Core 1B); only enters Core 1A when combined with direct personal restrictions (e.g., movement bans or detention). |

Items that resemble movement, assembly, association, or border controls but primarily concern membership, sovereignty, external matters, political architecture, or economic reorganisation rather than direct restrictions on individuals already present.

**Core Rules Applied**: Core 2A – National Sovereignty; Core 2B – International Sovereignty / External matters; Political Structure Elements; Pure Economic Reorganisation and Labour Allocation Mechanisms (Core 1B Boundary); Laws of Armed Conflict / Treatment of Enemy Combatants (Core 2B Boundary); Immigration and Citizenship; External / Territorial and Core 2B Boundary Matters.

**Example elements reviewed and excluded**:
- Immigration and Citizenship: Policies governing entry, residency, naturalisation, deportation/removal, visa/asylum/border regimes, or differential rights based on immigration/citizenship status (when not creating direct personal autonomy restrictions on individuals already lawfully present). Routed to Core 2A.
- Political Structure Elements: Core constitutional rules on government structure, federalism, separation of powers, electoral systems, or regime type (when not tied to specific personal rights restrictions). Routed to Core 2A or political structure analysis.
- External / Territorial and Core 2B Boundary Matters: Policies concerning individuals not living under the domestic legal order in the normal sense (e.g., Guantanamo-style indefinite detention of foreign enemy combatants, enhanced interrogation, denial of habeas in that context; other extraterritorial detention/rendition/targeted killing of non-residents). Routed to Core 2B.
- Laws of Armed Conflict / Treatment of Enemy Combatants (Core 2B Boundary): Battlefield military operations, laws of war applied to enemy forces, rules on targeting/detention/legal status of individuals actively participating in armed conflict (distinct from civilian repression). Routed to Core 2B / laws of armed conflict.
- Pure Economic Reorganisation and Labour Allocation Mechanisms (Core 1B Boundary): State-directed economic planning, nationalisation, centralised labour allocation, production quotas, or War Communism-era grain requisition analysed purely as economic extraction (when they do not combine with movement restrictions, detention, or daily life controls on individuals). Routed to Core 1B.

**Detailed Reasoning for Exclusion**: These primarily regulate membership of the political community, territory/borders, external sovereignty, political/institutional architecture, or economic organisation (Core 1B/2A/2B). They only enter Core 1A when they create additional direct restrictions on voluntary actions of individuals already present. Maintains clean separation between personal autonomy and sovereignty/economic questions.

**Cross-References**: France party scoring (national preference, deportation proposals in RN, LR, Attal, NFP); Pre-Stalin USSR War Communism economic elements; Guantanamo and Algeria War distinctions (military vs civilian); general command economy mechanisms.

### 4. Outside of Scope - Physical Restrictions Related

**Quick Reference Table**

| Item | Quick Summary | Reason Outside of Scope |
|------|---------------|-------------------------|
| Pure Punishment Elements (distinct from the underlying restriction) | Enforcement mechanisms, penalties, or coercive measures applied after a violation (e.g., forced abortions/sterilisations as punishment for unauthorised pregnancies). | Dominant effect is punishment/enforcement rather than the substantive restriction itself (Punishment vs Restriction rule). |
| Social Structure Determinism | Policies attributing outcomes/status to deterministic social/structural/group-based factors (e.g., denial of rights to children born outside quota plans framed as structural consequence). | Better captured as measures of attitudes toward structural determinism/hierarchy than as ongoing direct restrictions on voluntary action. |
| Long-term Structural or Inherited Consequences Framed Purely as Group Positioning | Long-term denial of opportunities/rights framed as automatic structural consequence of parents’ or group’s position in a state system. | Focus is on inherited/group positioning rather than direct ongoing personal restrictions. |
| Long-term Demographic or Cultural Engineering Framed Purely as Structural Positioning | Policies influencing long-term population composition or group distribution through structural consequences or incentives. | Primary framing and effect is societal engineering/structural positioning rather than immediate personal restrictions. |
| Creation of Positive Rights or Entitlements (as Distinct from Removal of Restrictions) | Establishment of new state-provided entitlements, services, or positive rights that did not previously exist. | Core 1A measures restrictions on existing voluntary action; creation of positives operates in a different space (state provision/equity) and does not generate scoring credit under Negative-Only Weighting. |

Items that resemble physical, bodily, medical, or structural restrictions on individuals but are primarily punishment/enforcement, structural determinism, long-term inherited/group positioning, demographic engineering, or creation of positive entitlements rather than direct ongoing restrictions on voluntary action.

**Core Rules Applied**: Punishment vs Restriction rule; Social Structure Determinism; Long-term Structural or Inherited Consequences Framed Purely as Group Positioning; Long-term Demographic or Cultural Engineering Framed Purely as Structural Positioning; Creation of Positive Rights or Entitlements (as Distinct from Removal of Restrictions); Pure Punishment Elements (distinct from the underlying restriction).

**Example elements reviewed and excluded**:
- Pure Punishment Elements (distinct from the underlying restriction): Enforcement mechanisms, penalties, coercive measures, or enhanced sentencing applied after a violation of an underlying rule (e.g., Deng-Era One-Child Policy – Physical coercion such as forced abortions/sterilisations/IUD insertions as punishment for unauthorised pregnancies, when the dominant effect is enforcement rather than the core reproductive restriction). Routed to Punishment not Restriction. (The underlying numerical birth limit remains in the main Physical Restrictions category.)
- Social Structure Determinism: Policies or elements that primarily attribute individual outcomes/status/life chances to deterministic social, structural, or group-based factors (e.g., Deng-Era One-Child Policy – Denial of legal status and rights/hukou/education/healthcare to children born outside the plan, framed as structural consequence of parents’/child’s position in the quota system). Routed to Social Structure Determinism + Punishment not Restriction.
- Long-term Structural or Inherited Consequences Framed Purely as Group Positioning: Long-term denial of opportunities/rights to individuals or descendants framed primarily as an automatic structural consequence of parents’ or group’s position within a state system (distinct from ongoing direct restrictions on their own voluntary actions). Builds on Deng-Era examples.
- Long-term Demographic or Cultural Engineering Framed Purely as Structural Positioning: Policies designed to influence long-term population composition, cultural character, or group distribution through incentives, restrictions on family formation, or structural consequences, when the primary framing and mechanical effect is societal engineering rather than immediate personal restriction. Builds on Deng-Era downstream effects.
- Creation of Positive Rights or Entitlements (as Distinct from Removal of Restrictions): Policies that establish new positive entitlements, state-provided services, benefits, or rights to resources/support that did not previously exist as legal entitlements (e.g., expansion of welfare/education/healthcare as new positive provisions, distinct from removal of discriminatory barriers). Does not generate scoring credit under Negative-Only Weighting (which focuses on restrictions); operates in a different analytical space (state provision/equity).

**Detailed Reasoning for Exclusion**: These are either enforcement/punishment mechanisms (distinct from the substantive restriction), measures of structural determinism or long-term group positioning (better captured under attitudes toward hierarchy/equity), or creation of positives (symmetric to Negative-Only rule). The core substantive restrictions (where they exist) remain scored in the main Physical Restrictions category. Maintains clean separation.

**Cross-References**: Deng-Era One-Child Policy elements (thread analysis); general positive rights expansions; structural positioning in various historical contexts.

### 5. Outside of Scope - Surveillance & Monitoring Related

**Quick Reference Table**

| Item | Quick Summary | Reason Outside of Scope |
|------|---------------|-------------------------|
| Purely Administrative or Financial Reporting Requirements (Without Operational or Behavioural Impact) | Requirements to submit reports or financial information with only minor administrative consequences and no meaningful restriction on activity. | Does not create direct or significant restrictions on voluntary conduct or operational barriers. |
| Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon | Widespread self-censorship or behavioural changes in response to perceived monitoring, expectations, or social climates. | Consequence of restrictions or environment rather than an enforceable restriction itself (Chilling Effects rule). |
| Institutional Ownership or Structural Control of Media, Education, or Cultural Institutions (Without Direct Content or Conduct Restrictions) | State ownership or appointment of oversight bodies where primary effect is institutional/monitoring structure. | Primary effect is institutional architecture or oversight rather than direct compulsion on individual behaviour (downstream restrictions scored in main categories). |

Items that resemble surveillance, monitoring, or institutional oversight but are purely administrative, consequential (self-censorship), or structural without creating direct behavioural restrictions or meaningful operational impact.

**Core Rules Applied**: Chilling Effects (permanent exclusion); Purely Administrative or Financial Reporting Requirements (Without Operational or Behavioural Impact); Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon; Institutional Ownership or Structural Control... (as above).

**Example elements reviewed and excluded**:
- Purely Administrative or Financial Reporting Requirements (Without Operational or Behavioural Impact) — as in Information Control Related (overlap for monitoring/reporting aspects).
- Self-Censorship or Behavioural Adaptation as an Independent Phenomenon — as above (when resulting from perceived monitoring or expectations).
- Institutional Ownership or Structural Control of Media, Education, or Cultural Institutions (Without Direct Content or Conduct Restrictions) — as above (when it involves oversight/monitoring structures without direct individual compulsion).

**Detailed Reasoning for Exclusion**: These do not create sufficiently direct or significant restrictions on voluntary conduct or meaningful operational barriers. They are administrative, consequential, or institutional rather than enforceable surveillance/monitoring limits on individuals. More substantial versions (creating operational barriers or stigma) are scored in the main Surveillance & Monitoring category.

**Cross-References**: General administrative reporting in various contexts; institutional oversight without downstream restrictions.
