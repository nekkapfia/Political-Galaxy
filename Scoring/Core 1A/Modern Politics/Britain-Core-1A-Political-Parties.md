### Broad Summary

The contemporary British party system on Core 1A is defined by a clear gradient of tolerance for direct state restrictions on voluntary expression, association, and online communication. At the higher-autonomy end stands Reform UK, whose platform centres on the repeal of the Online Safety Act 2023 and the substantial narrowing or abolition of non-violent “hate speech” and public-order speech offences that currently criminalise or chill speech causing “harassment, alarm or distress.” The Conservative Party under its present leadership occupies a mid-high position: it retains the existing statutory framework while emphasising clearer lines between adult free expression and child-protection measures, and has moved away from the more expansive regulatory instincts of earlier periods. The Liberal Democrats sit slightly lower, combining traditional civil-liberties language with acceptance of regulated online harms regimes.

Labour, as the governing party, anchors the lower-to-mid range through active defence and proposed extension of the Online Safety Act architecture, introduction of election-period social-media rules, and the adoption of an official definition of “anti-Muslim hostility” that critics argue risks creating de-facto blasphemy-adjacent constraints. The Green Party and the SNP occupy the more restrictive end of the spectrum. The Greens favour expansive identity-based and content-moderation controls; the SNP both supports and operates under Scotland’s Hate Crime and Public Order Act 2021, which extends “stirring up hatred” offences and applies even to private settings in certain formulations. Where Endos/Xenos splits appear they reflect differential enforcement intensity or definitional focus on particular religious or identity groups rather than a wholesale dual legal system. Overall, the system remains constrained by a dense legacy of Public Order Act, Communications Act, and Racial and Religious Hatred Act provisions that no major party proposes to dismantle entirely; the principal axis of contestation is therefore the degree to which the Online Safety Act and residual hate-speech apparatus should be retained, expanded, or rolled back.

# Conservative Party

The Conservative Party presents itself as the defender of ordered liberty and adult free expression within a framework of child protection and public order. Its current leadership emphasises that government should draw clear, enforceable lines to protect minors online while refusing to police adult speech or tell adults what to think. The party retains the existing statutory baseline of hate-speech and public-order offences while signalling greater scepticism toward expansive non-crime hate-incident recording and over-zealous enforcement of communications offences.

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

* Retention of the Online Safety Act 2023 framework for illegal content and child-protection duties, with emphasis on highly effective age assurance rather than broad adult content controls.
* Explicit policy preference for a clear under-16 social-media age limit while protecting free choice and free speech for adults.
* Maintenance of existing Public Order Act 1986 (including Section 5 “harassment, alarm or distress”) and Racial and Religious Hatred Act provisions as part of the baseline legal order.
* Support for reform or curtailment of non-crime hate incident recording practices that have previously captured lawful speech.
* Opposition to new election-period social-media amplification or “trusted news” prominence duties that would treat platforms as broadcasters.
* Acceptance of communications offences under the Communications Act 2003 and Malicious Communications Act 1988 where they target intentional threats or false communications intended to cause harm, while criticising over-broad application to non-threatening speech.

The party’s overall profile is therefore one of managed retention of the post-1986 and post-2023 speech-restriction architecture coupled with a rhetorical and policy preference for clearer adult/child distinctions and reduced administrative recording of non-criminal speech. It does not propose wholesale repeal of the core hate-speech or public-order statutes, nor does it introduce new identity-based speech offences. The cumulative weight of the retained baseline (Public Order Act speech offences, communications offences, and the Online Safety Act’s illegal-content and age-assurance duties) keeps the score in the upper-mid range; the absence of further expansionary measures and the explicit adult free-speech language prevent a lower placement.

No Endos/Xenos split is warranted: the party’s platform does not create or intensify a stable, discretely targetable outgroup differential in the application of these restrictions. The score reflects the cumulative direct restrictions on expression and online communication that the party would maintain rather than any positive credit for adult free-speech rhetoric.

# Green Party of England and Wales

The Green Party frames personal autonomy within an ecological and social-justice matrix that prioritises protection from harm, including online and identity-based harm. It supports robust regulation of online platforms, expansive interpretations of hate speech, and institutional measures against discrimination that frequently translate into content and expression controls.

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

* Support for the Online Safety Act’s duties on platforms to remove illegal content and protect users from harmful material, with emphasis on identity-based and ecological harms.
* Advocacy for stronger enforcement of stirring-up-hatred offences and related Public Order Act provisions, particularly where speech is characterised as racist, transphobic, or otherwise identity-hostile.
* Institutional and educational measures that treat certain forms of critical speech (on gender, race, or immigration) as potentially harmful and therefore subject to restriction or institutional sanction.
* Support for platform-level content moderation and algorithmic interventions aimed at reducing the visibility of speech deemed discriminatory or harmful.
* Retention and potential expansion of Equality Act-related speech and association constraints in public and quasi-public settings.
* Opposition to repeal of existing hate-speech or online-safety frameworks.

The party’s restrictive profile is driven by the combination of retained baseline offences and an operative preference for broader application of identity-based speech controls and platform duties. While formal doctrine emphasises justice and ecological limits, the practical effect is a denser set of direct restrictions on voluntary expression and association than parties further up the autonomy scale. The absence of any proposal to narrow the Public Order Act, Communications Act, or Online Safety Act architecture, coupled with affirmative support for expansive enforcement, produces a lower score.

### Why this produces Endos 48 / Xenos 42

The cumulative weight of support for the Online Safety Act, expansive hate-speech enforcement, and identity-based institutional controls yields an Endos score of 48. A modest Xenos differential (42) is recorded because the party’s platform and institutional practice apply heightened scrutiny and potential restriction to speech critical of certain protected identity categories, creating a stable, discretely targetable outgroup differential in the intensity of expression controls. Negative-Only Weighting applies; no credit is given for rights language.

# Labour Party

The Labour Party, as the governing party, defends and seeks to extend the post-2023 online-safety and speech-regulation architecture. It maintains the Online Safety Act, advances election-period social-media rules, and has adopted an official definition of anti-Muslim hostility while continuing to operate the broader hate-speech and public-order framework.

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

* Active defence and continued implementation of the Online Safety Act 2023, including illegal-content duties, age-assurance requirements, and platform accountability for harmful material.
* Proposed or supported measures within the Representation of the People Bill framework to impose duties on platforms regarding the amplification and sharing of political information during election periods, analogous to broadcast impartiality rules.
* Adoption of an official definition of “anti-Muslim hostility” intended to guide institutional and potentially legal responses, which critics argue risks constraining legitimate criticism of religious doctrine or Islamist ideology.
* Maintenance of Public Order Act 1986 (including Section 5 and stirring-up provisions), Racial and Religious Hatred Act, Communications Act, and Malicious Communications Act offences.
* Support for further child-protection measures such as device-level scanning or social-media access restrictions for under-16s and voluntary curfews for older teens.
* Institutional promotion of “trusted news” prominence and related discoverability duties that would shape the visibility of political and news content online.

Labour’s profile is defined by retention of the dense existing speech-restriction baseline plus concrete proposals to extend platform duties into election-period political speech and to operationalise identity-specific hostility definitions. These additions increase the cumulative weight of direct restrictions on voluntary online expression and association relative to parties that seek roll-back. The party does not propose repeal of core hate-speech or public-order statutes.

### Why this produces Endos 52 / Xenos 48

Negative-Only Weighting applied to the retained Online Safety Act architecture, existing Public Order and communications offences, plus the proposed election-period platform duties and anti-Muslim hostility definition produces an Endos score of 52. A limited Xenos differential (48) is recorded because the anti-Muslim hostility definition and related institutional focus create a modestly heightened risk of restriction on speech critical of Islam or Islamism relative to other religious or ideological criticism, constituting a stable (if not absolute) outgroup differential. No positive credit is assigned for child-protection or “accuracy” framing.

# Liberal Democrats

The Liberal Democrats present themselves as the party of civil liberties and open society while accepting a regulated online environment and the enforcement of existing hate-speech and public-order laws. Their platform combines formal free-speech commitments with support for platform duties against illegal and harmful content.

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

* Support for the Online Safety Act’s core duties on illegal content and child protection, while emphasising the need for Ofcom to be properly resourced to enforce both restriction and free-expression safeguards.
* Retention of Public Order Act, Racial and Religious Hatred Act, and communications offences as necessary limits on speech that incites hatred or violence.
* Advocacy for robust anti-SLAPP protections and other measures intended to shield journalists and whistleblowers from abusive litigation, which indirectly supports expression.
* Acceptance of platform-level content moderation for illegal material and certain categories of harmful content.
* Opposition to wholesale repeal of the Online Safety Act or core hate-speech statutes.
* Support for transparency and accountability mechanisms around online political advertising and content amplification.

The party’s restrictive profile sits between the higher-autonomy parties and Labour/Greens. It does not expand the speech-restriction architecture with new identity-specific definitions or election-period amplification rules of the kind associated with Labour, yet it also declines to support the repeal agenda of Reform UK. The cumulative weight of the retained baseline therefore produces a mid-range score.

### Why this produces Endos 62 / Xenos N/A

The retained Online Safety Act duties and existing Public Order/communications offences, tempered by explicit free-expression safeguards and anti-SLAPP language, produce an Endos score of 62 under Negative-Only Weighting. No Endos/Xenos split applies: the platform does not create or intensify a stable, discretely targetable outgroup differential in the application of expression restrictions.

# Reform UK

Reform UK positions itself as the primary vehicle for restoring free speech by dismantling key elements of the post-1986 and post-2023 speech-restriction regime. Its platform centres on the repeal of the Online Safety Act and the substantial reduction of non-violent hate-speech and public-order speech offences.

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

* Explicit commitment to repeal the Online Safety Act 2023 as a first-order priority, on the grounds that it suppresses freedom of speech and forces platform censorship of anti-government and other disfavoured speech.
* Advocacy for the repeal or radical narrowing of Section 5 of the Public Order Act 1986 (speech causing harassment, alarm or distress) and of non-violent “hate speech” / stirring-up offences.
* Support for replacing the current suite of communications offences with a narrower offence limited to intentionally menacing and targeted communications.
* Rejection of DEI-driven institutional speech codes and related administrative controls on expression.
* Defence of religious freedom within a framework that prioritises British cultural and Christian-heritage norms, without introducing new speech offences targeted at religious criticism.
* Opposition to election-period platform amplification duties or “trusted news” prominence requirements that would treat social media as broadcast media.

Reform UK’s profile is the least restrictive among major parties. By proposing the removal of the Online Safety Act and the substantial narrowing of the Public Order and hate-speech apparatus, it would reduce the cumulative weight of direct restrictions on voluntary expression and online communication. Under Negative-Only Weighting the score rises relative to parties that retain or expand those frameworks; the absence of new identity-based speech offences prevents a lower placement.

### Why this produces Endos 78 / Xenos N/A

Negative-Only Weighting applied to the proposed repeal of the Online Safety Act and the narrowing of non-violent hate-speech and public-order speech offences produces an Endos score of 78. The residual baseline of direct-harm and intentional-threat offences remains, preventing a higher score. No Endos/Xenos split is warranted: the platform does not create a stable, discretely targetable outgroup differential in the remaining restrictions.

# Scottish National Party

The Scottish National Party operates within and supports the Hate Crime and Public Order (Scotland) Act 2021 framework, which extends stirring-up-hatred offences and applies a broader set of expression controls than the equivalent English and Welsh statutes in certain respects. Its platform prioritises identity-based protections and institutional measures against hatred.

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

* Support for and continued operation under the Hate Crime and Public Order (Scotland) Act 2021, including the offence of stirring up hatred on the basis of race, religion, sexual orientation, transgender identity, and other characteristics, with application that can reach private settings in some formulations.
* Retention of the broader UK Online Safety Act architecture as it applies in Scotland.
* Institutional and educational measures that treat certain forms of critical speech as potentially stirring up hatred or creating a hostile environment.
* Support for robust enforcement of existing public-order and communications offences against identity-hostile expression.
* Opposition to repeal of the Scottish hate-crime framework or the UK Online Safety Act.

The SNP’s restrictive profile is shaped by the denser Scottish hate-crime statute plus the retained UK online-safety baseline. The combination produces a lower autonomy score than parties that seek to narrow these frameworks.

### Why this produces Endos 45 / Xenos 42

The cumulative weight of the Hate Crime and Public Order (Scotland) Act 2021 together with the Online Safety Act and residual public-order offences yields an Endos score of 45. A limited Xenos differential (42) is recorded because the Act’s protected characteristics and enforcement practice create heightened scrutiny of speech critical of certain identity categories, constituting a stable outgroup differential in intensity of restriction. Negative-Only Weighting applies throughout.
