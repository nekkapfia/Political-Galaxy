# Core 1A: Eras of Personal Autonomy in the United States (1945–Present)

## Introductory Overview

The United States maintains a structurally higher Core 1A baseline than most European or authoritarian systems because of the First Amendment’s robust protection of speech and association. From 1945 the trajectory is one of gradual expansion of administrative, institutional, and later platform-mediated controls rather than the dense statutory hate-speech codes characteristic of the United Kingdom or France. The period 1945–1965 requires an Endos/Xenos split because the formal Jim Crow legal system imposed a stable, racially targeted dual structure of restrictions on movement, association, public accommodation and related domains. After the Civil Rights Act 1964 and Voting Rights Act 1965 dismantled that formal dual system, scoring returns to a single Base Score. Later decades saw the rise of campus speech codes, workplace harassment doctrines under Title VII, and, after approximately 2010, intense social and political pressure for platform content moderation. COVID produced a temporary multi-domain restriction of movement, association and bodily autonomy. The post-2020 period features polarisation over the scope of private and administrative speech controls, but the constitutional floor keeps overall scores higher than in comparator democracies.

## **1945–1965: High Constitutional Baseline with Jim Crow Dual System – Endos 85 / Xenos 55**

The post-war decades featured a robust First Amendment regime for the core population, tempered by the practical effects of the Second Red Scare. At the same time, the formal Jim Crow legal system remained operative in large parts of the country, imposing a stable, racially targeted set of restrictions on movement, association, public accommodation, education and related domains for Black Americans. This creates a clear Endos/Xenos divergence.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Strong First Amendment baseline and narrow categories of unprotected speech for the Endos population.
* Smith Act prosecutions, loyalty programmes and institutional blacklisting creating practical constraints on left-wing political speech and association.
* Formal Jim Crow statutes and ordinances enforcing racial segregation in public facilities, transportation, education, housing and marriage (Xenos).
* State and local enforcement of segregation through police, courts and public institutions.
* Core criminal prohibitions on violence and direct harm applied across the population, but within a dual legal framework.
* Progressive judicial and legislative pressure on Jim Crow culminating in the Civil Rights Act 1964 and Voting Rights Act 1965.

The restrictive architecture is defined by a high constitutional floor for the core population coexisting with a formal, racially defined dual legal system. The divergence is significant, consistent and observable, satisfying the conditions for an Endos/Xenos split.

**Key Shift:** Continuation of the post-war constitutional baseline alongside the still-operative Jim Crow dual legal system.

### Why this produces Endos 85 / Xenos 55

Endos reflects the high First Amendment baseline tempered by Red Scare constraints. Xenos reflects the additional, systematic restrictions imposed by Jim Crow on movement, association, public accommodation and related domains. The split is required by the significant and consistent observable divergence.

## **1965–1969: Post-Civil Rights Transition – Base Score: 84**

(No Endos/Xenos split)

The major civil-rights statutes of 1964–1965 and their immediate enforcement ended the formal Jim Crow dual legal system. The high constitutional baseline continued, still tempered by the residual effects of the Red Scare architecture and the final years before *Brandenburg* (1969). With the formal racial dual system dismantled, the justification for a system-wide Endos/Xenos split on Jim Crow grounds ends.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Termination of the formal Jim Crow legal structure through the Civil Rights Act 1964, Voting Rights Act 1965 and related enforcement.
* Continued First Amendment baseline with residual Smith Act / loyalty-era constraints declining in practical force.
* Core criminal prohibitions on violence and direct harm.
* Absence of the later campus speech-code and Title VII hostile-environment layers.
* No new system-wide dual legal structure replacing Jim Crow.

The restrictive architecture is defined by the removal of the formal racial dual system while the high constitutional floor remains in place. Because the significant, consistent, legally structured divergence has ended, the optional split is not applied.

**Key Shift:** Formal dismantling of the Jim Crow dual legal system and return to a single scoring framework.

### Why this produces a Base Score of 84

Removal of the Jim Crow dual structure raises the practical autonomy floor for the former Xenos population. The residual Red Scare constraints prevent a full return to the absolute peak of the constitutional baseline. Single Base Score: 84.

## **1970–1990: Institutional and Workplace Expansion – Base Score: 80**

The 1970s and 1980s saw the growth of campus speech codes, the development of workplace harassment doctrine, and related institutional controls that began to add effective restrictions on expression in universities and employment settings. These were largely non-criminal and often private or quasi-private, yet they constituted a durable expansion of the practical restrictive environment.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Expansion of hostile-environment and harassment doctrines affecting workplace speech. These created direct institutional restrictions on voluntary expression in employment settings.
* Proliferation of campus speech codes that regulated expression on the basis of content and viewpoint in university settings.
* Retention of the core First Amendment baseline for pure government restriction of public speech; the new controls operated primarily through institutional and employment leverage rather than criminal law.
* Continued absence of broad federal statutory hate-speech offences of the European type.
* The cumulative effect was a measurable densification of the expression environment in two major institutional domains (higher education and the workplace).

The era is defined by the rise of institutional (primarily non-criminal) speech regulation that reduced the practical autonomy of speakers in settings where most citizens spend significant portions of their lives.

**Key Shift:** Rise of institutional speech regulation in universities and employment, adding a new layer of restriction outside the traditional criminal and pure-government domains.

### Why this produces a final score of 80

Modest downward adjustment from the 1945–1969 baseline of 85, driven by the cumulative weight of campus and workplace speech controls. Negative-Only Weighting applies; the restrictions are direct institutional constraints on voluntary expression even though they are not primarily criminal. The constitutional floor prevents a steeper drop.

## **1991–2010: Continued Institutional Density – Base Score: 76**

The institutional layer continued to consolidate. Early debates over internet regulation occurred, but many of the more ambitious federal attempts were narrowed or struck down. The overall environment remained one of persistent campus and workplace controls alongside a still-robust formal constitutional baseline.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Persistent and in some institutions intensifying campus speech codes and related administrative enforcement.
* Continued operation of workplace harassment and hostile-environment doctrines.
* Early attempts at online content regulation (several of which were judicially limited), preventing the emergence of a dense federal statutory online speech code in this period.
* Retention of the core First Amendment protections against government content discrimination.
* No major liberalising rollback of the institutional controls that had developed since the 1970s.

The restrictive profile is characterised by the consolidation of the institutional layer without a corresponding major expansion of federal criminal speech law.

**Key Shift:** Consolidation of the institutional speech-control layer accompanied by judicial and political resistance to broad new federal online content mandates.

### Why this produces a final score of 76

Further modest downward movement from 80 to 76 under Negative-Only Weighting, reflecting the continued cumulative weight of institutional restrictions. The absence of successful broad federal content legislation prevents a steeper decline.

## **2011–2019: Platform Moderation Era – Base Score: 70**

Intense social, political and commercial pressure on major platforms to moderate content became a central feature of the expression environment. Combined with the continuing institutional layer, this produced a further reduction in the practical autonomy of online speech.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Platform content-moderation practices driven by public pressure, advertiser preferences, and political scrutiny. While not direct state prohibitions in the classic sense, state actors frequently amplified and sought to shape these practices, and the resulting environment constituted a material restriction on the visibility and reach of voluntary online expression.
* Ongoing campus and workplace speech regimes.
* Limited new federal criminal speech legislation; the primary expansion occurred through private and quasi-private intermediaries.
* Heightened polarisation over the legitimacy and scope of content moderation, but no wholesale dismantling of the institutional or platform layers.

The era is defined by the emergence of large-scale platform-mediated content controls as a major component of the practical restrictive architecture.

**Key Shift:** Platform-level content controls become a central and durable feature of the expression environment, adding a significant new layer of restriction.

### Why this produces a final score of 70

Additional downward pressure from the platform layer produces a further drop from 76 to 70 under Negative-Only Weighting. The score reflects the denser practical environment for online expression even while formal First Amendment protections against direct government censorship remained intact.

## **2020–early 2022: COVID Restrictions Block – Base Score: 45**

Federal, state and local movement, gathering and medical mandates produced a temporary but severe multi-domain restriction of personal autonomy. Variation across states created internal differentiation, but the national cumulative weight was substantial.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Stay-at-home orders and lockdowns imposing direct restrictions on movement.
* Gathering limits and business closures restricting association.
* Vaccine and masking mandates that conditioned access to work, travel, education and other activities.
* Significant interstate variation; some states imposed lighter regimes, but the overall national impact justifies single-block treatment under the established protocol.
* Temporary character of the measures provides only modest mitigation.

The restrictive profile is defined by the simultaneous imposition of severe controls across movement, association and bodily autonomy.

**Key Shift:** Temporary multi-domain emergency restrictions on a nationwide (though federally differentiated) scale.

### Why this produces a final score of 45

Protocol-consistent single-block scoring of the cumulative COVID restrictions yields a Base Score of 45, the lowest era score in the post-1945 American series. Negative-Only Weighting is applied. The temporary nature of the measures is acknowledged but does not prevent the large downward movement while the restrictions were in force.

---

## **Mid-2022 onward: Contested Recovery – Base Score: 72**

Lifting of most COVID measures and renewed political and legal contestation over platform moderation, campus speech and government jawboning produced a substantial recovery. The recovery remains incomplete because the long-standing institutional layer and ongoing platform practices continue to exert downward pressure.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Termination of the most sweeping COVID movement, gathering and medical mandates. These removals raise the score by reducing negative weight.
* Ongoing litigation and legislation concerning platform moderation, viewpoint discrimination, and government pressure on private intermediaries.
* Continued operation of campus and workplace speech regimes in many institutions.
* Retention of the formal First Amendment baseline against direct government content regulation.
* Heightened political polarisation over speech governance, but no comprehensive new federal statutory speech code of the European type.

The restrictive profile is defined by the removal of the temporary COVID superstructure amid continued contestation over the institutional and platform layers.

**Key Shift:** End of the COVID superstructure accompanied by intensified political and legal contestation over platform and institutional speech controls.

### Why this produces a final score of 72

Recovery from COVID is substantial. The persistent but non-criminal institutional and platform environment prevents a return to the mid-century highs. Under Negative-Only Weighting the net Base Score is 72 — well above the COVID low of 45 and above the 2011–2019 platform-era level of 70, yet still below the 1945–1969 constitutional peak of 85. The placement reflects the cumulative architecture of institutional and platform controls layered on the high constitutional baseline.
