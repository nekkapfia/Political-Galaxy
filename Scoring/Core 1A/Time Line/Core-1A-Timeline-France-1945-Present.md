# Core 1A: Eras of Personal Autonomy in France (1945–Present)

## Introductory Overview

France’s Core 1A trajectory from 1945 to the present shows a high post-war protective baseline that was progressively eroded by the expansion of content-based speech restrictions, emergency powers, and targeted secularism measures, interrupted by one major liberalisation (abortion) and one temporary but severe collapse (COVID). The immediate post-liberation period retained strong protections against core violent crime while imposing only limited speech and association controls. The Algerian War introduced the first major downward movement through expression suppression. Subsequent decades saw the cumulative weight of hate-speech expansions (Pleven Law, Gayssot Law, Article 24 enforcement) offset by the removal of the discriminatory age-of-consent restriction and the shift from blanket abortion prohibition to regulated access. From 2015 the State of Emergency and glorification-of-terrorism provisions produced a sharp drop; COVID constituted the lowest point. The post-COVID recovery has been real but incomplete, leaving the Base Score well below the mid-century levels because of persistent and selectively enforced expression controls.

The long-term pattern is one of gradual tightening of the speech and association environment, punctuated by security crises that expand the state’s direct restrictive repertoire, with only partial reversals. Endos/Xenos differentials become analytically relevant in later eras where laïcité and anti-separatism measures create stable, discretely targetable differences in treatment. The overall arc is therefore a movement from a relatively high-autonomy post-war settlement toward a denser web of content-based and identity-adjacent restrictions, with the COVID block as a temporary extreme.

## **1945–1955: Fourth Republic Baseline – Base Score: 71**

The period begins with the Liberation and the establishment of the Fourth Republic. The dominant political project was the restoration of republican legality after Vichy and occupation, combined with the reconstruction of a democratic order under the shadow of the emerging Cold War and the unfinished colonial situation.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Retention of core criminal prohibitions on murder, rape, assault and related direct-harm offences (protective baseline, approximately 95–96 band).
* Limited application of the 1881 Press Law and early hate-speech provisions; enforcement intensity remained relatively narrow.
* Discriminatory higher age of consent for homosexual acts (age 21 versus 15 for heterosexual acts) – scored as paternalism at approximately 55.
* Absence of the later comprehensive anti-discrimination and identity-based speech apparatus.
* No systematic administrative house-arrest or gathering-ban regime of the 2015 type.
* Colonial-era movement and association controls existed for non-metropolitan populations but are treated as outside the metropolitan France baseline for this scoring (Algeria scored separately in later analysis).

The cumulative weight of the protective baseline minus the paternalistic age-of-consent restriction and the limited speech controls produces the opening score of 71. No Endos/Xenos split is applied; the restrictions were not structured around a stable, discretely targetable outgroup receiving markedly worse treatment within metropolitan France.

**Key Shift:** Establishment of the post-Liberation republican legal order after the collapse of Vichy, resetting the restrictive architecture to a relatively high-autonomy starting point.

### Why this produces a final score of 71

Negative-Only Weighting applied to the strong protective baseline (direct-harm prohibitions) reduced by the paternalistic age-of-consent differential and the modest contemporaneous speech controls yields 71. The score is consistent with a polity that had not yet layered the later ideological and identity-based expansions onto the republican framework.

## **1956–1958: Algeria War Expression Controls – Base Score: 64**

The intensification of the Algerian War produced a distinct restrictive episode focused on the suppression of advocacy and expression supporting Algerian independence or criticising French policy.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Systematic censorship, seizures, and legal actions against pro-Algeria advocacy and related political expression (scored in the 44–50 early ideological/identity-based controls band at approximately 46).
* Retention of the prior baseline (age-of-consent paternalism and limited hate-speech provisions).
* Emergency and public-order powers used to restrict association and demonstration in connection with the war.
* No general expansion of content-based speech offences beyond the Algeria-related targeting.

The new expression-suppression measures constitute the primary downward driver. The episode is short but intense enough to justify a distinct era under the guide’s durability and score-movement criteria.

**Key Shift:** Introduction of targeted, ideologically framed suppression of political expression linked to the Algerian conflict, moving the architecture below the Fourth Republic baseline.

### Why this produces a final score of 64

Previous baseline of 71 reduced by the material weight of the Algeria-related expression controls (approximately 46) produces 64 under Negative-Only Weighting. The restrictions were direct prohibitions on voluntary political speech and association.

## **1959–1974: Post-Algeria Recovery and Pleven Expansion – Base Score: 69**

The end of the Algerian War and the transition to the Fifth Republic allowed recovery from the specific war-related expression controls, but the period also saw the first major legislative expansion of hate-speech protections (Pleven Law 1972).

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Termination of the Algeria-specific expression suppression regime.
* Pleven Law (1972) broadening of hate-speech provisions and increasing the scope of prohibited expression, particularly against the far right.
* Continued operation of the paternalistic age-of-consent differential.
* Retention of core direct-harm prohibitions.
* No yet of the later Gayssot or glorification-of-terrorism layers.

The net effect is a partial recovery from the Algeria-era low, offset by the new downward weight of the expanded hate-speech framework.

**Key Shift:** End of war-related expression controls combined with the first significant post-war legislative expansion of content-based speech restrictions via the Pleven Law.

### Why this produces a final score of 69

Recovery from the 46-band Algeria measures raises the score from 64; the Pleven expansion and residual paternalism prevent a return to the 1945–1955 level, settling at 69.

---

## **1975–1982: Abortion Liberalisation and Residual Paternalism – Base Score: 72**

The Veil Law of 1975 replaced the previous blanket prohibition on abortion with a regulated-access framework, producing a clear upward movement on bodily autonomy, while the age-of-consent differential and post-Pleven hate-speech rules remained in force.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Veil Law: shift from blanket criminal prohibition (previously scored approximately 65, betterment-of-society band) to regulated access under health-related conditions (approximately 84, protection-of-the-vulnerable band).
* Continued paternalistic higher age of consent for homosexual acts (55).
* Continued post-Pleven hate-speech enforcement.
* No major new speech or association expansions in this short interval.

**Key Shift:** Material liberalisation of the abortion restriction, the single largest positive movement on bodily autonomy in the post-war period.

### Why this produces a final score of 72

The upward effect of moving abortion from a 65-band prohibition to an 84-band regulated framework outweighs the residual downward pressure of the age-of-consent rule and hate-speech baseline, producing 72.

---

## **1982–2015: Age-of-Consent Equalisation and Ideological Hate-Speech Peak – Base Score: 72**

Equalisation of the age of consent in 1982 removed a long-standing paternalistic restriction. The period is otherwise dominated by the strongest ideological enforcement of hate-speech laws in modern French history, including the Gayssot Law (1990) and expansive application of Article 24.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Legislative removal of the higher age of consent for homosexual acts (upward movement from the previous 55).
* Gayssot Law (1990): specific content ban on Holocaust denial / negationism with no truth defence and strong ideological character (approximately 43, early ideological restrictions).
* Broad and selective enforcement of Article 24 (incitement to hatred, discrimination, violence) with increasing ideological application.
* Retention of the post-Veil abortion framework.
* No State of Emergency or glorification-of-terrorism layers yet.

The upward effect of the 1982 equalisation is largely offset by the intensified ideological use of hate-speech instruments, producing a net score identical to the preceding era.

**Key Shift:** Removal of the last major formal sexual-orientation paternalism combined with the institutionalisation of strong content-based and historical-memory speech restrictions.

### Why this produces a final score of 72

Negative-Only Weighting: the removal of the 55-band restriction raises the score; the addition and aggressive enforcement of Gayssot and related Article 24 measures pull it back to 72. The two movements cancel, leaving the Base Score unchanged from the previous era.

## **November 2015 – February 2020: State of Emergency and Security Expansion – Base Score: 58**

The November 2015 attacks triggered a formal State of Emergency whose administrative powers (house arrests, gathering bans, searches) and the parallel expansion of glorification-of-terrorism provisions produced the largest sustained drop in the post-war period prior to COVID.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Administrative house arrests under the State of Emergency (restriction on movement, scored near Control Orders levels, approximately 84-band weight when active).
* Bans on public gatherings and demonstrations (association restriction, approximately 20–25 band when broadly applied).
* Aggressive use of glorification-of-terrorism provisions (expression restriction with vague standards, approximately 58).
* Retention of the full prior hate-speech and Gayssot architecture.
* Formal end of the emergency in 2017 but lingering effects and normalised security tools through early 2020.

**Key Shift:** Introduction of sweeping administrative (non-criminal) restrictions on movement and association plus broadened expression controls under the terrorism rubric.

### Why this produces a final score of 58

The cumulative weight of house-arrest powers, gathering bans, and glorification provisions, layered on the already elevated hate-speech baseline, produces the sharp drop to 58 under Negative-Only Weighting.

---

## **March 2020 – early 2022: COVID Restrictions Block – Base Score: 40**

Treated as a single block per the established UK/Germany/France COVID protocol. Nationwide lockdowns, curfews, gathering bans, *pass sanitaire*, and related medical and movement mandates constituted the most severe multi-domain restriction of personal autonomy in the post-war period.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Nationwide lockdowns and curfews (movement restriction, approximately 18–22).
* Broad bans on gatherings and association (approximately 20–25).
* Medical mandates and *pass sanitaire* requirements conditioning access to normal activities (bodily/medical autonomy, approximately 25–30).
* School and business closures contributing to the overall restrictive weight.
* Temporary character provides only modest mitigation under the protocol.

**Key Shift:** Unprecedented breadth and duration of direct restrictions on movement, association, education, work, and bodily autonomy.

### Why this produces a final score of 40

Single-block scoring of the cumulative COVID restrictions yields 40, the lowest era score in the entire post-1945 series. Negative-Only Weighting and the protocol’s treatment of temporary measures are applied consistently with prior country documents.

---

## **Mid-2022 onward: Post-COVID Recovery with Persistent Expression Controls – Base Score: 67**

Lifting of the most extreme COVID measures produced a substantial recovery. The recovery is incomplete because the pre-existing hate-speech, glorification-of-terrorism, and security architecture remained in force and, in some respects, continued to be applied with politicised intensity.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Termination of lockdowns, curfews, *pass sanitaire*, and the most sweeping gathering bans.
* Retention of the full post-2015 security and glorification-of-terrorism framework.
* Ongoing and in some domains intensified enforcement of Article 24, Gayssot, and related speech offences.
* Continued laïcité and anti-separatism measures (religious expression and association).
* 2022 constitutional protection of abortion (limited additional upward effect; the substantive liberalisation had already occurred in 1975).

**Key Shift:** Removal of the temporary COVID superstructure while the denser post-2015 speech and security baseline remains.

### Why this produces a final score of 67

Strong upward movement from the elimination of COVID restrictions is partially offset by the persistent weight of politicised hate-speech enforcement and residual security powers, producing a Base Score of 67 – a clear recovery from 40 but still below the 1982–2015 plateau of 72 and well below the early post-war levels.
