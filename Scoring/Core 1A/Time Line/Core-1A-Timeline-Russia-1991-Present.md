# Core 1A: Eras of Personal Autonomy in Russia (1991–Present)

## Introductory Overview

Post-Soviet Russia began with a sharp liberalisation of speech and association relative to the late Soviet period, producing a relatively high Base Score in the 1990s. From the early 2000s the trajectory reversed through successive layers of extremism legislation, media controls, “foreign agent” and “undesirable organisation” regimes, public-morality restrictions (including the “gay propaganda” law), tightened protest rules, and, after February 2022, comprehensive wartime speech prohibitions. The result is a low-autonomy equilibrium in which the formal multi-party system operates inside a dense restrictive framework. Endos/Xenos differentials appear where additional intensity is directed at designated categories (LGBTQ expression, certain political dissidents, and “foreign agents”).

The long-term pattern is therefore one of initial post-collapse opening followed by progressive re-densification of direct restrictions on voluntary expression, association and related domains. The largest single downward movements occur with the early-2000s re-consolidation, the 2012–2021 foreign-agent and morality layer, and the post-2022 wartime speech regime. No major liberalising repeal of the core restrictive statutes has occurred.

## **1991–1999: Post-Soviet Opening – Base Score: 65**

The collapse of the Soviet control system produced a rapid expansion of press freedom, political association and public expression. The dominant political project of the early Yeltsin years was the dismantling of the formal censorship apparatus and the Party monopoly on organisation, creating a relatively open environment for voluntary speech and association by the standards of both the late Soviet period and the later Russian present.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Dismantling of formal pre-publication censorship (Glavlit) and the Communist Party’s monopoly on association and media.
* Emergence of independent newspapers, television channels, political parties and civil-society organisations on a scale not seen since 1917.
* Residual criminal speech provisions remained on the books but were enforced with sharply reduced intensity compared with the Soviet period.
* No comprehensive “foreign agent,” extremism or wartime speech regime of the later type.
* Core criminal prohibitions on violence and direct harm were retained as the protective baseline.
* Exit controls were relaxed relative to the late Soviet period, expanding external movement autonomy.

The restrictive architecture of the era is defined by the abrupt removal of the Soviet multi-domain control system and the absence of the later layers of extremism, foreign-agent and wartime legislation. The practical environment for political and public expression was substantially freer than at any previous point in the twentieth century for the territory of the Russian Federation. Negative-Only Weighting records the substantial reduction in direct restrictions as an upward movement from the late Soviet baseline.

**Key Shift:** Abrupt removal of the Soviet multi-domain control apparatus and the emergence of a relatively open environment for voluntary speech and association.

### Why this produces a final score of 65

Negative-Only Weighting applied to the substantial reduction in direct restrictions yields a Base Score of 65. The score reflects a system that had shed the comprehensive Soviet controls without yet constructing the denser post-2000 restrictive architecture. No Endos/Xenos split is applied for this period.

## **2000–2011: Re-consolidation – Endos 48 / Xenos 42**

Recentralisation under the first Putin presidency included the early development of extremism legislation, pressure on independent media, and the beginning of tighter controls on political association and protest. These measures began to reverse the 1990s opening. Media ownership changes and regulatory pressure reduced the space for independent television and critical journalism.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Early extremism legislation used against political and religious expression, introducing content-based restrictions with vague standards that could be applied selectively.
* Media ownership changes and regulatory pressure that reduced the space for independent television and critical journalism; major independent outlets faced ownership changes or closure.
* Tightening of rules on public assembly and political organisation, increasing the administrative burden on protest organisers and opposition groups.
* Retention of residual 1990s liberalisations but with declining practical effect as enforcement intensity rose.
* Differential intensity began to appear against certain political and religious targets.
* Core direct-harm prohibitions remained in place.

The restrictive profile is characterised by the re-introduction of systematic administrative and legal controls on speech and association after the relatively open 1990s. The cumulative weight of extremism laws, media pressure and assembly restrictions produced a clear downward movement from the post-Soviet peak. Negative-Only Weighting records the addition of these new restrictive layers.

**Key Shift:** Re-introduction of systematic administrative and legal controls on speech and association, beginning the reversal of the post-Soviet opening.

### Why this produces Endos 48 / Xenos 42

Cumulative new restrictive layers produce a clear downward movement from 65. A limited Xenos differential is recorded because enforcement intensity was already higher against certain political and religious categories. Negative-Only Weighting applies throughout.

## **2012–2021: Foreign-Agent and Morality Layer – Endos 35 / Xenos 28**

The period after the 2011–2012 protests saw the rapid expansion of the “foreign agent” and “undesirable organisation” regimes, the “gay propaganda” law, and further tightening of protest and assembly rules. These measures densified the restrictive environment substantially. The foreign-agent designation imposed direct administrative and expressive burdens, including mandatory labelling of all materials and detailed reporting.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Foreign-agent registration and labelling requirements for NGOs, media and later individuals (Federal Law 121-FZ of 2012, repeatedly expanded).
* “Undesirable organisation” designations that criminalised involvement with certain foreign or international groups.
* Federal law of 2013 prohibiting “propaganda of non-traditional sexual relations” to minors, later expanded, creating content-based restrictions on LGBTQ-related expression.
* Expanded rules on public assembly, notification requirements and restrictions on protest that increased the practical barriers to collective expression.
* Continued and intensified use of extremism legislation against political and religious targets.
* The cumulative effect was a multi-domain tightening of voluntary expression and association, with clear differential intensity against designated categories.

The era is defined by the addition of targeted morality, foreign-influence and protest-control layers on top of the earlier extremism and media controls. The foreign-agent regime in particular created a durable administrative tool for stigmatising and constraining independent civil society and media. Negative-Only Weighting records the further downward movement under the cumulative weight of the post-2012 architecture.

**Key Shift:** Addition of the foreign-agent regime, LGBTQ-related expression restrictions and tightened protest rules, producing a further durable reduction in autonomy.

### Why this produces Endos 35 / Xenos 28

Further downward movement under Negative-Only Weighting. The Xenos differential widens because the new measures (especially foreign-agent rules and the propaganda law) create stable, discretely targetable differences in treatment for designated groups.

## **2022–Present: Wartime Speech Regime – Endos 22 / Xenos 18**

Following the full-scale invasion of Ukraine, Russia enacted comprehensive wartime speech legislation criminalising the “discrediting” of the armed forces and the dissemination of “false information” about the military. These provisions, layered on the pre-existing foreign-agent, extremism and morality framework, produced the lowest post-1991 scores. Investigative Committee data indicate more than 600 criminal cases opened under the wartime speech articles between 2022 and early 2025.

### Core 1A Relevant Restrictions / Elements – Expanded Analysis

* Criminalisation of public actions or statements aimed at “discrediting” the use of the armed forces (Article 280.3 Criminal Code), with significant penalties including imprisonment.
* Criminalisation of the dissemination of “knowingly false information” about the armed forces and their activities (Article 207.3), with maximum penalties reaching 15 years.
* Administrative counterparts under the Code of Administrative Offences that supply lower-level sanctions and can escalate to criminal liability on repetition.
* Intensified enforcement of foreign-agent, undesirable-organisation and extremism provisions.
* Broad online and media controls, including blocking of materials judged to contain false or discrediting information.
* Continued operation of the “gay propaganda” and related morality restrictions, expanded in 2022 to all ages.

The restrictive profile is defined by the addition of a comprehensive wartime speech-restriction regime on top of an already dense pre-2022 architecture. The cumulative multi-domain system now in force produces the lowest scores in the post-1991 series. Negative-Only Weighting records the heavy additional weight of the wartime provisions.

**Key Shift:** Introduction of a comprehensive wartime speech-restriction layer criminalising criticism and certain reporting related to the armed forces and the war.

### Why this produces Endos 22 / Xenos 18

Heavy additional weight of the wartime provisions, added to the prior foreign-agent, extremism and morality architecture, produces the lowest scores in the post-1991 series. Negative-Only Weighting applies. The Xenos differential continues because designated categories remain subject to heightened intensity.
