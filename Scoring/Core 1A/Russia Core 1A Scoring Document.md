# Russia Core 1A Scoring Document

**Version:** 1.0 (4 June 2026)  
**Status:** Initial Draft – Partially Complete

This document follows the same structure and quality standards as the UK, USA, and PRC Core 1A scoring documents. It records all Core 1A (Personal Autonomy) analysis for Russia from the post-Soviet period to the present, with a focus on the Putin eras.

Only direct restrictions on personal autonomy are scored. Processes, enforcement actions, consequences (such as chilling effects), Core 1B elements, and political structure changes are excluded.

---

## 1. Methodology & Rules Applied

All scoring follows the locked rules in the current **Core 1A Scoring Guide v2.1** and the master `Policies.md`.

Key principles applied to Russia:
- Strict **direct restriction only** (punishment vs restriction, process vs restriction).
- **Negative-only weighting**.
- **Current-policy basis**.
- Many governance, administrative, and economic measures were classified as outside Core 1A scope or better suited to Core 1B.
- Wartime policies (especially Chechen wars) were heavily debated; many were treated as falling under military operations or Core 2B rather than domestic personal autonomy restrictions.

**Key Points from Thread**:

- Distinction was made between economic/administrative mechanisms (often Core 1B) and the resulting editorial/content control (potential Core 1A).

**Status**: Partially scored (editorial control element accepted at 28–36). Needs clean separation of economic mechanisms from speech/information restrictions.

### Putin 1 & 2 Eras – Assembly and Protest Restrictions
**Key Points from Thread**:

### General Classification Challenges Identified in Thread
**Recurring Issues**:
- Many Russia policies sit at the boundary between Core 1A (personal autonomy) and Core 1B (economic autonomy / state control of assets).
- Distinguishing between "governance/administrative process" and "direct restriction on individuals".
- Treatment of measures justified on national security or stability grounds.
- Wartime vs domestic application (especially Chechnya).

These classification debates consumed significant thread time and are the main reason more Russia policies were not finalized into clean scored entries.

## 3. Fully Scored Policies (Element Level)

Very few Russia-specific policies have been fully dissected and scored under the current 5-category element-based system. The following represent the cleanest scored elements identified so far:

### Media Control & Editorial Alignment (Putin 1–2)
**Neutered Base Policy**: Replacement of management at independent national television networks with Kremlin-aligned personnel and enforcement of editorial line alignment.

**Core 1A Score**: **28–36** (where scored as ideological enforcement / police state)

**Elements**:
- Imposition of full editorial control on formerly independent national networks → **28–36**
- Replacement of management with state-aligned personnel → Often classified as administrative / Core 1B (economic control of assets)

**Status**: Partially scored. The editorial control element was accepted under ideological enforcement. The corporate takeover mechanics were largely moved to Core 1B.

### Restrictions on Public Assemblies and Protests (Multiple Eras)
**Neutered Base Policy**: Laws and practices granting authorities broad powers to restrict or prohibit public assemblies, including on grounds of public order, national security, or "extremism".

**Core 1A Score**: Varies significantly by justification and implementation (60–69 or higher when used to suppress political dissent).

**Elements**:
- Power to ban or impose heavy conditions on protests → **60–69** (betterment of society) or higher when applied to political content.
- Use of "extremism" or anti-terror laws against political opponents → **28–36** (ideological enforcement / police state)

**Status**: Partially scored. Clear pattern identified but full element breakdown for each era not yet completed.

## 4. Partially Analysed / Discussed Eras (Block Form for Future Work)

These eras were discussed in detail but not fully scored. Key findings and open questions are recorded below for efficient continuation.

### Putin 1 Era (2000 – mid/late 2000s)
**Key Elements Discussed**:
- State-controlled corporation (Gazprom-Media) used to enforce debt claims and seize private media assets (NTV takeover).
- Physical seizure of broadcast facilities and forced management replacement (April 2001 NTV studio takeover).
- Court-ordered liquidation and licence revocation of remaining independent channel (TV-6, January 2002).
- Imposition of full editorial control on formerly independent national networks.

**Classification Issues**:
- Many elements were argued to be **administrative / governance** or **Core 1B** (economic autonomy / state control of corporations).
- Editorial control element was the strongest candidate for Core 1A scoring (28–36 band).
- User argued that state ownership itself is not the issue — only how it is used.

**Status**: Not fully scored. Needs clean element dissection separating economic/administrative aspects from direct speech/editorial restrictions.

### Additional Information from Thread Extraction – Putin 1 Era
**Media Takeovers (Gazprom-Media / NTV / TV-6)**:
- Detailed discussion of how state-controlled corporations were used to acquire and control independent television networks.
- Physical seizure of facilities and forced management changes (e.g. April 2001 NTV).
- Court-ordered liquidation of TV-6 (January 2002).
- Resulting imposition of editorial alignment with Kremlin positions.
- Strong user position that the economic mechanism (debt enforcement, corporate takeover) is largely Core 1B, while the editorial control outcome has Core 1A implications (information and expression control).

**Assembly and Protest Environment**:
- Early signs of tightening restrictions on public demonstrations and opposition activity.
- Use of existing public order laws in ways that disproportionately affected political opponents.

**Overall Assessment from Thread**:
- Putin 1 era was heavily analysed but much of the work remained at the level of classification debates rather than finalized element-level scoring.
- The era shows a clear pattern of increasing state control over information flows, but the mechanical path (corporate/economic vs direct legal restriction) was contested.

### Chechen Wars (Especially Second Chechen War)
**Key Issues Discussed**:
- Whether restrictions and military operations in Chechnya count as Core 1A domestic personal autonomy violations or as wartime / Core 2B measures.
- Distinction between "civil unrest" framing (Ukraine view of Donbas) vs "separate entity / war" framing.
- Application of restrictions only inside the conflict zone vs nationwide.

**Classification Issues**:
- Strong argument that many measures were legitimate wartime policies against armed separatists rather than general domestic restrictions on the Russian population.
- User noted that if Chechnya is treated as a de facto separate entity during active conflict, then Russian actions are closer to Core 2B (external/wartime) than Core 1A.

**Status**: Not scored under Core 1A. Decision pending clearer framework for wartime vs domestic restrictions.

### Later Putin Eras (Foreign Agents Law, Undesirable Organizations, etc.)
**Key Elements Discussed**:
- Foreign Agents Law and its expansion.
- Undesirable Organizations Law.
- Increasing restrictions on assemblies and protests.
- Growing surveillance and monitoring requirements.

**Classification Issues**:
- Many elements sit in the **28–36** ideological enforcement / police state band when applied to political dissent.
- Some elements (especially financial reporting and registration requirements) were viewed as more administrative than direct personal autonomy restrictions.

**Status**: Partially mapped but not fully element-dissected or scored across eras.

### Additional Information from Thread Extraction – Putin 2 Era
**Key Developments Discussed**:
- Continuation and consolidation of media control established in Putin 1.
- Early versions and expansion of laws targeting "foreign agents" and foreign-funded organizations.
- Increasing use of administrative and legal tools to restrict opposition protests and public assemblies.
- Growing integration of surveillance and monitoring practices into domestic governance.

**Classification Notes from Thread**:
- Foreign agent / undesirable organization laws were viewed as having both administrative components and direct impacts on freedom of association and expression.
- Protest restrictions were discussed in terms of both "betterment of society" (60–69) and "ideological enforcement" (28–36) depending on selective application against political opponents.

**Overall Assessment from Thread**:
- Putin 2 built upon the foundations of Putin 1 with more formalized legal tools.
- Significant discussion occurred, but again much of it remained in classification and scoping debates rather than producing finalized scored policy entries.

## 5. Notes on Integration & Next Steps

- Russia has received significantly less complete scoring than the UK, USA, or PRC.
- The main challenge has been distinguishing **direct personal autonomy restrictions** from **governance/administrative measures** and **economic control** (Core 1B).
- Wartime policies (Chechnya) require a clearer boundary rule between Core 1A and Core 2B.
- Recommended next step: Perform a clean element-level dissection of the major media control and assembly restriction policies from Putin 1 and Putin 2 eras, then score them consistently with the current 5-category system.
