## 1. Purpose of This Manual

This document provides a complete, detailed, and replicable methodology for scoring any country on **Core 2A (National Sovereignty)**. It is intended for use in Layer 3 of the project — the technical scoring layer — where full justifications, granular breakdowns, and methodological transparency are required.

The goal is to produce consistent, defensible scores that can support the higher layers (Layer 2: Slider rules & index; Layer 1: Front-facing summaries and comparisons).

---

## 2. Core 2A Definition

Core 2A measures the observable degree to which a nation maintains or surrenders control over its own **territory, borders, laws, institutions, currency, military, and decision-making authority** versus ceding that control to external entities, supranational organisations, or international agreements.

It tracks the boundary between **national self-determination** and **external governance**.

**Key Focus**: Internal/national sovereignty. Constraints that primarily govern activity **outside** national territory (e.g. High Seas, deep seabed, international straits) are generally rerouted to Core 2B.

---

## 3. Non-Negotiable Core Principles

1. **Observable Mechanical Effect**  
   Scores must be based exclusively on what is actually occurring in practice — enforcement, outcomes, and real constraints — never on rhetoric, official claims, or aspirational policy.

2. **Negative-Only Weighting**  
   Only measurable restrictions or losses of control reduce the score. The absence of constraints, or political opposition to them, does not generate positive credit.

3. **Primary Drag Dominance**  
   When component scores are significantly apart, the lowest scores exert disproportionate influence on the final result. Higher-scoring elements become largely irrelevant. This prevents "averaging away" major weaknesses.

4. **Internal vs External Sovereignty**  
   Core 2A is strictly about control over the nation’s own territory and institutions. External behaviour and obligations in international spaces are rerouted.

5. **EEZ Exception**  
   Sovereign rights over the **economic output** of the Exclusive Economic Zone (living and non-living resources) count toward national sovereignty. Most other EEZ obligations (navigation, overflight, etc.) do not.

6. **Redundancy Rule**  
   If an international obligation is fully superseded by existing domestic law, it is discarded or heavily discounted.

## 4. Step-by-Step Scoring Process

### Step 1: Identify Major Constraints
Compile a comprehensive list of all significant external constraints affecting the country. Include:
- Supranational bodies and treaties
- Border and migration arrangements
- Legal removal constraints
- Internal border or regulatory alignment agreements
- Major international organisations with enforcement mechanisms

### Step 2: Apply Rerouting Filter
For every constraint ask:
- Does its dominant mechanical effect impact **internal control** over the nation’s own territory, borders, laws, or institutions?
  - Yes → Retain on Core 2A
  - No → Reroute to Core 2B (International Sovereignty)

**Common Rerouting Examples**:
- High Seas, The Area, straits regime, marine scientific research → Core 2B
- Dispute settlement mechanisms between states → Core 2B

### Step 3: Granular Breakdown & Redundancy Check
Break each retained constraint into its smallest mechanical rules. For each rule ask two binary questions:

1. **Does it apply to the country?** (Yes/No)
2. **Is it superseded by existing domestic law?** (Yes/No)

Only rules that are **both applicable and not superseded** remain for scoring.

### Step 4: Assign Isolated Scores
For each surviving granular element:
- Score it as if it were the **only** constraint on a perfect 100 baseline.
- Consider **scope** (how broad the impact is) and **scale** (how significant the impact is).
- Justify using specific bullet points from the Core 2A band descriptors.

### Step 5: Holistic Scoring with Primary Drag Dominance
Combine all isolated scores:
- Identify the lowest (dominant) scores.
- Apply heavier weight to significantly lower scores.
- Produce a single holistic score in the appropriate band.
- Document the reasoning, especially how primary drag dominance influenced the final number.

### Step 6: Era and Country Context
Place the current score in historical context by comparing it to previous eras. Highlight what changed and why the score moved.

### Step 7: Party Analysis (if required)
For each party:
- Classify as **Nationalist**, **Internationalist**, or **Mixed Cooperation**.
- Use the modular framework, selecting only relevant sections.

---

## 5. Party Classification Guidelines

- **Nationalist**: Prioritises maximising unilateral national control over territory, borders, and decision-making.
- **Internationalist**: Prioritises deeper international integration and shared sovereignty.
- **Mixed Cooperation**: Takes a hybrid approach — reclaims sovereignty in some areas while prioritising international integration in others.

For **Internationalist** parties, reframe the Overall Assessment around levels of international integration rather than loss of sovereignty.

## 7. Final Scoring Guidelines

- Always justify scores with specific bullet points from the Core 2A band descriptors where possible.
- Be transparent about all judgment calls and rerouting decisions.
- Document why elements were discarded or rerouted.
- For party analysis, clearly state the classification and use the modular framework.

---

## 8. Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

- **Over-counting external obligations** → Always ask: Does this affect control over our own territory?
- **Averaging away major weaknesses** → Apply primary drag dominance rigorously.
- **Confusing intent with outcome** → Score based on mechanical effect, not stated goals.
- **Forcing binary classification** → Use "Mixed Cooperation" for grey-area parties.
