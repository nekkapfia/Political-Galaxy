## 1. Purpose and Scope of This Manual

This manual provides a complete, detailed, and replicable methodology for scoring any country on **Core 2A (National Sovereignty)**. It is intended for use in Layer 3 (the technical scoring layer) of the 14-slider project.

The goal is to produce documents that are sufficiently detailed and justified to support the higher layers of the project (Layer 2: Slider rules & index; Layer 1: Front-facing summaries).

This is a **dense technical manual**, not a summary. It prioritises depth and clarity of process over brevity.

---

## 2. Core 2A Definition

Core 2A measures the observable degree to which a nation maintains or surrenders control over its own **territory, borders, laws, institutions, currency, military, and decision-making authority** versus ceding that control to external entities, supranational organisations, or international agreements.

It tracks the boundary between national self-determination and external governance.

**Core Focus**: Internal/national sovereignty. Constraints that primarily govern external behaviour or activity outside national territory are generally rerouted to Core 2B.

---

## 3. Non-Negotiable Core Principles

### 3.1 Observable Mechanical Effect
Scores must be based exclusively on what is actually occurring in practice — enforcement, legal effects, and real-world outcomes — never on rhetoric, official claims, or aspirational policy.

### 3.2 Negative-Only Weighting
Only measurable restrictions or losses of control reduce the score. The absence of constraints, or political opposition to them, does not generate positive credit.

### 3.3 Primary Drag Dominance
When component scores are significantly apart, the lowest scores exert disproportionate influence on the final result. Higher-scoring elements become largely irrelevant. The final score should reflect the dominant drags rather than a simple average.

### 3.4 Internal vs External Sovereignty
Core 2A only scores constraints that affect control over the nation’s own territory and institutions. Rules governing activity outside national territory (High Seas, The Area, international straits, etc.) are generally rerouted to Core 2B.

### 3.5 EEZ Economic Exception
Sovereign rights over the economic output of the Exclusive Economic Zone (living and non-living resources) count toward national sovereignty. Most other EEZ obligations (navigation, overflight, scientific research, etc.) do not.

### 3.6 Redundancy Rule
If an international obligation is fully superseded or covered by existing domestic law, it should be discarded or heavily discounted.

## 4. Detailed Step-by-Step Scoring Process

### Step 1: Identify All Major Constraints
Create a comprehensive list of every significant external constraint affecting the country. This includes:
- Supranational legal systems and treaties with enforcement mechanisms
- Border control and migration arrangements
- Removal and deportation constraints
- Internal border or regulatory alignment agreements
- Major international organisations that impose binding rules

### Step 2: Apply Rerouting Filter
For each constraint, determine its **dominant mechanical effect** by asking:
- Does this primarily affect the country’s control over its own territory, borders, laws, or institutions?
- Or does it primarily govern external relations or activity outside national territory?

**Rerouting Examples**:
- High Seas regime, The Area, straits used for international navigation → Core 2B
- Marine scientific research in international waters → Core 2B
- Most dispute settlement mechanisms between states → Core 2B

### Step 3: Granular Breakdown (Critical Step)
Break every retained constraint into its smallest mechanical rules. For each rule, answer these questions clearly:

1. Does this specific rule apply to the country?
2. Is this rule already fully superseded or covered by domestic law?
3. What is the scope (breadth) and scale (significance) of this rule?

Only rules that are both applicable **and** not superseded should proceed to scoring. Document all decisions.

### Step 4: Detailed Scoring of Individual Components
For every surviving component:
- Write a short **Mechanical Description** explaining what the rule actually does.
- Assign an isolated score using the Core 2A band descriptors.
- Justify the score by referencing a specific bullet point from the slider.
- Consider both scope and scale when choosing the band.

### Step 5: Holistic Scoring with Primary Drag Dominance
Combine all component scores into a single holistic score:
- Identify the lowest (dominant) scores.
- Apply heavier weight to significantly lower scores.
- Produce a final score in the appropriate band.
- Explicitly explain in the document how primary drag dominance influenced the final result.

### Step 6: Place Score in Historical Context
Compare the current score with previous eras. Explain what changed and why the score moved up or down.

### Step 7: Party Analysis (When Required)
For each party:
- Classify as **Nationalist**, **Internationalist**, or **Mixed Cooperation**.
- Use the modular framework and only include relevant sections.
- For internationalist parties, reframe the Overall Assessment around levels of international integration.

## 5. Party Classification and Analysis Framework

### 5.1 Classification Categories
- **Nationalist**: Prioritises maximising unilateral national control over territory, borders, and decision-making.
- **Internationalist**: Prioritises deeper international integration and shared sovereignty.
- **Mixed Cooperation**: Takes a hybrid approach — reclaims sovereignty in some areas while prioritising international integration in others.

### 5.2 Modular Party Analysis Framework
Use only the sections that are relevant to the specific party:

1. Party Name
2. Policy Positions (short bullet list)
3. Relevant Claims (more detailed, with scores where applicable)
4. Position After Changes (what major constraints are removed or altered)
5. Component Assessment (detailed breakdown of specific areas)
6. Dominant Issues (summary table)
7. Overall Assessment (reframe for internationalist parties)
8. Final Score

## 7. Quality Standards for Layer 3 Documents

- Every major constraint in the Current Era should have its own short analysis with a Mechanical Description.
- Primary drag dominance must be explicitly addressed when explaining the final score.
- All rerouting and exclusion decisions must be clearly documented.
- Party analyses should maintain structural consistency while adapting to the party’s complexity.
- Justifications should reference specific bullet points from the Core 2A band descriptors wherever possible.

---

## 8. Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

- **Skipping the granular breakdown step** → Always break constraints into their smallest rules before scoring.
- **Averaging scores instead of applying primary drag dominance** → When scores are significantly apart, the lowest scores should dominate the final result.
- **Writing vague justifications** → Use specific slider bullet points and mechanical descriptions.
- **Over-counting external obligations** → Strictly apply the internal vs external distinction.
- **Forcing grey-area parties into binary categories** → Use “Mixed Cooperation” for parties that take a hybrid approach.
