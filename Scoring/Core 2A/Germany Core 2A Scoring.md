## 1. Introduction & Methodology

This document assesses Germany’s position on **Core 2A (National Sovereignty)**. The slider measures the degree of effective unilateral control the state retains over its territory, borders, laws, regulatory authority, and decision-making, relative to external constraints.

Scoring follows these core principles:

- **Observable Mechanical Effect**: Scores are based on what is actually occurring in practice, not legal theory, political claims, or aspirational policy.
- **Negative-Only Weighting**: Only measurable restrictions or losses of control reduce the score. The absence of constraints, or political opposition to them, does not generate positive credit.
- **Era-Based Analysis**: Germany’s sovereignty position is assessed across distinct periods defined by major shifts in external legal arrangements, economic governance, and border regimes.
- **Component Breakdown**: Complex arrangements are broken into their constituent mechanical elements where this improves analytical clarity.
- **Current-Policy Basis**: Scoring reflects policies and constraints that are currently in force or actively maintained.
- **Primary Drag Dominance**: When scores are significantly apart, the lowest scores exert disproportionate influence on the final result.

Core 2A focuses exclusively on **state-level sovereignty**. It does not assess individual or firm-level economic autonomy (Core 1B) or personal autonomy (Core 1A), except where these intersect directly with national control over territory and borders.

## 3. Detailed Era Analysis

### Schröder Era (1998–2005) — Score: 53 (51–59 band)

Germany was already the dominant economic and political force within the European Union. Most developments in Single Market integration and early Eurozone rules reflected German policy preferences. Migration-related constraints were not yet significant. The mechanical effect on national sovereignty was moderate, resulting in a strong score.

### Merkel Era – Early (2005–2010) — Score: 52 (51–59 band)

Germany continued to exert strong influence over EU policy. Further integration occurred in justice and home affairs, but Germany retained substantial control and often shaped outcomes. The sovereignty cost of EU membership remained moderate.

### Eurozone Crisis Era (2010–2015) — Score: 49 (41–50 band)

Germany played the central role in designing the post-crisis Eurozone architecture (including the Banking Union, European Semester, and reformed fiscal rules). While some authority was transferred to the European level, Germany largely determined the direction of those transfers. The sovereignty impact was noticeable but not severe.

### Migration & Stability Era (2015–2022) — Score: 41 (41–50 band)

The 2015–2016 migration crisis exposed the clearest limitations on Germany’s unilateral control over its borders and removals due to EU asylum and responsibility rules. This period represents the lowest point for German sovereignty in the post-1998 era, although Germany retained significant influence and later secured greater flexibility.

### Current Era (2022–Present) — Score: 45 (41–50 band)

Germany maintains deep EU integration while playing a leading role in NATO. The primary constraints on sovereignty remain concentrated in migration and asylum policy, with a secondary constraint emerging in state aid flexibility. Most other areas of EU policy continue to reflect significant German influence.

## 4. Current Era Analysis (2022 – Present)

**Final Core 2A Score: 45 (41–50 band)**

### 4.1 European Union Influence and Sovereignty Impact

Germany’s membership in the European Union is the dominant external factor affecting its national sovereignty. As the largest and most influential member state, Germany exerts substantial structural power over EU decision-making. This position significantly reduces the sovereignty cost of EU integration in many policy areas.

After applying the Core 2A methodology (including primary drag dominance and Core 2B reclassification), the following constraints remain as meaningful limitations:

#### 4.1.1 Return / Deportation Rules

**Scoring Band**: 41–50 band

* “Several core functions (trade, migration, regulation) operate under substantial external agreements.”

**Mechanical Description**:  
Legal and procedural barriers that limit Germany’s ability to remove rejected asylum seekers and irregular migrants from its territory.

**Analysis**:  
While Germany retains operational capacity, EU rules and associated ECHR obligations create recurring legal and procedural friction. The mechanical effect is significant and ongoing. It aligns with the 41–50 band when combined with other constraints.

#### 4.1.2 Dublin System (Responsibility Rules)

**Scoring Band**: 41–50 band

* “Several core functions operate under substantial external agreements.”

**Mechanical Description**:  
Rules that allocate responsibility for asylum claims, restricting Germany’s freedom to manage inflows according to national preference.

**Analysis**:  
This creates a clear external legal rule that limits Germany’s control over who it must process and potentially accept. The mechanical impact is noticeable but does not rise to the level of completely uncontrolled flows.

#### 4.1.3 State Aid Rules

**Scoring Band**: 41–50 band

* “National decision-making remains dominant but faces observable external pressure in specific domains.”

**Mechanical Description**:  
Restrictions on Germany’s ability to provide targeted financial or regulatory support to domestic and strategic industries.

**Analysis**:  
Germany helped create the strict framework but now finds it constraining on its industrial policy ambitions. The mechanical effect is significant in specific domains but does not amount to a broad ceding of authority.

#### 4.1.4 Schengen Internal Border Controls

**Scoring Band**: 51–59 band

* “Noticeable constraints from treaties or organisations begin to constrain national policy options. Borders maintain strong controls but allow limited, highly managed immigration and trade routes.”

**Mechanical Description**:  
Limits on Germany’s ability to unilaterally control movement across its internal EU borders.

**Analysis**:  
Germany has successfully maintained internal border controls for extended periods through legal arguments. The constraint is real but manageable and does not fundamentally undermine border sovereignty.

#### 4.1.5 2024 Migration Pact – Solidarity Mechanism

**Scoring Band**: 41–50 band

* “National decision-making remains dominant but faces observable external pressure in specific domains.”

**Mechanical Description**:  
New binding solidarity obligations, though Germany secured significant flexibility during negotiations.

**Analysis**:  
Germany avoided the worst-case scenario but still accepted new obligations it cannot easily avoid. The mechanical effect is noticeable but not severe.

**EU-Related Sovereignty Score: 45 (41–50 band)**

### Alternative for Germany (AfD) (Nationalist)

**Policy Positions**
* Strongly restrict immigration and increase deportations.
* Reform or leave the Eurozone.
* Reduce EU competences and restore national sovereignty.
* More sceptical of NATO and deeper EU foreign policy alignment.

### Position After Changes

In a single parliamentary term, the AfD could deliver noticeable but limited improvements. It could accelerate deportations and tighten migration enforcement using existing legal tools. However, major structural changes — such as Eurozone reform or exit, or a significant rollback of EU competences — are not realistically achievable within four years. Over an extended period in government, the party could plausibly achieve a significantly higher score, though this would not be realistic within a single term.

### Dominant Issues

| Dominant Issues | Estimated Score |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| **EU Migration & Asylum Framework** | 46 |
| **State Aid Rules** | 49 |
| **Eurozone & Economic Governance** | 50 |

### Overall Assessment

Within one parliamentary term, an AfD government could make real progress on immigration enforcement and become a more difficult negotiating partner for Brussels. However, fundamental changes to Germany’s relationship with the EU — particularly regarding the Eurozone or the broader rollback of EU competences — would require either major treaty reform or a German exit from key parts of the EU. These outcomes are not realistically deliverable in a single 4-year term.

**Final Score: 49 (41–50 band)**

## 6. Routing & Exclusion Summary

**Routing Principles**  
Elements were assessed according to whether their dominant mechanical effect impacts internal/national sovereignty (Core 2A) or external/international sovereignty (Core 2B).

**Routed to Core 2B (International Sovereignty)**  
- Most elements of EU Common Foreign and Security Policy  
- Large portions of Single Market regulation, Eurozone governance, and Green Deal policy (where Germany was a primary driver or would likely maintain similar rules domestically)  
- Most elements of NATO operational structures

**Retained in Core 2A (National Sovereignty)**  
- EU Migration & Asylum Framework (particularly returns and responsibility rules)  
- State Aid rules  
- Schengen internal border control restrictions  
- 2024 Migration Pact – Solidarity Mechanism

**Exclusions**  
Elements fully superseded by German domestic law or with negligible mechanical effect on internal sovereignty were excluded from Core 2A scoring.
