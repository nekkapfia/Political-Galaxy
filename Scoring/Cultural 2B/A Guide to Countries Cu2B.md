# Guide: How to Produce a Cultural 2B Timeline Document for a Country

This guide explains how to create a high-quality document analysing shifts in a country’s emotional and cultural stance toward its national history, symbols, founding myths, people, continuity, and collective identity (Pride vs Shame). It is designed to produce consistent, well-structured, and analytically rigorous results.

## 1. Preparation and Scope Definition

**Define the time period**
- Choose a clear starting point that marks a significant baseline (e.g. 1945 for Britain, or a major constitutional, independence, or regime change for other countries).
- Decide whether the document will run to the present day.

**Define the analytical focus clearly**
- The document analyses the **emotional and cultural stance** a society takes toward its own nation.
- The central question is: To what extent is the nation’s history, symbols, founding myths, people, and continuity treated as a source of **pride and affirmation**, versus **shame, guilt, or active critique**?

**Strict scope boundaries**
Do **not** analyse:
- Allocation of rights and opportunities based on individual merit vs group identity (Cultural 2A)
- How the nation defines its core in-group (“us”) (Cultural 3A)
- Immigration, borders, or national sovereignty (Core 2A)
- Moral or ideological justifications for pride or shame (Cultural 1A / 1B)

Focus narrowly on the **observable emotional tone** toward the nation itself.

## 2. Research and Data Collection

Gather evidence focused on **observable expressions** of pride or shame. Prioritise:

- Government rhetoric and official policy language regarding national history and identity
- Education curricula and how national history is taught
- Treatment of national symbols, monuments, statues, and flags (removal, reframing, celebration, or restriction)
- Public rituals, commemorations, and national holidays
- Cultural output (films, television, literature, museums, public art)
- Significant political speeches and campaigns that address national pride or historical shame
- Party manifestos and platforms (used mainly for the Political Parties section)
- Institutional and elite discourse (media, academia, civil service)

Focus on **what was actually done and said** in public and institutional life rather than private opinion.

## 3. Segmenting History into Eras (High Granularity First)

**Critical rule:** Start by creating **more eras than you initially think are necessary**. It is far easier to merge similar eras later than to split an era that has become too broad.

**Good reasons to create a new era:**
- A clear shift in the dominant emotional tone toward national history or identity
- Major political or cultural events that significantly alter national self-perception
- Periods of sustained political contestation over national identity and history
- Significant changes in how national symbols are treated

Aim for relatively short, coherent periods rather than long eras that contain multiple distinct phases.

## 4. Scoring Each Era

Use the official **Cultural 2B bands (0–100)**.

**Scoring approach:**
- Assess the **dominant emotional and cultural stance** toward the nation’s history, symbols, founding myths, people, continuity, and collective identity.
- Consider both elite/institutional attitudes and broader public/political discourse.
- Be consistent. Give greater weight to sustained patterns rather than isolated events.
- Justify the score with reference to specific, observable expressions (rhetoric, policy, treatment of symbols, education, public rituals, etc.).

## 5. Writing the Era Sections

Use a consistent structure for every era. Maintain similar depth and analytical quality across all eras.

### Recommended Structure

**Heading format (use exactly this style):**  
## **StartYear–EndYear: Era Name – Final Score: XX**

**Intro paragraph** (3 to 5 sentences)

**Subheading:**  
### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- Bullet point 1
- Bullet point 2
- Bullet point 3
- Bullet point 4 (optional)

**Analysis paragraphs** (1–2 paragraphs)

**Key Shift sentence** at the end of every era (this is mandatory).

**Rules for Era Sections:**
- Keep eras reasonably concise but with sufficient analytical depth.
- Every single era **must** have a **Key Shift** sentence at the end.
- Focus on observable expressions of pride or shame rather than justifications.
- Use 3–5 bullets maximum.

## 6. Refining the Eras

After completing a first draft:

- Review the full set of eras and ask:
  - Can any eras be merged without losing important nuance?
  - Does any era contain distinct phases that would be better separated?
  - Are there periods where content could be better redistributed between adjacent eras?
- Merge eras that show very similar emotional stances toward national identity.
- Keep eras that represent meaningful shifts in the balance between pride and shame.
- Aim for the smallest number of eras that still preserves the important turning points.

## 7. Writing the Political Parties Section

Include a Political Parties section at the end of the document.  
**Parties should always be arranged alphabetically.**

**Structure:**
- Short overall summary of the current state of national pride vs shame.
- Summary table with party names and Final Scores.
- Individual party entries with this format:
  - Brief Overview
  - Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis (with bullets)
  - Why This Produces a Final Score of XX

Base this section on current party platforms, rhetoric, and record (especially where parties have held power or significant influence). Focus on what each party communicates and does regarding national history, symbols, and pride.

## 8. Final Assembly and Quality Control

**Include these elements in the final document:**
- Opening summary paragraph (no main title above it)
- Era Summary Table
- All individual era sections (in chronological order)
- # Political Parties heading
- Political Parties intro paragraph (with bridging reference to the eras)
- Table of parties
- All individual party sections (alphabetically)

**Quality Standards:**
- All eras should be written to a similar depth and analytical standard.
- The Overview section should identify the overall trajectory and main drivers of change in national pride vs shame.
- Repetitive phrasing across eras should be minimised.
- Later eras must receive the same level of analytical attention as earlier eras.
- Scoring justifications must be tied to observable expressions of pride or shame.
- Clear bridging language should connect the historical eras section with the Political Parties section.

## 9. Recommended Number of Eras

| Time Span       | Recommended Number of Eras | Guidance |
|-----------------|----------------------------|----------|
| 30–50 years     | 6–10                       | Start with more, then merge |
| 70–80+ years    | 9–14                       | Start with 10–12, then refine |

## Summary Workflow

1. Define the time period and starting point.
2. Research observable expressions of national pride and shame.
3. Create an initial high-granularity set of eras.
4. Score each era using the Cultural 2B bands.
5. Write the era sections to a consistent standard (including Key Shift sentences).
6. Review and refine the eras (merge where appropriate).
7. Write the Political Parties section.
8. Write the opening summary paragraph (make it analytical).
9. Review the full document against the quality standards above.
10. Edit for repetition, consistency of depth, and analytical clarity.

This process is designed to produce a rigorous, consistent, and analytically strong Cultural 2B timeline document.
