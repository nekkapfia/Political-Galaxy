Germany’s post-1998 emotional and cultural stance toward its national history, symbols, founding myths, people, continuity, and collective identity remains firmly inside the 10–19 (Guilt) band. The dominant pattern is one of systematic de-emphasis, de-nationalisation, and institutional prioritisation of atonement over pride or continuity. Traditional German national identity is treated as largely illegitimate or dangerous in education, media, and elite institutions. Post-1945 public space shows near-absence of new traditional national statues of longer German history or achievement, combined with heavy institutional investment in atonement and victim memorials, critical reinterpretation of remaining older symbols, and selective colonial-era renamings. Even the limited pride that is socially acceptable is restricted almost exclusively to the post-1945 Federal Republic — a construct deliberately built in opposition to traditional German identity — and is routinely de-nationalised or subordinated to European and historical-responsibility frames. These patterns hold across the major parties, with only the AfD offering a partial and constrained challenge.

## **1998–2005: Schröder Red-Green Government – Critical Normalisation – Final Score: 14**

Gerhard Schröder’s Red-Green government made limited rhetorical gestures toward “normalisation” of German identity, arguing that Germany should not be reduced solely to Auschwitz. In practice these gestures remained entirely within the post-1945 guilt framework. Traditional national pride was almost entirely absent; cultural and historical figures continued to be de-nationalised; and public space showed the near-total absence of affirmative traditional national statues.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- Official rhetoric and policy treated the Nazi period as a total moral catastrophe and extended critical scrutiny to large parts of pre-1945 German history (militarism, nationalism, empire).
- Cultural and scientific figures (Beethoven, Goethe, Humboldt, etc.) were celebrated primarily as universal or European individuals rather than as sources of German national pride.
- Post-1945 public space continued the pattern of near-absence of new traditional national statues of longer German history or achievement, with heavy institutional investment instead in atonement and victim memorials.
- Education and public institutions maintained strong emphasis on atonement, responsibility, and the need to move beyond traditional German national identity.
- Any assertion of German interests or identity was routinely balanced with historical guilt.

This period shows only the most limited and heavily qualified attempts at self-confidence. The dominant stance remained one of historical guilt and the systematic dissolution of traditional national identity. The near-total refusal to erect affirmative traditional national statues after 1945 is itself one of the strongest empirical signals of that stance.

**Key Shift**: Compared with the late Kohl years there was a slight rhetorical push toward “normalisation,” but the underlying emotional and institutional stance remained deep inside the Guilt band. The score stays firmly in 10–19.

## **2005–2015: Early Merkel Governments – Managed Remembrance and Constraint – Final Score: 16**

Angela Merkel’s first decade stabilised the post-war culture of remembrance while allowing carefully managed, temporary expressions of national pride (especially the 2006 World Cup). Even these moments remained heavily constrained. Traditional national identity continued to be treated as something to be managed or contained rather than affirmed. The near-absence of new traditional national statues persisted.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The 2006 World Cup was widely described as a rare moment of flag-waving without immediate historical caveats, yet it was still carefully framed as temporary, sporting, and non-nationalistic.
- Education and media continued to prioritise Holocaust remembrance and critical approaches to German history; traditional national pride was treated as potentially dangerous.
- Cultural figures remained de-nationalised; celebrations of German achievement were routinely universalised.
- Post-1945 public space continued to show near-absence of new traditional national statues of longer German history or achievement, combined with heavy institutional investment in atonement and victim memorials.
- Policy and elite discourse emphasised European integration and historical responsibility over any affirmative vision of German nationhood.

This era shows only marginal movement within the Guilt band. Limited pride in the post-1945 Federal Republic was permitted, but traditional German national identity and longer history continued to be systematically de-emphasised. The refusal to build affirmative traditional national statues remained a defining feature.

**Key Shift**: A modest increase in the social acceptability of temporary, post-1945 pride (especially sporting), but the dominant institutional stance remained one of historical guilt and the ongoing dissolution of traditional national identity. The score rises only slightly within 10–19.

## **2015–2021: Late Merkel Era – Polarisation Within Guilt – Final Score: 13**

The late Merkel years, especially after the 2015 refugee crisis, produced growing polarisation. The rise of the AfD forced more open contestation, yet the dominant institutional and elite culture deepened its orientation toward historical guilt and the rejection of traditional national pride. Critical and atonement-focused narratives intensified.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The refugee crisis was frequently framed through the lens of Germany’s historical responsibility and the need to move beyond traditional national identity.
- Mainstream media, education, and cultural institutions continued to treat strong national pride and traditional German identity as suspect or dangerous.
- Cultural and historical figures were still presented in de-nationalised terms; any attempt to claim them as sources of national pride was often criticised.
- Post-1945 public space continued the near-absence of new traditional national statues, with selective colonial-era renamings and critical reinterpretation of remaining older symbols.
- Large-scale institutional efforts continued to emphasise atonement and the need to dissolve older forms of German national identity.

This period represents a slight deepening of the guilt orientation in elite and institutional settings. Traditional national pride remained largely illegitimate in mainstream discourse.

**Key Shift**: Polarisation increased, but the dominant cultural and institutional stance moved further into active de-emphasis and suspicion of traditional national identity. The score drops slightly deeper into the Guilt band.

## **2021–present: Scholz Government and Zeitenwende – Marginal Reassertion Within Guilt – Final Score: 17**

The Scholz government and the *Zeitenwende* following Russia’s invasion of Ukraine produced the most significant (though still limited) reassertion of German self-interest and national capacity since 1998. Even so, this reassertion remains firmly embedded within the post-1945 guilt framework and does not restore pride in traditional German national identity or longer history. The near-absence of affirmative traditional national statues continues.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The *Zeitenwende* rhetoric emphasised Germany’s responsibility and the need for greater military and strategic capacity, but still framed these in terms of historical lessons and European duty rather than national pride.
- Traditional German history and identity continued to be treated with deep caution; pride remained almost exclusively limited to the post-1945 democratic project.
- Cultural figures and achievements were still largely de-nationalised in public discourse.
- Post-1945 public space continues to show near-absence of new traditional national statues of longer German history or achievement, combined with heavy institutional investment in atonement and victim memorials, critical reinterpretation of remaining older symbols, and selective colonial-era renamings.
- Expressions of national pride outside the carefully managed post-1945 frame remained heavily constrained.

This period shows the highest score within the Guilt band for the post-1998 era, but the improvement is marginal. Germany has not escaped the dominant emotional stance of historical guilt and the systematic de-emphasis of traditional national identity.

**Key Shift**: External events forced a limited reassertion of German capacity and self-interest, producing a slight upward movement within the Guilt band. Traditional national pride and historical identity remain largely illegitimate.

# Political Parties

Germany’s major political parties operate almost entirely within the Guilt framework. Only the AfD offers a partial and heavily constrained challenge to the dominant culture of historical guilt and the de-emphasis of traditional national identity. All other major parties either actively reinforce the guilt orientation or accept it as the legitimate baseline. Pride in traditional German history or national identity remains almost entirely absent from mainstream party discourse. Even the AfD’s more affirmative stance is limited by the broader cultural environment and does not reach confident national affirmation. The near-absence of new traditional national statues after 1945 and the institutional prioritisation of atonement continue to define the landscape.

## AfD – Final Score: 52

The AfD is the only major German party that actively challenges the post-1945 culture of historical guilt and the systematic de-emphasis of traditional national identity. It argues for a more positive and less self-critical view of German history. Even so, its position remains heavily constrained by the broader German cultural environment.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The party explicitly criticises the “cult of guilt” and calls for greater pride in German culture and achievements.
- It rejects the framing of traditional German national identity as inherently dangerous or illegitimate.
- Cultural and historical figures are more readily claimed as German rather than universalised.
- Even the AfD operates within a political culture that still treats strong national pride as extreme; its rhetoric is often reactive rather than confidently affirmative.
- It is the only major party that would reverse the near-absence of affirmative traditional national statues if it gained power.

The AfD is the clear outlier. Its score places it at the top of the Balanced band, reflecting a genuine (if constrained) challenge to the Guilt framework. It remains well below the levels of national pride found in equivalent parties elsewhere.

## CDU/CSU – Final Score: 36

The CDU/CSU occupies a cautious middle position that largely accepts the post-1945 guilt framework while seeking limited room for national confidence. Traditional German national identity remains heavily qualified and secondary to historical responsibility.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The party defends Holocaust remembrance and the culture of historical responsibility as non-negotiable.
- It allows limited pride in the post-1945 Federal Republic and reunification, but treats longer German history with significant caution.
- Cultural figures are usually presented in de-nationalised or carefully balanced ways.
- Strong expressions of traditional national identity are routinely distanced from; the party has historically been quick to police its own right flank.
- It accepts and continues the post-1945 pattern of near-absence of new traditional national statues of longer German history or achievement.

The CDU/CSU reinforces rather than challenges the dominant guilt orientation. Its score places it in the Negative band.

## Die Linke – Final Score: 19

Die Linke maintains one of the most consistently critical stances toward traditional German national identity and history. It treats German nationalism and militarism as deeply suspect and prioritises historical guilt and internationalist frameworks.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The party views traditional German national identity as largely illegitimate and associated with aggression and authoritarianism.
- It emphasises atonement, anti-militarism, and the need to move beyond national frameworks.
- Cultural and historical figures are almost never claimed as sources of national pride; when discussed, they are usually universalised or critically contextualised.
- Some East German nostalgia exists, but this is framed as socialist rather than national pride.
- It fully supports the institutional prioritisation of atonement memorials and the absence of traditional national statues.

Die Linke sits at the bottom of the Guilt band. Its stance is one of active de-emphasis and suspicion of traditional German national identity.

## FDP – Final Score: 39

The FDP takes a liberal, relatively detached approach to national identity. It supports a thin form of constitutional patriotism but shows little interest in traditional German history or collective national pride.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The party emphasises individual freedom, European integration, and constitutional values rather than national history or identity.
- Traditional German national pride is neither strongly affirmed nor actively attacked; it is largely ignored or treated as outdated.
- Cultural figures are presented in universal or liberal terms rather than as national symbols.
- The FDP has been critical of both the AfD’s nationalism and the more extreme guilt narratives of the left, but its own position remains thin and de-nationalised.
- It accepts the post-1945 public-space pattern of near-absence of traditional national statues.

The FDP’s score places it in the Negative band. Its approach is one of liberal detachment from traditional national identity.

## Greens – Final Score: 14

The Greens maintain the most consistently critical and de-nationalising stance among the major parties. They treat traditional German national identity as largely illegitimate and prioritise historical atonement, European integration, and post-national frameworks.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The party views strong national pride and traditional German identity with deep suspicion.
- Education and cultural policy positions emphasise expanding historical guilt (including colonialism) and moving beyond national frameworks.
- Cultural and historical figures are routinely de-nationalised and presented as European or universal.
- Rhetoric consistently frames German history through the lens of responsibility, atonement, and the need to dissolve older forms of national identity.
- The Greens actively support the institutional prioritisation of atonement memorials and the continued absence of affirmative traditional national statues.

The Greens sit deep in the Guilt band. Their position is one of active cultural and political effort to move beyond traditional German national identity.

## SPD – Final Score: 27

The SPD occupies a middle-to-low position that accepts the post-1945 guilt framework while occasionally attempting limited expressions of national confidence. Traditional German national identity remains heavily subordinated to historical responsibility.

### Emotional and Cultural Stance – Expanded Analysis

- The party maintains a strong commitment to Holocaust remembrance and historical atonement.
- Pride is almost exclusively limited to the post-1945 democratic and social achievements of the Federal Republic.
- Cultural figures and longer German history are treated with caution and rarely claimed as sources of national pride.
- Rhetoric frequently balances any national assertion with references to historical guilt and European responsibility.
- The party accepts and continues the post-1945 pattern of near-absence of new traditional national statues.

The SPD’s score places it in the upper Shame / lower Guilt range. It reinforces the dominant culture of self-limitation and the de-emphasis of traditional national identity.

Germany’s post-1998 trajectory remains firmly inside the **10–19 (Guilt)** band. The scores (eras 13–17, parties as listed) are justified. The dominant stance is still Guilt.
