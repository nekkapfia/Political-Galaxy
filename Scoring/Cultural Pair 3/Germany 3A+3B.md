## Basic Analysis / Overview of the Timeline

Germany’s post-1998 history on Cultural 3A and 3B shows a clear trajectory from a traditionally descent-based system toward greater civic inclusion, with periodic tightening of cultural expectations in response to large-scale migration.

On **3A (Ethnic Exclusivity)**, Germany scores **0** across the entire period. There is no ethnic or racial requirement for becoming German. While the system has historically been more *jus sanguinis*-oriented than Britain’s, it has never imposed ethnic or blood-based criteria that exclude any group from membership. Naturalisation has remained open to people of all ethnic backgrounds. The 2000 citizenship reform and subsequent changes further reduced any structural role of ancestry without ever introducing ethnic exclusivity.

On **3B (Cultural Exclusivity)**, the picture is more dynamic. Cultural expectations were moderate during the Schröder era, rose noticeably during the Merkel years (particularly after 2015), and were then partially rolled back under the Scholz government. The 2015–2021 period produced the highest 3B scores due to the political reaction to the refugee crisis and the increased emphasis on *Leitkultur* and constitutional values. The 2024 citizenship reform marked a clear liberalising turn, reducing the cultural bar for naturalisation.

Overall, Germany has moved from a more descent-oriented but still ethnically open model toward a more civic one, while cultural expectations for membership have fluctuated in response to demographic pressures rather than following a consistent liberalising or restrictive trend.

### Era 1: 1998–2005 – Schröder Red-Green Citizenship Reform
**Scores: 3A 0 | 3B 30**

### Brief Description of the Era
The 1998 election brought a Red-Green coalition to power. The new government passed a major reform of citizenship law in 1999 (effective 2000), introducing elements of *jus soli* and easing naturalisation. This marked Germany’s most significant move away from its traditional descent-based model.

### Relevant Policies / Elements

| Policy / Element | Relevance & Quick Summary | Slider |
|------------------|---------------------------|--------|
| 2000 Citizenship Reform (StAG) | Added *jus soli* elements and reduced residency requirement from 15 to 8 years | Both |
| Dual citizenship provisions (limited) | Allowed dual citizenship for children born in Germany until age 23 | 3B |
| Language requirement for naturalisation | Basic German knowledge required | 3B |
| Continued *jus sanguinis* core | Descent remained the primary principle alongside new birthright elements | 3A |

### More Detailed Analysis
The 2000 reform represented a deliberate shift toward a more civic conception of German membership. By introducing *jus soli* for children of long-term residents, the government moved away from the purely descent-based model that had defined German citizenship law for most of the 20th century. Naturalisation was also made more accessible by reducing the residency requirement.

No ethnic criteria were introduced or maintained. The reform was open to people of all backgrounds. Cultural expectations remained moderate and were largely limited to basic language knowledge for naturalisation. There was no strong political push for deep cultural transformation or *Leitkultur* at this stage. Ethnicity played no role in determining eligibility for membership.

### 3B Summary (Cultural Exclusivity)
**Score: 30**  
Cultural requirements were present but limited. Basic language knowledge was expected for naturalisation, but there were no strong demands for cultural transformation or alignment with a defined *Leitkultur*.

### Era 2: 2005–2015 – Merkel Pre-Refugee Integration Consolidation
**Scores: 3A 0 | 3B 38**

### Brief Description of the Era
Angela Merkel’s early governments focused on managing integration after the 2000 reforms. The *Leitkultur* debate gained prominence, and integration requirements were gradually strengthened without major changes to the underlying citizenship framework.

### Relevant Policies / Elements

| Policy / Element | Relevance & Quick Summary | Slider |
|------------------|---------------------------|--------|
| *Leitkultur* debate | Growing political emphasis on German leading culture and values | 3B |
| Integration courses and tests | Mandatory language and civics requirements for many immigrants | 3B |
| Naturalisation requirements | Language and integration expectations maintained | 3B |
| No ethnic criteria | Membership remained open to all ethnic groups | 3A |

### More Detailed Analysis
This period saw increasing political concern about integration, particularly regarding Muslim communities. The concept of *Leitkultur* became more prominent in conservative discourse, framing successful membership as requiring alignment with German values and culture. Integration courses were expanded and made more central to the immigration process.

Despite these developments, no ethnic barriers were introduced. Naturalisation remained open to people of any background. The rise in 3B reflects a growing expectation that cultural and values-based integration should be demonstrated, particularly for those seeking long-term membership. Ethnicity continued to play no role in eligibility for German citizenship.

### 3B Summary (Cultural Exclusivity)
**Score: 38**  
Cultural expectations increased modestly. The *Leitkultur* debate and strengthened integration requirements reflected a growing view that cultural alignment was relevant to successful membership.

### Era 3: 2015–2021 – Merkel Refugee Crisis and Cultural Pushback
**Scores: 3A 0 | 3B 52**

### Brief Description of the Era
The 2015–2016 refugee crisis brought over one million arrivals, mostly from culturally distant regions. This triggered a significant political reaction and a noticeable strengthening of cultural expectations for membership.

### Relevant Policies / Elements

| Policy / Element | Relevance & Quick Summary | Slider |
|------------------|---------------------------|--------|
| 2015–2016 refugee intake | Largest post-war migration wave | 3B (trigger) |
| Strengthened integration requirements | Greater emphasis on language, values, and *Leitkultur* | 3B |
| Naturalisation and settlement rules | Cultural and integration tests maintained or tightened | 3B |
| Political discourse on integration | Growing focus on cultural compatibility and parallel societies | 3B |

### More Detailed Analysis
The scale of the 2015 refugee influx exposed limitations in Germany’s existing integration model and led to a clear rise in cultural expectations. Political discourse increasingly emphasised that successful long-term membership required alignment with German *Leitkultur* and constitutional values. Integration requirements were strengthened in practice, even if major legislative changes were limited.

No ethnic criteria were introduced at any point. The reaction to the crisis was framed in cultural and values-based terms rather than ethnic ones. Ethnicity played no role in determining who could become German. This period produced the highest 3B score in the timeline due to the intensity of the cultural integration debate.

### 3B Summary (Cultural Exclusivity)
**Score: 52**  
This was the period of strongest cultural expectations. The political response to mass migration from culturally distant backgrounds led to heightened demands for cultural alignment as a condition of successful membership.

### Era 4: 2021–Present – Scholz Liberalisation Era
**Scores: 3A 0 | 3B 28**

### Brief Description of the Era
The 2021–2025 traffic-light coalition implemented the most significant liberalisation of German citizenship law since 2000, including dual citizenship for all and reduced residency requirements. Some tightening occurred after 2025.

### Relevant Policies / Elements

| Policy / Element | Relevance & Quick Summary | Slider |
|------------------|---------------------------|--------|
| 2024 Citizenship Reform | Dual citizenship allowed for all, residency reduced to 5 years | 3B |
| Removal of Optionsmodell | Most children born in Germany no longer required to choose nationality | 3B |
| Stricter exclusion criteria | Reinforced rules against extremism and antisemitism | 3B |
| Repeal of 3-year fast-track (2025) | Some re-tightening of naturalisation rules | 3B |

### More Detailed Analysis
The 2024 reform represented a clear liberalising shift. By allowing unrestricted dual citizenship and reducing the residency requirement, the government lowered both practical and cultural barriers to membership. The reform was explicitly presented as recognising Germany as an immigration country.

While some tightening occurred in 2025, the overall direction remained more open than during the 2015–2021 period. Cultural expectations were not eliminated but were made less burdensome. No ethnic criteria were present at any point. Ethnicity played no role in determining eligibility for German citizenship.

### 3B Summary (Cultural Exclusivity)
**Score: 28**  
Cultural requirements were eased compared with the previous period. The liberalisation of dual citizenship and residency rules reduced the cultural bar for naturalisation, though language and values commitments remained.

# Political Parties

Modern Germany’s political parties show near-complete consensus on the ethnic dimension of membership (Cultural 3A), with all major parties scoring **0**. No party proposes ethnic, ancestral, or blood-based criteria for becoming German. Differentiation occurs almost entirely on the cultural dimension (Cultural 3B) — specifically, how much cultural alignment, language proficiency, values commitment, and integration should be required for full membership of the core in-group.

The AfD takes the strongest position that cultural integration must be meaningful and that large-scale culturally distant migration poses risks to social cohesion. The CDU/CSU maintains a moderate but clear expectation of cultural alignment through its emphasis on *Leitkultur*. The FDP and SPD occupy more liberal positions, supporting easier naturalisation while retaining basic integration requirements. The Greens advocate the most open approach, treating cultural requirements as minimal and often framing stronger expectations as exclusionary.

## AfD – Final Score: 3A 0 | 3B 60

The AfD maintains complete ethnic openness in formal policy while advocating the strongest cultural expectations for membership among major parties.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

* Calls for significantly stricter integration requirements, including deeper commitment to German *Leitkultur* and values.
* Opposes the 2024 liberalisation of citizenship law and advocates reversing many of its provisions.
* Frames large-scale culturally distant migration as a threat to social cohesion and national identity.
* Supports making naturalisation more difficult and contingent on successful cultural integration.
* Explicitly rejects ethnic criteria for membership while strongly emphasising cultural ones.

The AfD combines absolute rejection of ethnic criteria with the clearest argument that cultural alignment should be a central condition of Endos status. It views the current system as too permissive and argues that German membership should require meaningful adoption of German culture and values, particularly for those from culturally distant backgrounds. This produces the highest 3B score among the parties analysed.

---

## CDU/CSU – Final Score: 3A 0 | 3B 42

The CDU/CSU maintains complete ethnic openness while advocating moderate but consistent cultural expectations for membership.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

* Strong support for the concept of *Leitkultur* as a guiding framework for integration and naturalisation.
* Defence of language requirements, citizenship tests, and values commitments as conditions for becoming German.
* Opposition to overly rapid or low-threshold naturalisation, arguing that citizenship should reflect successful integration.
* Criticism of the 2024 citizenship reforms as weakening the link between membership and cultural alignment.
* Continued emphasis that Germany is an immigration country, but one that requires newcomers to adopt core German values and norms.

The CDU/CSU treats legal status as the gateway to membership but insists that cultural integration should be a meaningful part of that process, particularly for those entering through naturalisation. It rejects ethnic criteria entirely while maintaining that shared cultural values and language proficiency are legitimate expectations for full Endos status. This produces a moderate 3B score that reflects support for the existing framework with resistance to further liberalisation.

---

## FDP – Final Score: 3A 0 | 3B 18

The FDP maintains absolute ethnic openness and favours relatively low cultural expectations for membership.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

* Supported the 2024 liberalisation of citizenship law, including dual citizenship.
* Generally prefers streamlined and less burdensome requirements for naturalisation.
* Emphasises economic contribution and individual merit over strong cultural integration tests.
* Shows limited enthusiasm for *Leitkultur* framing or expanded values-based requirements.
* Focuses more on attracting skilled workers than on cultural transformation.

The FDP takes a pragmatic, economically liberal approach to membership. It supports relatively easy access to citizenship for those who can contribute economically, while accepting basic language and legal requirements. Cultural expectations are kept low and functional rather than transformative. This produces a low-to-moderate 3B score.

---

## Greens – Final Score: 3A 0 | 3B 8

The Greens maintain absolute ethnic openness and take the most minimal position on cultural requirements for membership.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

* Strong support for the 2024 citizenship liberalisation, including dual citizenship and reduced residency requirements.
* Opposition to strengthening cultural or integration tests, viewing them as potential barriers.
* Emphasis on rights-based and inclusive approaches to membership with minimal cultural hurdles.
* Framing of higher cultural expectations as potentially discriminatory or exclusionary.
* Consistent advocacy for easier access to citizenship for long-term residents and refugees.

The Greens treat both ethnicity and cultural alignment as largely irrelevant to full membership. They argue that legal residence and basic compliance with the law should be sufficient for Endos status, with cultural identity remaining a private matter. This produces one of the lowest 3B scores among major parties and reflects a strong preference for minimal barriers on both the ethnic and cultural dimensions.

---

## SPD – Final Score: 3A 0 | 3B 25

The SPD maintains complete ethnic openness while supporting light but formal cultural expectations for naturalisation.

### Allocation Mechanisms – Expanded Analysis

* Supported the 2024 reforms that eased naturalisation and introduced dual citizenship.
* Accepts the continued existence of language requirements and the citizenship test.
* Emphasises that citizenship should be accessible to long-term residents who contribute to society.
* Avoids strong *Leitkultur* rhetoric, preferring more inclusive framings of German identity.
* Generally resistant to significantly raising the cultural bar for membership.

The SPD operates within the post-2000 framework that includes modest cultural requirements for naturalisation while keeping birth and descent routes culturally unrestricted. It has shown support for further liberalisation in recent years. This produces a moderate 3B score that reflects acceptance of basic integration expectations without enthusiasm for stronger cultural exclusivity.
