Over the eight decades from 1945 to the present, Britain moved from a high-Orthodoxy post-war consensus era of strong internal discipline and national reconstruction toward greater pluralism on social and moral questions, while identity and sovereignty issues produced recurring waves of Tribalism. The overall pattern shows Orthodoxy peaking during periods of strong governing consensus or institutional embedding of new principles, then gradually softening as economic crises, cultural liberalization, and internal party fractures increased. Tribalism has been more variable, rising sharply during identity shocks and during assertive promotion or defence of particular national or progressive frameworks, resulting in a system that has rarely been extremely dogmatic but has frequently sat in the Moderate-Strong to Predominant range with notable bidirectional contestation in recent decades.


---

## **1945-1951: Post-War Reconstruction – Final Score: 70**

The immediate post-war years were defined by exceptionally strong internal closure around the new social-democratic reconstruction project, with high party discipline and broad acceptance of the welfare state, nationalizations, and managed economy as foundational principles.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Very high party discipline inherited from wartime governance, with minimal backbench rebellion against core reconstruction policies.
- Strong cross-party consensus limited major internal challenges to the welfare state and full-employment framework.
- National identity remained tied to victory, Empire/Commonwealth, and reconstruction, with limited early contestation.
- Alternatives such as extreme fascism or radical communism faced clear institutional rejection in the early Cold War context.

The 1945 Labour landslide and the scale of reconstruction needs produced unusually tight Orthodoxy. Mechanisms of party discipline and elite consensus effectively closed off major revision of the new social settlement. Tribalism remained moderate, centred on a cohesive post-victory British identity that still encompassed the Commonwealth while beginning to manage early immigration flows. The result was a system with very high internal protection of the reconstruction project and only moderate external rejection of alternatives.


---

## **1951-1959: Butskellite Consensus – Final Score: 70**

The 1950s saw the continuation of high Orthodoxy through cross-party policy continuity (“Butskellism”), even as economic recovery and decolonization introduced new pressures.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Exceptional party discipline continued, with almost no government defeats from backbench dissent.
- Cross-party agreement on welfare, full employment, and managed economy reduced the need for coercive enforcement.
- Suez Crisis (1956) created the first major shock to imperial British identity.
- Rising Commonwealth immigration began generating social tensions while policy remained largely assimilationist.

Orthodoxy stayed high because the core post-war settlement was not seriously contested by either major party. Tribalism began a gradual rise as decolonization and early immigration challenged the imperial dimension of British identity, though mainstream rejection of overt racism coexisted with growing public anxiety. The era retained the high internal cohesion of the immediate post-war years while identity questions started to surface more clearly.


---

## **1959-1968: Social Liberalization & Identity Tensions – Final Score: 68**

This period saw noticeable decline in Orthodoxy on social and moral questions alongside rising contestation over national identity and immigration.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Cultural shifts and scandals began eroding deference to traditional authority.
- Social reforms challenged older moral orthodoxies.
- Race Relations legislation introduced institutional pluralism on race.
- Enoch Powell’s 1968 speech sharply increased Tribalism around immigration and British identity.

Orthodoxy softened as cultural liberalization and political scandals reduced the previous level of internal closure on moral and social issues. Tribalism rose as immigration and identity became more contested. The era shows the first sustained movement away from the high-consensus character of the earlier post-war decades.


---

## **1968-1979: Powell Shock to Winter of Discontent – Final Score: 66**

The 1970s were marked by declining Orthodoxy across multiple domains and persistently contested Tribalism on both identity and class/economic issues.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Industrial conflict and union power repeatedly challenged government authority.
- Economic crises and governance failures weakened traditional party discipline.
- Immigration and race remained sources of polarization.
- Repeated changes of government demonstrated eroding state capacity.

Orthodoxy continued to fall as economic and industrial crises made sustained internal closure around any single framework difficult. Tribalism remained moderate-to-high, driven by ongoing identity tensions alongside dominant class-based conflicts. The period shows the clearest breakdown of the post-war high-Orthodoxy model.


---

## **1979-1990: Thatcher Ascendancy & High Thatcherism – Final Score: 73**

The Thatcher years re-established higher Orthodoxy around a new set of market-oriented and individualist principles while maintaining strong Tribalism against the previous consensus.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Strong leadership and party discipline enforced privatization, union reform, and monetarism.
- Robust rejection of socialist and collectivist alternatives.
- National revival and patriotism were actively promoted.
- Some conservative social pushback occurred on specific issues.

Orthodoxy rose on the new Thatcherite framework through determined leadership and institutional change. Tribalism was elevated in the explicit rejection of the post-war settlement and the promotion of a more individualistic and patriotic national narrative. The era represents a clear reversal of the declining Orthodoxy trend of the 1970s.


---

## **1990-1997: Major Government & Fractures – Final Score: 69**

The Major period saw Orthodoxy soften again as internal party divisions and scandals reduced governing cohesion.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Growing backbench rebellions over European integration.
- Scandals eroded public and internal authority.
- Continued emphasis on national identity and immigration controls.
- Institutional multiculturalism persisted alongside rising contestation.

Orthodoxy declined from the Thatcher peak as internal fractures made sustained closure harder. Tribalism remained moderate-to-high on sovereignty and identity questions. The era marks a transitional phase between the assertive Thatcher realignment and the new progressive consensus that followed.

---

## **1997-2010: New Labour Progressive Era – Final Score: 79**

This was the period of highest institutional Orthodoxy and Tribalism around a progressive multicultural and human-rights framework.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Strong central party control and institutional embedding of diversity and human rights.
- Devolution and the Human Rights Act created new structural protections.
- Critics of high immigration or traditional national identity were frequently marginalized.
- Multiculturalism was actively promoted as a core national value.

Orthodoxy was high through centralized control and the embedding of progressive principles across state institutions. Tribalism was also high as alternatives emphasizing ethnic cohesion or stricter immigration were often treated as illegitimate in mainstream discourse. The era represents the clearest peak of institutional dogmatism in the post-war period.

---

## **2010-2016: Austerity & Rising Contestation – Final Score: 76**

Orthodoxy softened somewhat under coalition government and growing challenges, while Tribalism remained high amid rising polarization.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Coalition dynamics and austerity created governance tensions.
- Scottish independence referendum highlighted sovereignty issues.
- EU membership and immigration became increasingly contested.
- Institutional mechanisms continued protecting diversity frameworks.

Orthodoxy declined from the New Labour peak as coalition politics and public contestation made internal closure more difficult. Tribalism stayed high on both progressive institutional lines and emerging nationalist challenges. The era shows the beginning of the fragmentation that intensified after 2016.

---

## **2016-2019: Brexit Shock & May Negotiations – Final Score: 76**

The immediate post-referendum years featured high bidirectional Tribalism and contested Orthodoxy as the political system adjusted to the Brexit decision.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Major internal Conservative divisions over Brexit terms.
- Strong institutional defence of previous diversity and human-rights frameworks.
- Sharp polarization between nationalist and progressive visions of British identity.
- Immigration and sovereignty became central contested issues.

Orthodoxy was weakened by repeated internal rebellions, while Tribalism rose sharply as competing moral frameworks around national identity and sovereignty directly confronted each other. The era marks the clearest popular challenge to the institutional consensus of the previous two decades.

---

## 2019-present: Johnson Delivery, COVID, Instability & Starmer – Final Score: 76

The most recent period shows continued high bidirectional Tribalism alongside moderate Orthodoxy that varies with changes in government but remains institutionally protected on progressive identity issues.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis
- Brexit delivery and COVID governance temporarily strengthened central authority.
- Successive leadership changes and scandals reduced long-term cohesion.
- Progressive institutional mechanisms continued defending diversity and gender frameworks.
- Populist and nationalist challenges maintained strong external contestation.

Orthodoxy has been inconsistent at the political level but remains relatively robust in state institutions. Tribalism has stayed high and explicitly two-sided, with progressive frameworks continuing to reject nationalist and gender-critical alternatives while those alternatives equally reject progressive orthodoxy on immigration and cultural change. The era reflects a more fragmented but still predominantly dogmatic system.

# Political Parties

Modern Britain exhibits moderate to strong dogmatism in its institutional and cultural spheres. Progressive identity and equity frameworks are strongly protected in education, public sector policy, and media, often with mechanisms that penalise dissent (e.g., hate speech expansions, disciplinary actions in institutions). However, the multiparty democratic system and the rise of populist challengers (Reform and Restore) provide avenues for internal challenge and external alternatives, preventing total closure at the national level. The overall state is transitional to predominant dogmatism, with high-dogmatism pockets in dominant institutions offset by political pluralism and pragmatic adaptations.

## Conservative Party – Final Score: 49

The Conservative Party maintains a relatively open approach to its foundational principles, permitting internal debate, factional differences, and pragmatic revision on cultural and policy questions while engaging critically with external moral frameworks on empirical grounds rather than rejecting them as illegitimate.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis

- Historically broad internal structure tolerating significant factional pluralism and regular leadership challenges.
- Recent leadership has driven shifts toward biological sex realism in schools, sports, and single-sex spaces without systematic purges of previous positions.
- External progressive frameworks on expansive gender self-identification and equity-over-merit are critiqued on data and outcomes rather than dismissed as morally invalid.
- Institutional discipline such as the whip exists but has not been deployed to suppress internal discussion or revision of core cultural positions.

The Conservative Party has retained a longstanding organisational culture that accommodates internal contestation and leadership turnover. This has allowed observable pragmatic adjustments, particularly on questions of biological sex in policy and the costs of net zero commitments, without the need for large-scale disciplinary action against members holding differing views. The party’s broad church tradition continues to support meaningful space for debate across ideological wings, with mechanisms like leadership contests providing outlets for revision rather than suppression. 

Externally, the party tends to engage opposing frameworks through evidence-based critique rather than outright delegitimisation, treating alternatives on merit, national cohesion, and biological reality as legitimate points of debate even when it disagrees with them. While standard party discipline operates on parliamentary matters, it has not extended to the comprehensive closure seen elsewhere. This pattern of relative internal openness combined with substantive rather than total rejection of external frameworks produces the Final Score of 49.

## Green Party – Final Score: 89

The Green Party applies strong institutional protection to its foundational principles on gender self-identification, expansive trans inclusion, and radical environmental positions, using disciplinary processes to limit internal challenge and treating alternative frameworks grounded in biological sex and material reality as illegitimate.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis

- Repeated use of disciplinary panels resulting in suspensions and expulsions of members, including former spokespeople and women’s group co-chairs, for questioning self-ID or raising biological sex conflicts.
- Severed official ties with internal women’s organisations and restricted debate at conference over these questions.
- Core principles on identity and environmental emergency presented as settled with limited allowance for critical examination.
- External frameworks prioritising biological sex, child safeguarding evidence, or incremental approaches framed as harmful or invalid within party structures.

The Green Party has developed consistent governance mechanisms that actively penalise internal deviation on its core identity and environmental commitments. Disciplinary processes have been applied across multiple cases to remove or sideline members who challenge self-identification policies or highlight tensions with sex-based rights and review evidence. This has been reinforced by the severance of links with internal women’s groups and the narrowing of official debate spaces, creating a high threshold for any substantive internal revision. 

Externally, frameworks grounded in biological materiality or more cautious policy approaches receive little institutional legitimacy and are routinely positioned as incompatible with the party’s foundational positions. The combination of firm internal enforcement and strong external rejection creates a tightly closed system on these issues, with minimal room for revision or engagement. This pattern of robust institutional protection against internal challenge paired with comprehensive dismissal of external alternatives produces the Final Score of 89.

## Labour Party – Final Score: 70

The Labour Party maintains strong internal disciplinary mechanisms to protect its leadership’s direction while showing selective pragmatic adaptation on certain cultural questions, alongside a general rejection of external frameworks that emphasise national sovereignty, strict immigration control, or individual merit over systemic explanations.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis

- Extensive use of candidate deselection, NEC control, and repeated whip removals or suspensions targeting left-wing and rebel members over economic and foreign policy direction.
- Some capacity for revision demonstrated on gender self-identification, shifting toward biological sex definitions and single-sex spaces following legal and public evidence.
- External perspectives on stricter national sovereignty and controlled immigration frequently marginalised or rejected as regressive.
- Broader equity and diversity frameworks remain protected, with alternatives focused on individual agency receiving limited institutional space.

Labour has relied on centralised control mechanisms, including candidate selection processes and parliamentary discipline, to limit internal challenge to the leadership’s chosen direction, particularly on economic and foreign policy matters. At the same time, it has shown a degree of pragmatic adjustment on gender self-identification, moving away from earlier self-ID commitments in response to evidence and legal clarification. This selective flexibility demonstrates some responsiveness but does not extend to core economic or broader progressive commitments. 

Externally, frameworks emphasising national sovereignty, stricter immigration controls, or critiques of systemic explanations for group outcomes are generally treated with hostility or marginalisation rather than substantive engagement. The overall effect is a system with firm internal boundaries on many issues but with pockets of adaptation where external pressures have been strong. This mix of strong disciplinary protection, selective revision, and broader rejection of certain external perspectives produces the Final Score of 70.

## Liberal Democrats – Final Score: 80

The Liberal Democrats apply consistent institutional and procedural pressure to protect progressive social principles, particularly around expansive LGBT+ inclusion and identity frameworks, while limiting internal debate and treating alternative perspectives rooted in biological sex or traditional liberal individualism as illegitimate.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis

- Conference and candidate selection processes used to block debate on trans women in gender-based diversity quotas and to disadvantage gender-critical candidates.
- Support for expanded hate crime legislation that creates institutional pathways potentially penalising challenge to core identity positions.
- External frameworks emphasising biological sex as binary or national sovereignty over supranational integration routinely dismissed as beyond legitimate debate.
- Long-standing progressive social and EU integration positions have historically received strong protection from internal revision.

The Liberal Democrats have used party conference procedures and candidate processes to constrain internal discussion on contested identity questions, particularly where challenges arise to expansive trans inclusion in quotas or single-sex spaces. This has been accompanied by policy support for measures such as misogyny as a hate crime, which widen the scope for institutional responses to dissent. The party’s structures consistently favour protection of progressive social commitments over open revision. 

Externally, perspectives grounded in biological sex realism or stronger national sovereignty receive limited legitimacy and are often characterised as incompatible with the party’s foundational commitments. The result is a system in which progressive social principles are actively shielded from internal revision through procedural and policy mechanisms, while alternative moral frameworks face consistent delegitimisation. This pattern of sustained institutional closure internally and rejection externally produces the Final Score of 80.

## Reform UK – Final Score: 81

Reform UK maintains firm protection for its core principles of strict immigration control, national sovereignty, biological sex realism, and opposition to institutional “woke” capture, enforcing these through leadership direction and explicit policy commitments while strongly rejecting external progressive frameworks as harmful.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis

- Leader-centric structure enforcing non-negotiable positions on border control, removal of transgender ideology from schools, and replacement of the Equality Act and DEI rules.
- Explicit policy commitments framing progressive identity frameworks and open-borders approaches as causing harm to children, women, and cohesion.
- Internal friction on governance and strategy has faced constraints, though aligned defectors have been welcomed.
- Education and institutional reform proposals positioned as necessary reversals of captured systems, with limited space for competing frameworks.

Reform UK has built its organisational model around clear, publicly stated red lines on immigration, biological sex in policy, and resistance to institutional identity politics. These commitments are enforced through leadership authority and detailed manifesto pledges that leave little ambiguity about acceptable positions. Internally, challenges to the overall direction or leadership style have encountered resistance, even as the party has absorbed defectors who broadly align with its platform. 

Externally, progressive frameworks on gender, diversity, and migration are presented not as legitimate alternatives but as actively damaging, justifying explicit policy reversals such as banning social transitioning in schools and scrapping the Equality Act. The combination of firm internal enforcement of core principles and strong external rejection of opposing moral systems produces the Final Score of 81.

## Restore Britain – Final Score: 90

Restore Britain applies tight protection to its foundational principles of national self-preservation, mass deportation of foreign offenders, demographic and cultural continuity, and rejection of woke ideology and radical Islam, structuring the party to minimise internal revision while treating external frameworks as comprehensively failed and illegitimate.

### Mechanisms of Protection and Rejection – Expanded Analysis

- Centralised structure under its founder with explicit “without apology” commitments to large-scale deportations and demographic reversal.
- Strong institutional limits on deviation from core national preservation principles.
- Frames mass immigration, woke ideology, and institutional multiculturalism as having hollowed out the country and requiring decisive reversal.
- Rejects external frameworks that accept high immigration, expansive identity politics, or supranational constraints as threats to the continuity of the British people.
- Grassroots and umbrella elements aligned to ideological clarity, with rapid development prioritising enforcement of non-negotiable positions on identity, borders, and cultural restoration.

Restore Britain has constructed a tightly controlled organisational model in which the leader’s authority and the party’s explicit foundational commitments function as non-negotiable boundaries. The combination of central ownership of the party structure and the deliberate framing of its mission as an urgent corrective to establishment failure leaves little room for internal factions or gradualist compromises. Positions on mass deportation, the reversal of demographic change, and the defence of Britain as a specific historic and demographic entity are presented not as policy options but as essential requirements for national survival. This creates a high threshold for any internal challenge: deviation is structurally difficult because the party’s identity and growth strategy are built around uncompromising clarity on these issues.

Externally, the party extends almost no legitimacy to competing moral frameworks. Approaches that tolerate sustained high levels of immigration, treat national identity as purely civic and open, or accept supranational limits on sovereignty are characterised as actively destructive to the British people’s continuity. There is no meaningful engagement with gradualist or accommodationist alternatives; these are positioned as part of the problem that Restore Britain exists to reverse. The result is a closed system in which both internal revision and external dialogue are heavily constrained by design. This pattern of strong institutional protection against internal challenge, paired with comprehensive rejection of external moral frameworks, produces the Final Score of 90.
