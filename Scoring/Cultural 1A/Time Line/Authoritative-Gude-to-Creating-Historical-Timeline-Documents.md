# Guide to Creating a Historical Timeline Document for Any Country

This document provides a standardised method for producing a historical timeline analysis of a country’s political and moral justificatory patterns from a defined starting point to the present (or to a defined end point). The structure is deliberately generic so that it can be applied to any analytical axis or framework. Elements that are specific to the Cultural 1A axis are clearly marked **(Cultural 1A)**.

**This guide is binding on any instance of the model that produces or revises a timeline document.** Depth and analytical completeness take absolute priority over brevity, token-saving, or artificial compression. Producing thin, summary-level text when full analysis is required is a failure to follow this guide.

---

## 0. Overriding Instruction on Depth

Before any other rule, the following applies:

- **Proper depth is mandatory.** Every section that requires analysis must contain genuine analytical content, not placeholder or compressed text.
- **Artificial brevity is forbidden.** Do not shorten paragraphs, reduce bullet counts, or omit required elements in order to keep the overall document short.
- **Compression that forces later rework is a net waste.** Writing a thin version first and then expanding it later consumes more total resources than writing to the required depth on the first pass.
- **If a choice exists between a shorter response and a complete one, always choose completeness.**
- **Summary paragraphs must be real paragraphs** (normally 70–120 words). Two-sentence “summaries” are non-compliant.
- **Bullet lists must contain concrete justificatory language**, not abstract category labels alone.
- When in doubt about length, err on the side of greater depth and specificity.

Any future instance that produces under-depth text after reading this guide is violating an explicit instruction.

---

## 1. Purpose of the Document

The timeline document exists to:

- Divide a country’s modern political history into analytically coherent eras.
- Identify the dominant sources of moral and political justification in each era.
- Track continuity and change in those sources over time.
- Allow systematic comparison across countries and across historical regimes.

The document is not a general political history. It focuses specifically on *how authority and moral legitimacy were justified* in each period.

---

## 2. Required Overall Structure

Every completed timeline document must contain the following elements in order:

1. **Title**
2. **Introductory Overview** (1–3 full paragraphs)
3. **Converted Final Scores Table**
4. **Individual Era Sections** (one for each defined era)
5. (Optional) Brief concluding note on long-term trajectory

Omitting any required element, or reducing it below the depth standards set out below, renders the document incomplete.

---

## 3. Title Format

```
# [Axis Name]: Eras of [Focus] in [Country] ([Start Year]–[End Year or Present])
```

**Examples:**
- `# Cultural 1A: Eras of Moral Justification in the United States (1945–Present)`
- `# Cultural 1A: Eras of Moral Justification in Nazi Germany (1933–1945)`

**(Cultural 1A)** The focus phrase is normally “Moral Justification”.

---

## 4. Introductory Overview – Depth Requirements

Write **1–3 full paragraphs** that:

- Summarise the country’s overall trajectory on the relevant axis across the whole period.
- Identify the major long-term pattern (e.g., movement from one type of justification toward another).
- Note any extreme peaks, collapses, or persistent dual structures.
- Avoid detailed era-by-era description (that belongs in the individual sections).

**Depth standard:** The overview must be substantial enough to give a reader who knows nothing about the country a clear sense of the long-term justificatory arc. One thin paragraph is normally insufficient for complex countries.

**(Cultural 1A)** The overview must characterise the balance between *transcendent* sources (higher law, ideology as truth, religious duty, historical destiny, natural rights treated as independent of consent, ecological or intergenerational duties, etc.) and *mundane* sources (popular sovereignty, concrete interests of living citizens, evidence, performance, pragmatism, reciprocity).

---

## 5. Converted Final Scores Table

Immediately after the overview, insert a markdown table:

```markdown
## Converted Final Scores

| Era | Period          | Final Score | Source of Moral Authority |
|-----|-----------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Era 1 | YYYY–YYYY       | XX          | Short characterisation of dominant sources. |
| Era 2 | YYYY–YYYY       | XX          | Short characterisation of dominant sources. |
| ... | ...             | ...         | ... |
```

Rules for the table:

- Every era that appears later in the document must appear in the table.
- The “Source of Moral Authority” column should be a concise (one-line) summary, not a full analysis.
- Scores must be consistent with the detailed justifications given in the era sections.

**(Cultural 1A)** Scores run from 0 (purely mundane) to 100 (purely transcendent). Typical bands:
- 0–20: Extremely strong mundane dominance
- 21–40: Clear mundane lean
- 41–60: Mixed
- 61–80: Clear transcendent lean
- 81–100: Extremely strong transcendent dominance

---

## 6. Defining Eras

### 6.1 Principles for Periodisation

Eras should be defined by **shifts in justificatory sources**, not merely by changes of government, election results, or external events.

An era boundary is justified when there is a durable and observable change in:

- Which sources of moral authority are treated as primary.
- How competing sources are ranked or subordinated.
- The intensity with which a dominant source is asserted.

Short-term crises that do not produce lasting justificatory change should not create new eras.

### 6.2 Number and Length of Eras

- Most modern countries from 1945 (or 1917, 1922, 1949, etc.) to the present will have between 5 and 9 eras.
- Very short or highly intense regimes (e.g., Khmer Rouge) may have only 1–2 eras.
- Long regimes with internal evolution (e.g., USSR, Fascist Italy, Islamic Republic of Iran) should be broken into multiple analytically distinct phases.

### 6.3 Naming Eras

Era titles should be descriptive and neutral, combining a chronological label with a short characterising phrase:

```
## **YYYY–YYYY: [Descriptive Title] – Final Score: XX**
```

---

## 7. Structure of Each Individual Era Section – Depth Requirements

Every era section must contain the following subsections in order. **Each has a non-negotiable minimum depth.**

### 7.1 Opening Description (2–4 substantive sentences)

State what the period was and what its dominant political project or self-understanding was. Do not yet analyse justificatory sources in detail.

**Depth standard:** Must give a clear orientation. One sentence is almost always insufficient.

### 7.2 Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

Use a level-3 heading:

```markdown
### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis
```

Provide **4–7 bullet points** that identify the concrete justificatory language and logic actually used in the period. Each bullet must:

- Name a specific source or type of claim.
- Indicate how it was used (primary, secondary, instrumental, rejected, etc.).
- Stay close to the regime’s or polity’s own language where possible.
- Avoid pure abstraction (“justice was important”) in favour of concrete characterisation.

**Depth standard:** Four thin or generic bullets are non-compliant. The bullets must collectively give a reader a precise picture of how authority was actually justified.

**(Cultural 1A)** Explicitly distinguish transcendent sources from mundane sources. Note which were primary, which were secondary, and which were marginal or rejected.

### 7.3 Summary Paragraph – Critical Depth Requirement

After the bullets, write **one coherent paragraph of normally 70–120 words** that:

- Synthesises the justificatory pattern of the era.
- Explains how the dominant sources shaped the polity’s goals and self-presentation.
- Draws the bullets together into an analytical whole.

**This paragraph is mandatory and is the most frequently under-delivered element.**

**Hard rules:**
- Two-sentence “paragraphs” are non-compliant.
- The paragraph must do real analytical work; it is not a restatement of the bullets in prose form.
- If the paragraph is shorter than approximately 70 words or fails to synthesise, it must be rewritten before the document is considered finished.

### 7.4 Key Shift

One or two sentences, introduced by the bold label:

```markdown
**Key Shift:** 
```

State the most important change in justificatory sources that defines the beginning of this era relative to the previous one (or that defines the era as a whole if it is the first).

### 7.5 Why This Produces a Final Score of XX

Use a level-3 heading:

```markdown
### Why this produces a final score of XX
```

Write **2–4 substantive sentences** that directly link the score to the relative weight of the sources analysed above. The justification must be consistent with the scoring bands and with the evidence presented in the bullets and summary paragraph.

**Depth standard:** A single vague sentence is non-compliant. The justification must make the scoring decision transparent and defensible.

**(Cultural 1A)** The justification must explicitly address the degree of dominance, subordination, or absence of transcendent versus mundane sources.

---

## 8. Scoring Discipline **(Cultural 1A)**

- Scores are not measures of moral goodness, success, or desirability. They measure only the relative weight of transcendent versus mundane sources of justification.
- Extreme scores (below 15 or above 85) require clear evidence of near-total dominance of one type of source.
- Mixed scores (roughly 40–60) require evidence that both types of source were active and neither fully subordinated the other.
- Continuity of formal ideology does not automatically produce a high transcendent score if, in practice, performance, stability, or popular consent have become the real organising justifications.
- Conversely, the presence of elections or pragmatic policy does not automatically produce a low score if higher-law, ideological, or religious claims remain primary and can override popular will.

---

## 9. Language and Analytical Standards

- Stay as close as possible to the justificatory language actually used by the regime, party, or polity under analysis.
- Avoid anachronistic projection of later categories.
- Distinguish official doctrine from practical operative justification when they diverge.
- Treat self-presentation seriously: how a polity describes what is broken and what it is restoring or achieving is evidence of its justificatory sources.
- Maintain neutrality of tone. The task is analytical description of sources of authority, not moral evaluation of regimes.

**Depth implication:** Generic or boilerplate language is a failure. Each era’s justificatory profile must be rendered with enough concrete texture that a reader can distinguish it from neighbouring eras.

---

## 10. Handling Special Cases

### 10.1 Single-Party or Highly Centralised Systems
Focus on the ruling party or leadership’s justificatory framework. Note subordinate parties or factions only if they possess distinct and consequential justificatory logics.

### 10.2 Short, Intense Regimes
It is acceptable to have only one or two eras. Do not artificially multiply periods. Depth within the existing eras remains mandatory.

### 10.3 Occupied, Puppet, or Collapsing Regimes
Note the degree of autonomy. Justificatory language that is merely residual or externally imposed should be scored and described accordingly.

### 10.4 Highly Plural or Contested Systems
When multiple strong justificatory currents coexist (e.g., Israel), the era score should reflect the overall balance of the political system, while the text notes the principal competing currents.

---

## 11. Final Checklist Before Completion

Before considering a timeline document finished, verify every item:

- [ ] Title follows the standard format
- [ ] Introductory overview is 1–3 substantial paragraphs and characterises the long-term trajectory
- [ ] Converted Final Scores table is complete and consistent with the era sections
- [ ] Every era has a clear justificatory rationale for its boundaries
- [ ] Every era contains: opening description, 4–7 concrete analysis bullets, a real summary paragraph (≈70–120 words), Key Shift, and a multi-sentence score justification
- [ ] No summary paragraph is only two thin sentences
- [ ] Scores are consistent with the evidence presented and with the axis scoring bands
- [ ] **(Cultural 1A)** Transcendent vs mundane balance is explicitly addressed in every era
- [ ] Language remains analytical, concrete, and close to historical justificatory practice
- [ ] No section has been artificially shortened to reduce overall length

**If any item fails, the document is not finished.**

---

## 12. Relationship to Party Sections

This guide covers **era / timeline documents** only. Political party or faction analyses are separate documents and follow a different template (party totality + faction tables + individual faction write-ups). Era documents and party documents may cross-reference each other but should not be merged into a single file.

---

## 13. Explicit Instruction to Future Model Instances

You are required to follow the depth standards in this guide. 

- Do not produce thin bullets, two-sentence summaries, or single-sentence score justifications.
- Do not prioritise keeping the total document short over meeting the analytical requirements of each section.
- If meeting the depth standard makes the document long, that is the correct outcome.
- Writing a compressed version first and expanding it only after correction is inefficient and violates the spirit of this guide.

Proper depth on the first pass is the expected behaviour.
