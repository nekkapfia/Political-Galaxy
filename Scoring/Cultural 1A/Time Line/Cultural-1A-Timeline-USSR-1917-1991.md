# Cultural 1A: Eras of Moral Justification in the Soviet Union (1917–1991)

The Soviet Union was founded on the claim that Marxism-Leninism constituted scientific truth and historical necessity. The Party embodied the objective interests of the proletariat and the future communist society; popular sovereignty in the liberal sense was rejected. Across seven decades the intensity of transcendent ideological justification varied dramatically—from the revolutionary and Stalinist peaks, through partial pragmatic moderation, to the final crisis of belief.

## **1917–1928: Revolution, Civil War, and NEP – Final Score: 79**

The Bolshevik seizure of power and the subsequent civil war established Marxism-Leninism as the exclusive source of political truth. The Party claimed to act on the basis of historical science rather than the current preferences of the population.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Historical materialism was treated as scientific law; the Party’s correct line was therefore both true and morally obligatory.
* Class struggle and the dictatorship of the proletariat justified the suppression of “enemy” classes, the Cheka, and the Red Terror.
* War Communism pushed the logic of revolutionary necessity to an extreme; the NEP represented a tactical retreat justified as temporary and instrumental.
* Popular sovereignty in the liberal or even Soviet-democratic sense was subordinated to the Party’s claim to know the objective interests of the working class.
* The future communist society functioned as a transcendent goal that authorised present sacrifices.

The transcendent source was already dominant. The NEP introduced a visible pragmatic layer, but it was explicitly framed as a temporary concession rather than a change in ultimate authority.

**Key Shift:** This era installed Marxism-Leninism as scientific-moral truth and the Party as its exclusive interpreter.

### Why this produces a final score of 79
Transcendent sources (historical materialism, class struggle as moral duty, Party as embodiment of historical necessity) were primary. The NEP’s pragmatism remained secondary and tactical.

---

## **1929–1953: Stalinist Transformation and High Stalinism – Final Score: 86**

Forced collectivisation, industrialisation, the Great Terror, and the postwar reassertion of ideological control marked the highest intensity of transcendent justification in Soviet history.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Historical necessity and the correct Party line justified catastrophic human costs, including famine, mass deportation, and the Gulag.
* Class struggle was continuously re-invented to identify new internal enemies; loyalty to Stalin and the line became a moral test.
* Industrialisation and military strength were framed as proofs of ideological correctness and as preparation for the final historical victory of socialism.
* Individual rights, legal regularity, and popular consent were treated as irrelevant or as bourgeois survivals.
* The cult of Stalin fused personal authority with the claim to unique insight into historical laws.

The transcendent source reached its Soviet peak. Performance mattered enormously, but it was interpreted through and subordinated to the ideological framework; failure was attributed to sabotage or insufficient vigilance rather than to the framework itself.

**Key Shift:** This era pushed the logic of historical necessity and Party infallibility to its most extreme domestic expression.

### Why this produces a final score of 86
Transcendent sources were overwhelmingly dominant. Mundane considerations of popular welfare, consent, or even basic competence were systematically overridden by ideological requirements.

---

## **1953–1964: Khrushchev Thaw – Final Score: 61**

De-Stalinisation, the Secret Speech, and the emphasis on catching up with the West in living standards and technology introduced a significant pragmatic and popular-welfare element while retaining formal Marxist-Leninist orthodoxy.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* The crimes of the Stalin era were partially acknowledged, reducing the claim of continuous Party infallibility.
* Raising living standards, housing, and consumer goods became more central legitimating goals.
* Peaceful coexistence and technological competition with the West elevated performance criteria.
* Ideology remained the official language of authority, but its intensity and its capacity to justify unlimited coercion declined.
* Popular opinion and elite consensus began to matter more in practice, even if not in formal doctrine.

The transcendent source remained primary in official doctrine but lost intensity. Mundane sources (performance, popular welfare, stability) gained substantial ground.

**Key Shift:** This era marked the first major reduction in the intensity of transcendent ideological justification while preserving the formal monopoly of Marxism-Leninism.

### Why this produces a final score of 61
Transcendent sources were still formally dominant but significantly moderated. Mundane performance and welfare considerations became more organising in practice, producing a mixed-to-transcendent profile.

---

## **1964–1985: Brezhnev Stability – Final Score: 42**

The long Brezhnev period institutionalised a system in which ideology remained obligatory rhetoric while actual legitimacy rested increasingly on stability, great-power status, and modest improvements in living standards.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Developed socialism and the leading role of the Party continued to be asserted as historical and moral truths.
* In practice, stability, predictability, and the avoidance of both Stalinist terror and Khrushchevian upheaval became central.
* Great-power status, military parity, and the international position of the USSR supplied important legitimating resources.
* Economic performance and consumer satisfaction mattered, yet systemic reform was avoided.
* Ideology functioned more as a ritual language of authority than as a living source of radical mobilisation.

Mundane sources (stability, performance, great-power interest) became clearly dominant in practice. Transcendent ideological claims persisted as formal orthodoxy but lost much of their mobilising and justificatory force.

**Key Shift:** This era completed the transition from high-intensity ideological justification to a more pragmatic, stability-oriented authoritarianism still clothed in Marxist-Leninist language.

### Why this produces a final score of 42
Mundane sources were primary in the actual functioning of the system. Residual transcendent ideology kept the score from falling into the strongly mundane range.

---

## **1985–1991: Gorbachev and Collapse – Final Score: 33**

Glasnost, perestroika, and the attempt to revitalise the system through limited democratisation and radical economic reform accelerated the collapse of ideological authority.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Performance, efficiency, and the need to overcome stagnation became central diagnostic and justificatory themes.
* Glasnost elevated truth-telling, historical honesty, and limited public criticism as values, undermining the Party’s monopoly on truth.
* Popular input and electoral mechanisms were introduced, giving practical weight to consent.
* Official ideology was repeatedly revised and ultimately lost coherence as a binding source of authority.
* By the end, the Party’s claim to historical necessity was no longer credible to large parts of the elite or population.

Mundane sources (performance, pragmatism, emerging popular consent) became dominant. The transcendent ideological framework disintegrated.

**Key Shift:** This era marked the terminal crisis of Marxist-Leninist transcendent authority and the rapid rise of pragmatic and consent-based justifications that the system could not contain.

### Why this produces a final score of 33
Mundane sources were clearly primary. The residual formal ideology no longer functioned as an effective transcendent source, producing a strongly mundane profile in the system’s final years.
