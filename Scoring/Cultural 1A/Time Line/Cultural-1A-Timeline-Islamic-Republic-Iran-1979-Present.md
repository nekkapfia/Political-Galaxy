# Cultural 1A: Eras of Moral Justification in the Islamic Republic of Iran (1979–Present)

The Islamic Republic is founded on the doctrine of *Wilayat al-Faqih* (Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist) and the claim that legitimate political authority must implement divine law. Sovereignty ultimately belongs to God; the Supreme Leader and the qualified jurists are the authorised interpreters of that higher authority. Popular participation exists and has varied in importance, but it remains constitutionally and ideologically subordinated to clerical guardianship. The regime’s history shows fluctuations in the relative weight of revolutionary ideological intensity, pragmatic governance, and popular pressure, without ever abandoning the transcendent core.

## **1979–1989: Revolution, War, and Khomeini’s Leadership – Final Score: 83**

The Islamic Revolution and the eight-year war with Iraq established the core justificatory framework. Divine law, the authority of the jurist, and the moral drama of resistance against internal hypocrites and external arrogance were primary.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Sovereignty belongs to God; human law is valid only insofar as it conforms to Islamic criteria as interpreted by the qualified jurist.
* *Wilayat al-Faqih* located authoritative guardianship in Khomeini and the clerical system, standing above ordinary popular will.
* The Revolution itself was framed as a moral and religious uprising against tyranny, corruption, and foreign domination.
* The war with Iraq was presented as an imposed struggle and a test of faith; martyrdom and sacrifice carried high moral value.
* Popular mobilisation was real and intense, yet it was orchestrated and interpreted within the religious-revolutionary framework rather than as independent popular sovereignty.

The transcendent source was primary and highly mobilising. Mundane considerations of state survival and popular support were important but framed through religious duty and revolutionary justice.

**Key Shift:** This era institutionalised divine law and clerical guardianship as the ultimate sources of political legitimacy.

### Why this produces a final score of 83
Transcendent sources (divine law, Wilayat al-Faqih, revolutionary religious duty) were dominant and organising. The score reflects high ideological intensity during the founding and wartime period.

---

## **1989–1997: Post-Khomeini Reconstruction – Final Score: 71**

After Khomeini’s death the regime under Khamenei and Rafsanjani emphasised reconstruction, state-building, and economic recovery while preserving the theocratic core.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* *Wilayat al-Faqih* and the Islamic character of the state remained non-negotiable.
* Reconstruction, economic development, and the normalisation of state institutions gained greater justificatory weight.
* Revolutionary mobilisation language was partially de-emphasised in favour of expertise and pragmatism.
* Popular participation continued within the controlled electoral and clerical oversight system.

The transcendent core persisted, but mundane performance and state-consolidation considerations became more prominent.

**Key Shift:** This era reduced revolutionary intensity while locking in the institutional theocratic framework.

### Why this produces a final score of 71
Transcendent sources remained primary. Increased weight on reconstruction and pragmatism produced a moderate reduction from the revolutionary peak.

---

## **1997–2005: Reformist Opening – Final Score: 58**

The election of Mohammad Khatami brought a significant, though limited, elevation of popular participation, civil society, rule of law, and dialogue. The theocratic structure was not dismantled, but the relative weight of consent and legal-rational language increased.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Popular participation, electoral legitimacy, and the rights of the people within an Islamic framework were given greater emphasis.
* Rule of law, civil society, and dialogue among civilizations introduced more procedural and consent-oriented language.
* The Supreme Leader and the Guardian Council continued to exercise decisive oversight; reform remained bounded.
* Religious and revolutionary foundations were still affirmed by all major actors.

Mundane and consent-based sources gained substantial ground within a still-theocratic system, producing the lowest transcendent intensity of the Islamic Republic to that point.

**Key Shift:** This era demonstrated the maximum scope for popular and legal-rational justification inside the *Wilayat al-Faqih* framework.

### Why this produces a final score of 58
Transcendent sources (divine law and clerical guardianship) remained constitutionally primary, but popular participation and rule-of-law language acquired real weight, producing a mixed profile.

---

## **2005–2013: Hardliner Reassertion – Final Score: 74**

The Ahmadinejad presidency and the reassertion of more confrontational ideological language marked a return toward higher transcendent intensity, especially in justice, anti-imperialism, and the defence of the system against internal and external enemies.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Justice for the oppressed, resistance to arrogance, and the defence of the Islamic system regained prominence.
* Electoral controversy (2009) and subsequent repression were justified as defence of the Revolution and the Leader’s authority.
* Nuclear policy and foreign defiance were framed in terms of national dignity and resistance as moral duties.
* Clerical and revolutionary legitimacy were reasserted against reformist and popular-protest challenges.

The transcendent source strengthened relative to the reformist period. Popular consent was treated as secondary when it conflicted with the system’s ideological red lines.

**Key Shift:** This era reversed much of the reformist opening and restored a more confrontational ideological posture.

### Why this produces a final score of 74
Transcendent sources regained clear primacy. The score reflects a partial return toward revolutionary-era intensity within a more institutionalised system.

---

## **2013–Present: Mixed Pragmatism and Enduring Theocracy – Final Score: 69**

The Rouhani and subsequent periods have combined pragmatic engagement on economic and diplomatic issues with the unchanged theocratic core and periodic reassertion of ideological control, especially after popular protests.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* *Wilayat al-Faqih* and the Islamic character of the state remain the non-negotiable foundation.
* Economic performance, sanctions relief, and pragmatic diplomacy have been important legitimating themes at times.
* Popular protests have been met with a mixture of limited concession and forceful reassertion of the system’s religious-political red lines.
* Resistance, anti-imperialism, and the defence of the oppressed continue to supply moral language, especially in regional policy.

The profile remains mixed but still clearly on the transcendent side of the spectrum. The theocratic core has not been relinquished.

**Key Shift:** This era has shown the regime’s capacity to alternate between pragmatic and ideological registers without abandoning the ultimate authority of divine law and the Guardian Jurist.

### Why this produces a final score of 69
Transcendent sources (divine law, clerical guardianship) remain primary and constitutionally entrenched. Mundane performance and limited popular input influence policy but do not displace the higher religious-juridical framework.
