# Cultural 1A: Eras of Moral Justification in the People’s Republic of China (1949–Present)

The People’s Republic of China has undergone the most dramatic shifts in moral justification sources of any major power since 1949. The early decades were defined by a totalising transcendent ideology (Marxism-Leninism-Mao Zedong Thought treated as scientific truth and moral duty). The reform era under Deng and his successors introduced a strong pragmatic and performance-based (mundane) layer while retaining formal ideological continuity. Under Xi Jinping the Party has sought to re-elevate ideological and civilizational claims, producing a renewed emphasis on transcendent-style sources (Party leadership as historical necessity, Chinese national rejuvenation, and Marxist guidance) alongside continued performance legitimacy.

## **1949–1956: Revolutionary Foundation – Final Score: 82**

The founding years of the PRC established Marxism-Leninism-Mao Zedong Thought as the authoritative source of moral and political truth.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Party leadership and socialist transformation were justified as the inevitable and morally necessary outcome of historical laws.
- Class struggle and the construction of a new society carried binding moral force.
- Land reform, nationalisation, and early campaigns were framed as both scientific and ethically required.
- Alternative sources of legitimacy (traditional, liberal, or purely pragmatic) were systematically marginalised.

The transcendent source (ideology as scientific-moral truth) was overwhelmingly dominant.

**Key Shift:** This era installed a totalising ideological framework in which moral authority derived from the Party’s mastery of historical laws and revolutionary duty.

---

## **1957–1965: Socialist Construction and Campaigns – Final Score: 78**

The period from the Anti-Rightist Campaign through the Great Leap Forward and its aftermath maintained high ideological intensity while attempting rapid socialist construction.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Continuous revolution and ideological correctness remained central.
- Economic and organisational failures were frequently attributed to insufficient ideological commitment rather than pragmatic design flaws.
- Mass mobilisation was justified as both practical and morally elevating.
- Pragmatic adjustments after the Great Leap were limited and subordinate to ideological recovery.

Transcendent ideological sources retained clear primacy.

**Key Shift:** This era demonstrated the continued priority of ideological mobilisation over purely pragmatic correction, even after catastrophic policy failure.

---

## **1966–1976: Cultural Revolution – Final Score: 88**

The Cultural Revolution represented the extreme point of transcendent ideological justification in PRC history.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Class struggle, purity of revolutionary line, and loyalty to Mao Thought were treated as absolute moral requirements.
- Institutional, educational, and cultural destruction was justified as necessary to prevent revisionist betrayal.
- Personal and organisational loyalty tests functioned as moral tests.
- Pragmatic governance and expertise were systematically denigrated.

The transcendent source reached its highest intensity; mundane considerations of competence and stability were actively suppressed.

**Key Shift:** This era pushed ideological purity and continuous revolution to their most radical expression, producing the strongest transcendent profile of the entire period.

---

## **1978–1992: Deng Reform Era – Final Score: 34**

Deng Xiaoping’s reforms marked the most significant shift in moral justification sources since 1949.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- “Seeking truth from facts” and the criterion of productive forces elevated pragmatic performance.
- Economic growth, living standards, and national strength became central legitimating outcomes.
- Ideology was retained as formal orthodoxy but increasingly treated as flexible guidance rather than rigid command.
- The 1989 crisis produced a temporary reassertion of political control, yet the underlying pragmatic orientation continued.

Mundane sources (performance, pragmatism, national strength) became clearly dominant while formal ideological continuity was preserved.

**Key Shift:** This era decisively subordinated pure ideological transcendence to pragmatic performance and national strengthening.

---

## **1992–2012: Performance Legitimacy Consolidation – Final Score: 28**

The Jiang Zemin and Hu Jintao periods further entrenched economic performance, stability, and technocratic governance as the primary sources of legitimacy.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- GDP growth, poverty reduction, and social stability were the dominant justifications for Party rule.
- Ideological language continued but functioned largely as background orthodoxy.
- Scientific development and harmonious society concepts mixed limited moral language with technocratic goals.
- Popular support was treated as an empirical outcome of performance rather than a transcendent mandate.

Mundane performance-based sources were strongly dominant.

**Key Shift:** This era institutionalised performance legitimacy as the central pillar of moral and political authority.

---

## **2012–Present: Xi Era Reassertion – Final Score: 46**

Under Xi Jinping the Party has sought to re-elevate ideology, national rejuvenation, and civilizational confidence while retaining performance requirements.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Marxism, Party leadership, and the “Chinese Dream” of national rejuvenation have been reasserted as historical and moral necessities.
- Traditional Chinese culture and civilizational continuity have been incorporated as supporting sources.
- Performance and stability remain essential, but are now framed within a broader ideological and historical narrative.
- Universalist liberal claims are rejected as incompatible with Chinese characteristics and historical stage.

A more mixed profile has emerged: transcendent-style ideological and civilizational claims have regained weight, yet mundane performance legitimacy continues to operate as a necessary foundation.

**Key Shift:** This era has partially restored the weight of ideological and civilizational sources while continuing to rely on pragmatic performance, producing the most mixed profile since the early reform years.
