# Cultural 1A: Eras of Moral Justification in Russia (1991–Present)

Since the collapse of the USSR, Russia has moved from an initial experiment with liberal and market-oriented justifications toward a model centred on state sovereignty, national interest, historical continuity, and civilizational distinctiveness. The early 1990s featured relatively strong (if unstable) mundane and liberal-individualist sources. From the 2000s onward, and especially after 2012, justifications have increasingly drawn on the interests of the Russian state and people as a historical community, traditional values, and resistance to external ideological pressure. Transcendent universalist sources have remained marginal.

## **1991–1999: Yeltsin Transition – Final Score: 38**

The first post-Soviet decade was defined by rapid liberalisation, economic shock therapy, and the search for a new legitimating framework after the collapse of Marxism-Leninism.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Market reform and democratisation were justified through a mix of pragmatic necessity (avoiding collapse) and liberal claims about freedom and normalcy.
- Residual Soviet-era language of social protection and great-power status persisted.
- Western models were treated both as practical templates and as moral examples.
- Authority remained fragile; justifications often shifted with political survival needs.

A relatively mixed but unstable profile, with mundane and liberal-individualist sources prominent.

**Key Shift:** This era replaced Marxist-Leninist transcendent ideology with an unstable combination of liberal, market-pragmatic, and residual state-interest justifications.

---

## **2000–2012: Early Putin Consolidation – Final Score: 22**

Vladimir Putin’s first two presidential terms and the Medvedev interlude prioritised state capacity, economic recovery, and the restoration of order.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Stability, economic growth, and the restoration of state authority were the primary justifications.
- National interest and sovereignty were framed as practical necessities after the 1990s.
- Liberal and universalist language declined sharply.
- Tradition and historical continuity began to appear as supporting resources rather than primary sources.

Mundane sources (state interest, stability, pragmatic recovery) became clearly dominant.

**Key Shift:** This era established state capacity and national interest as the central sources of moral and political legitimacy.

---

## **2012–2022: Conservative Turn and Civilizational Framing – Final Score: 14**

The return of Putin to the presidency and the subsequent years saw stronger emphasis on traditional values, resistance to Western liberalism, and Russia as a distinct civilization.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Traditional values, historical continuity, and the defence of Russian civilizational identity became more prominent.
- These were framed primarily as practical and historical goods for the Russian people rather than as transcendent universal commands.
- Sovereignty and resistance to external pressure were justified through the interests of the existing Russian state and population.
- Universalist human-rights language was increasingly rejected as foreign and instrumental.

Mundane sources remained dominant, now reinforced by civilizational and traditional-values rhetoric treated as empirical and historical inheritance.

**Key Shift:** This era added a civilizational and traditional-values layer to an already strongly mundane foundation centred on state and national interest.

---

## **2022–Present: Wartime and Existential Framing – Final Score: 11**

The full-scale invasion of Ukraine and subsequent confrontation with the West have intensified justifications based on existential state interest and civilizational survival.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Policy is justified overwhelmingly through the existential interests of the Russian state and people.
- Historical continuity, resistance to external domination, and the defence of a distinct way of life are central.
- Universalist and liberal moral claims are treated as hostile instruments.
- Popular support and national resilience are invoked as sources of legitimacy.

Transcendent universalist sources are almost entirely absent. Moral authority rests on state survival, national continuity, and the interests of the existing political community.

**Key Shift:** This era has further intensified the primacy of existential state and national interest, producing one of the strongest mundane profiles in the post-Soviet period.
