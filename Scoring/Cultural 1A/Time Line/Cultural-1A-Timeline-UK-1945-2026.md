## Era 1: 1945–1951 – Post-War Moral Settlement  **(Final Score: 66)**

### Brief Overview
This short but foundational period immediately followed the Second World War. The Attlee Labour government oversaw a major programme of national reconstruction, including the creation of the National Health Service and the expansion of the welfare state. Public and governmental discourse framed these changes as both a practical response to wartime experience and a moral obligation to build a fairer society.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority during this era drew on a strong combination of transcendent and mundane sources, with transcendent elements carrying particular weight. The Beveridge Report and the establishment of the NHS were justified not only through arguments of economic efficiency and social stability, but as a fundamental moral duty to eliminate the “five giants” of Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, and Idleness. The war itself was frequently invoked as a shared national sacrifice that created a collective obligation to build a better Britain. Language around human dignity, social justice, and responsibility to future generations was common in both government statements and public debate. While evidence from wartime planning and economic necessity played an important role, these were often presented as supporting a deeper ethical project of national renewal and fairness after years of hardship and inequality.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 66
Both transcendent and mundane sources were actively present and integrated, but transcendent elements carried significant influence. The moral case for the welfare state was framed in terms of justice, dignity, and collective duty — principles that extended beyond immediate empirical outcomes. According to the rubric, the transcendent source is clearly present and influential while the mundane source remains active but secondary. This produces a relatively balanced score with a clear transcendent lean.

## Era 2: 1951–1964 – Post-War Pragmatic Consensus **(Final Score: 26)**


### Brief Overview
This period covers the Conservative governments of Churchill, Eden, Macmillan, and Douglas-Home. While the core institutions of the welfare state were accepted across the political spectrum, the tone of justification shifted toward economic growth, rising prosperity, and the maintenance of social stability.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority became more balanced but leaned increasingly toward mundane sources. Governments justified policy primarily through arguments of economic expansion, rising living standards, and Britain’s need to adapt to a changing world. Tradition and established institutions were increasingly defended as arrangements that had delivered stability and success rather than as higher moral goods. Public debate around issues such as immigration and the pace of decolonisation incorporated stronger pragmatic elements, focusing on housing, employment, and Britain’s global role. While some moral language around national duty and fairness remained, it was less central than in the immediate post-war years. Justification increasingly centred on what worked and what maintained Britain as a modern, prosperous, and stable society.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 26
The mundane source became clearly dominant during this era. Moral justification relied more heavily on evidence of economic performance, institutional continuity, and practical outcomes for citizens. Transcendent appeals were still present, particularly around ideas of national duty and social stability, but they exerted noticeably less influence on core policy reasoning than in the previous period. According to the rubric, the transcendent source is reduced from the previous era while remaining visible in some areas. This fits the 21–30M band.

## Era 3: 1964–1970 – Social Liberalisation  (**Final Score: 17**)

### Brief Overview
This period, dominated by Harold Wilson’s Labour governments, saw significant social reform alongside ongoing economic challenges. Major changes in social legislation occurred, and public discourse increasingly emphasised modernisation and individual freedom.

### Moral Justification Sources
Mundane sources strengthened considerably. Social reforms such as the legalisation of abortion, the decriminalisation of homosexuality, and changes to divorce law were justified increasingly through arguments of personal liberty, harm reduction, and the need to reflect modern social realities. The language of “modernisation” became prominent, often linked to claims about what evidence and progress required. While some transcendent moral rhetoric around equality and social justice remained, policy justification relied more heavily on pragmatic considerations and individual autonomy. Economic debates also focused on what measures would deliver growth and stability in a competitive world.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 17
The transcendent source continued to decline in influence. Moral authority was increasingly derived from individual liberty, empirical outcomes, and the need to modernise institutions and laws. Transcendent claims were still made, particularly around social justice, but they competed with — and were often secondary to — mundane arguments. According to the rubric, the transcendent source is noticeably absent from meaningful portions of moral reasoning, especially on social issues. This fits the 31–40M band.

## Era 4: 1970–1974 – Economic Crisis and Political Strain  
**Final Score: 10**

### Brief Overview
This short but difficult period covered Edward Heath’s Conservative government and the early return of Harold Wilson. It was marked by serious economic difficulties, industrial conflict, and growing pressure on the post-war economic model.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority became strongly mundane. Governments and public debate focused heavily on pragmatic questions of economic management, inflation control, industrial relations, and Britain’s entry into the European Economic Community. Justification for policy was increasingly framed in terms of what was necessary to restore economic stability and national competitiveness. While moral language around fairness and national interest was still used, it was largely tied to practical outcomes rather than higher principles. The period saw growing emphasis on competent crisis management over grand moral visions.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 10
The transcendent source was significantly reduced. Moral justification rested overwhelmingly on pragmatic considerations, economic necessity, and the interests of the current population. According to the rubric, the transcendent source has significant absence from substantial areas of moral argumentation. This fits the 31–40M band, moving toward the higher end as mundane sources became increasingly dominant.

## Era 5: 1974–1979 – Crisis of the Post-War Settlement  (**Final Score: 10**


### Brief Overview
This period covers the final years of Harold Wilson’s government and James Callaghan’s administration. It was marked by serious economic difficulties, high inflation, industrial unrest, and the visible strain on the post-war economic model, culminating in the Winter of Discontent.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority during this era was strongly mundane. Governments and public debate focused heavily on pragmatic questions of economic management, wage restraint, and how to respond to industrial conflict and rising prices. Justification for policy was framed primarily in terms of what was necessary to restore stability and protect living standards. While some traditional moral language around fairness and social solidarity remained, it was increasingly tied to immediate practical outcomes rather than higher principles. The period saw growing public frustration with grand post-war visions, with emphasis placed instead on competent crisis management and dealing with pressing economic realities.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 10
The transcendent source was significantly reduced. Moral justification rested overwhelmingly on pragmatic considerations, economic necessity, and the interests of the current population. According to the rubric, the transcendent source has significant absence from substantial areas of moral argumentation. This fits the 31–40M band, moving toward the higher end as mundane sources became dominant.

## Era 6: 1979–1983 – Early Thatcher Revolution  (**Final Score: 5**)

### Brief Overview
This period covers Margaret Thatcher’s first term in office. It was characterised by a deliberate and ideologically driven break with the post-war consensus, including major shifts in economic policy, industrial relations, and the role of the state.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority in this era became very strongly mundane, with a clear ideological edge. The government explicitly rejected collective moral frameworks and the idea of society as a moral entity. Margaret Thatcher’s famous statement that “there is no such thing as society” encapsulated a broader shift toward justifying policy through individual responsibility, market outcomes, and personal effort. Economic reforms such as privatisation, tax cuts, and union legislation were defended primarily on grounds of efficiency, incentives, and what would deliver better results for individuals and the country as a whole. National revival was framed in terms of restoring Britain’s competitiveness and pride through practical measures rather than through appeals to higher collective duties. Transcendent sources of moral authority were actively downplayed or rejected in favour of arguments rooted in individual agency and empirical outcomes.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 5
The transcendent source was almost entirely absent from substantive moral reasoning. Moral authority was derived overwhelmingly from individual responsibility, market results, and the interests of citizens as economic actors. According to the rubric, this level of practical absence of the transcendent source places the era at the highest end of mundane dominance. The Final Score of 5 reflects an extremely strong mundane lean during the most ideologically intense phase of the Thatcher revolution.

## Era 7: 1983–1990 – Thatcher Consolidation  (**Final Score: 7**)

### Brief Overview
This period covers Margaret Thatcher’s second and third terms. Following the 1983 election victory, the government moved from radical ideological reform toward consolidating and embedding the changes made in the early 1980s.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority remained very strongly mundane, though the tone became somewhat more pragmatic than in the first term. Policy was increasingly justified through arguments of what worked, what delivered results, and what strengthened Britain’s position. Individual responsibility and the benefits of a market-oriented economy continued to be central themes. While some moral language around national pride and self-reliance remained, it was largely tied to practical achievements such as economic growth, falling inflation, and home ownership. The government presented its reforms as having been validated by results rather than as part of an ongoing moral crusade. Transcendent sources continued to have very little presence in mainstream justification.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 7
The transcendent source remained almost entirely absent. Moral authority was grounded in individual responsibility, measurable economic outcomes, and the practical interests of citizens. While the ideological intensity of the early 1980s had somewhat moderated, the underlying mundane character of justification remained consistent. According to the rubric, this represents a very high level of practical absence of the transcendent source. The Final Score of 7 reflects a very strong mundane lean during the consolidation phase of Thatcherism.

## Era 8: 1990–1997 – Post-Thatcher Consolidation  (**Final Score: 12**)

### Brief Overview
This period covers John Major’s Conservative governments. It followed the intense ideological phase of the 1980s and was characterised by efforts to stabilise and normalise the changes introduced under Margaret Thatcher while managing internal party divisions and economic challenges.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority remained strongly mundane. The government justified policy primarily through arguments of economic prudence, continuity, and competent administration. While some moral language around personal responsibility and traditional values persisted from the Thatcher era, it was generally presented in a less confrontational and more pragmatic manner. Justification often focused on what was necessary to maintain stability, control public spending, and preserve Britain’s international reputation. The Major government tended to frame its approach as responsible stewardship rather than ideological transformation. Transcendent sources continued to have very little presence in mainstream political justification.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 12
The transcendent source remained marginal. Moral authority was grounded in pragmatic governance, institutional continuity, and the practical interests of citizens. According to the rubric, the transcendent source has significant absence from substantive moral reasoning. This fits the 31–40M band, though slightly lower than the peak Thatcher years as the tone became less ideologically charged.

## Era 9: 1997–2010 – New Labour Modernisation  (**Final Score: 24**)

### Brief Overview
This period covers the Labour governments of Tony Blair and Gordon Brown. It was marked by a deliberate attempt to modernise both the party and the country, combining economic prudence with social reform.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority was mundane dominant but included a more noticeable layer of transcendent rhetoric than the periods immediately before or after. New Labour frequently used moral language around social justice, opportunity, and “modernising” Britain. Concepts such as “ethical foreign policy” and the moral case for intervention (e.g. Kosovo, Sierra Leone) were prominent in the early years. At the same time, the government placed heavy emphasis on evidence-based policy, economic stability, and “what works.” Justification often combined pragmatic arguments about economic management and public service reform with moral appeals to fairness and social inclusion. This created a distinctive style where transcendent rhetoric was used more visibly than under the Conservatives, but the underlying logic remained largely outcome-focused and technocratic.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 24
The mundane source remained clearly dominant, but transcendent elements were more visible than in surrounding periods, particularly in the early Blair years. Moral justification frequently blended evidence and pragmatic governance with moral claims about justice and progress. According to the rubric, both sources were present, but the transcendent source was reduced and often served a rhetorical rather than foundational role. This fits the 21–30M band.

## Era 10: 2010–2016 – Austerity and Pragmatic Conservatism  (**Final Score: 8**)

### Brief Overview
This period covers the Conservative-Liberal Democrat Coalition government and the early years of David Cameron’s majority Conservative government. It was dominated by the response to the 2008 financial crisis and the subsequent programme of austerity.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority was very strongly mundane. The government justified austerity and public spending cuts primarily through arguments of fiscal responsibility, long-term economic sustainability, and fairness between generations. Policy was framed as necessary and evidence-based rather than ideological. While some moral language around “responsible” government and protecting future generations was used, it was presented in a pragmatic, almost technocratic manner. The emphasis was on what was required to restore economic stability and reduce the deficit, rather than on higher moral principles. Transcendent sources had very little influence on the core justification for major policy decisions.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 8
The transcendent source was largely absent from substantive moral reasoning. Justification rested overwhelmingly on pragmatic economic arguments, fiscal necessity, and measurable outcomes. According to the rubric, this represents a very high level of practical absence of the transcendent source. The Final Score of 8 reflects a very strong mundane lean.

## Era 11: 2016–2024 – Brexit Sovereignty Era  (**Final Score: 4**)

### Brief Overview
This period covers the Brexit referendum, the governments of Theresa May and Boris Johnson, and the short administrations of Liz Truss and Rishi Sunak. It was defined by the political dominance of the Brexit process and its aftermath.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority became extremely mundane, with popular sovereignty emerging as the primary source of legitimacy. The 2016 referendum result was repeatedly invoked as “the will of the people,” which was treated as having overriding moral and political authority. Justification for policy — particularly on immigration, trade, and the UK’s relationship with the EU — was framed overwhelmingly in terms of national sovereignty and the democratic right of British citizens to control their own laws and borders. While some moral language around national pride and taking back control was used, it was closely tied to the practical exercise of sovereignty rather than to higher transcendent principles. Transcendent sources had almost no meaningful presence in mainstream political justification during this period.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 4
The transcendent source was almost entirely absent. Moral authority was derived overwhelmingly from popular sovereignty and the interests of existing British citizens. According to the rubric, this represents one of the highest levels of practical absence of the transcendent source in modern British political history. The Final Score of 4 reflects an extremely strong mundane lean.

## Era 12: 2024–Present – Post-Brexit Technocratic Turn  (**Final Score: 9**)

### Brief Overview
This period covers the early years of Keir Starmer’s Labour government. It represents an attempt to move away from the high political drama of the Brexit years toward more stable and pragmatic governance.

### Moral Justification Sources
Moral authority is very strongly mundane. The government has justified its approach primarily through arguments of economic stability, competent administration, and delivering practical results for citizens. There has been a noticeable reduction in both populist sovereignty rhetoric and earlier forms of moral or ethical foreign policy language. Policy is increasingly framed in terms of what is necessary, evidence-based, and deliverable. While some moral language around fairness and public service remains, it is generally presented in a pragmatic rather than idealistic manner. Transcendent sources continue to have very limited influence on the core justification of government policy.

### Why This Produces a Final Score of 9
The transcendent source remains marginal. Moral authority is grounded in pragmatic governance, economic management, and the practical interests of citizens. According to the rubric, this represents a very high level of practical absence of the transcendent source, though slightly less extreme than the peak of the Brexit period. The Final Score of 9 reflects a very strong mundane lean.
