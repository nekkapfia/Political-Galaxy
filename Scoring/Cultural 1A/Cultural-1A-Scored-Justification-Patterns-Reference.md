# Cultural 1A Scored Justification Patterns Reference

**Version:** 1.0 (19 July 2026)  
Initial release built from the six completed country timelines (Britain, Germany, USA, France, Russia, PRC).

**Purpose:** Pure reference document for the **observable sources of moral justification** scored under Cultural 1A (Foundation). Contains only neutered descriptions of justification patterns, the typical Final Score ranges they produce, and the rationale drawn from the six country documents. No ideologies, historical regimes, politicians, or moral evaluations of content. This is the single source of truth for consistent scoring of eras and parties on Cultural 1A.

**Quick Scoring Workflow (for new eras or parties):**
1. Identify the dominant sources actually invoked when justifying why positions are right or necessary (manifestos, values statements, major speeches, legislative debates, institutional guidance).
2. Match the observed pattern to one of the main categories below.
3. Immediately check the corresponding **Outside of Scope** notes to confirm the element belongs in Cultural 1A.
4. Locate the closest precedent pattern and inherit the documented Final Score range + rationale (adjust only with explicit, rule-based justification based on breadth, consistency, and primacy).
5. Apply totality judgment across domains. One strong quote does not override a consistent pattern.
6. Convert original 0–50 + T/M score to Final Score using: Final Score = 50 + (Original Score × +1 for T or −1 for M).

## 1. Strong Transcendent – Ideological / Sacred / Absolute Patterns

**Quick Reference Table (sorted by typical Final Score descending)**

| Pattern | Typical Final Score | Short Summary |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|
| Extreme ideological purity and continuous revolution as moral absolute | 82–88 | Ideology treated as absolute moral requirement; pragmatism actively suppressed. |
| Revolutionary ideology as scientific and moral truth | 78–82 | Historical laws + moral duty; alternative sources systematically marginalised. |
| Reasserted ideological + civilizational claims alongside performance | 42–48 | Ideology and national rejuvenation regain weight while performance remains necessary. |

### Extreme Ideological Purity / Continuous Revolution as Moral Absolute
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Justification of policy and personal conduct through absolute ideological correctness, purity of line, and continuous struggle against deviation. Pragmatic competence and institutional stability are treated as secondary or suspect.

**Typical Final Score Range: 82–88**

**Rationale**: Transcendent source reaches maximum practical intensity. Mundane considerations of competence, evidence, or stability are actively subordinated or denounced. Observed in peak Cultural Revolution framing (PRC 1966–1976).

### Revolutionary Ideology as Scientific-Moral Truth
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Party or movement leadership and transformative programmes justified as the inevitable and morally necessary outcome of historical laws. Class or historical struggle carries binding moral force.

**Typical Final Score Range: 78–82**

**Rationale**: Ideology functions simultaneously as scientific truth and moral duty. Alternative sources (traditional, liberal, purely pragmatic) are marginalised. Observed in early PRC foundation and high-mobilisation periods.

### Reasserted Ideological and Civilizational Claims with Performance Legitimacy
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Formal ideology and civilizational/national rejuvenation narratives are elevated as historical and moral necessities, while economic performance and stability remain indispensable practical justifications.

**Typical Final Score Range: 42–48**

**Rationale**: Both transcendent-style ideological/civilizational claims and mundane performance sources are actively present. Produces a mixed profile. Observed in PRC under Xi (2012–Present).

## 2. Strong Transcendent – Ecological / Intergenerational / Planetary Patterns

**Quick Reference Table**

| Pattern | Typical Final Score | Short Summary |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|
| Ecological justice + duties to future generations and non-human nature | 76–86 | Planet and future generations treated as moral claimants that can override present preferences. |

### Ecological Justice and Intergenerational / Planetary Duty
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Radical transformation justified primarily through duties to the planet, non-human species, and future generations. Science is used diagnostically; the moral “ought” derives from the ethical framework of ecological justice and intergenerational responsibility.

**Typical Final Score Range: 76–86**

**Rationale**: Transcendent source is primary and organising. Mundane sources (science, evidence, pragmatic costs) are present but instrumental. Observed in Green Party platforms (Britain, Germany) and high ecological-justice framing.

## 3. Clear to Strong Transcendent – Inherent Rights / Universal Dignity / Justice as Moral Imperative

**Quick Reference Table**

| Pattern | Typical Final Score | Short Summary |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|
| Civil rights / inherent dignity as moral imperative | 58–68 | Justice and equal protection framed as binding moral duties independent of current consensus. |
| Progressive justice language with technocratic elements | 38–48 | Fairness and inclusion language carries real weight alongside evidence-based governance. |
| Fundamental liberal values / inherent rights framework | 68–76 | Liberty, equality, community, and human rights treated as fundamental values that the party exists to protect. |

### Inherent Dignity and Justice as Moral Imperative (Civil Rights Peak Style)
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Major reforms justified primarily through appeals to inherent human dignity, equal protection, and the moral duty to end systemic injustice. Pragmatic arguments are secondary or supportive.

**Typical Final Score Range: 58–68**

**Rationale**: Transcendent source (inherent rights and justice as moral duty) is primary in the defining domain. Observed in US civil rights peak (1964–1980) and similar justice-centred periods.

### Progressive Justice + Technocratic Pragmatism
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Policy justified through a combination of fairness/justice/inclusion language and evidence-based or outcome-focused governance. Neither source fully dominates.

**Typical Final Score Range: 38–48**

**Rationale**: Functional integration of both sources. Transcendent moral language is visible and recurring but does not displace pragmatic justification. Observed in New Labour, Obama-era, and similar centre-left modernising periods.

### Fundamental Liberal Values / Inherent Rights Framework
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Party or institutional identity defined by balancing fundamental values (liberty, equality, community) and protecting inherent rights against majoritarian or state overreach. Mundane mechanisms serve the higher principles.

**Typical Final Score Range: 68–76**

**Rationale**: Transcendent source (fundamental values and inherent rights) supplies primary moral authority. Observed in Liberal Democrat-style framing (Britain) and similar rights-centred liberal parties.

## 4. Mixed / Balanced Patterns

**Quick Reference Table**

| Pattern | Typical Final Score | Short Summary |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|
| Republican / constitutional principles as higher-order background legitimacy | 41–54 | Shared republican or constitutional order functions as quasi-transcendent background while practical governance remains pragmatic. |
| Early post-war moral reconstruction + pragmatic necessity | 54–66 | Strong moral duty language (justice, dignity, anti-totalitarian) combined with reconstruction pragmatism. |

### Republican / Constitutional Principles as Higher-Order Background
**Neutered Base Pattern**: The Republic, Constitution, or equivalent foundational order is treated as a higher source of legitimacy, while day-to-day policy is justified primarily through competence, stability, and outcomes.

**Typical Final Score Range: 41–54**

**Rationale**: Persistent transcendent background source (the Republic/Constitution) coexists with strongly pragmatic operational justification. Observed across multiple French eras.

### Early Post-War Moral Reconstruction
**Neutered Base Pattern**: National reconstruction and major social programmes justified through a combination of moral duty (justice, dignity, anti-fascist/anti-totalitarian obligation) and pragmatic necessity.

**Typical Final Score Range: 54–66**

**Rationale**: Both sources actively present and integrated; transcendent elements carry significant weight in the defining projects of the era. Observed in early post-1945 Britain and analogous reconstruction periods.

## 5. Strongly Mundane – Pragmatic / Performance / Evidence-Based Patterns

**Quick Reference Table**

| Pattern | Typical Final Score | Short Summary |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|
| Performance legitimacy + technocratic governance | 22–34 | Economic growth, stability, and measurable outcomes as primary sources of legitimacy. |
| Individual responsibility + market outcomes + popular will | 22–28 | Justification centred on personal agency, incentives, and results. |
| Competence, reform, and institutional functionality | 28–36 | Policy justified through what works, governability, and long-term sustainability. |

### Performance Legitimacy and Technocratic Governance
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Ruling authority justified primarily through economic performance, social stability, poverty reduction, and technocratic competence. Formal ideology is retained as background orthodoxy or flexible guidance.

**Typical Final Score Range: 22–34**

**Rationale**: Mundane sources (performance, evidence, outcomes) are clearly dominant. Observed in Deng reform era, Jiang-Hu period (PRC), and multiple technocratic Western periods.

### Individual Responsibility + Market Outcomes + Popular Will
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Economic and social policy justified through individual responsibility, incentives, measurable results, and the will or interests of citizens as economic actors.

**Typical Final Score Range: 22–28**

**Rationale**: Transcendent collective moral claims are reduced. Mundane sources of agency, results, and popular will predominate. Observed in Reagan-era and similar individualist-pragmatic periods.

### Competence, Reform, and Institutional Functionality
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Major reforms and governance justified through arguments of economic competitiveness, institutional effectiveness, evidence, and long-term sustainability. Higher moral abstraction is limited.

**Typical Final Score Range: 28–36**

**Rationale**: Strongly mundane. Observed in multiple French technocratic periods, Major-era Britain, and similar competence-focused governments.

## 6. Very Strong to Extreme Mundane – Popular Sovereignty / Citizen Interest / State Continuity Patterns

**Quick Reference Table**

| Pattern | Typical Final Score | Short Summary |
|---------|---------------------|---------------|
| Popular sovereignty + national interest of living citizens | 11–19 | Will of the people and interests of current citizens as primary or near-exclusive sources. |
| State capacity + civilizational continuity as historical inheritance | 11–22 | State interest and historical continuity treated as practical/historical goods. |
| Near-pure popular sovereignty / existential state interest | 3–12 | Existential interests of the political community or state as overriding source. |

### Popular Sovereignty and National Interest of Living Citizens
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Policy justified overwhelmingly through the democratic will of the current population and the practical interests, security, and cultural continuity of living citizens. Higher moral claims that override popular will are rejected or marginalised.

**Typical Final Score Range: 11–19**

**Rationale**: Transcendent sources have very limited presence. Observed in Brexit-era Britain, Trump 2016–2020, Rassemblement National-style framing, and similar sovereignty-centred periods.

### State Capacity + Civilizational Continuity as Historical Inheritance
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Legitimacy derived from state capacity, stability, and the continuity of the political community as a historical entity. Traditional or civilizational language is treated as empirical/historical inheritance rather than sacred command.

**Typical Final Score Range: 11–22**

**Rationale**: Strongly to very strongly mundane. Observed across post-2000 Russia and similar state-centred systems.

### Near-Pure Popular Sovereignty / Existential State Interest
**Neutered Base Pattern**: Justification rests almost exclusively on the existential interests of the existing political community or state, popular resilience, and resistance to external ideological pressure. Universalist moral claims are treated as foreign or instrumental.

**Typical Final Score Range: 3–12**

**Rationale**: Highest practical absence of transcendent sources. Observed in wartime/existential framing (Russia 2022–Present) and the most sovereignty-pure populist periods.

## Outside of Scope / Cross-Slider Exclusions

**Purpose**: This section records patterns or elements reviewed during Cultural 1A analysis that fall outside the defined scope (observable sources invoked in moral justification). They are excluded to maintain **Scope Purity**.

**How to Use**: When a new pattern appears, check whether it is primarily about:
- Enforcement, punishment, or coercion intensity → Cultural 1B (Dogmatism)
- Direction or content of change regarding tradition vs progress → Cultural 5A
- Pace and intensity of change → Cultural 5B
- National sovereignty, borders, membership → Core 2A
- Outcomes, effectiveness, or real-world results → Outside Cultural 1A entirely
- Private beliefs, rhetoric without justificatory use, or symbolic declarations without moral-authority claims

**Common Exclusions**:
- Pure enforcement or punishment mechanisms (distinct from the justificatory source)
- Policy outcomes or empirical success/failure
- Directional content of moral rules (what is prohibited or required)
- Institutional power or cultural dominance
- Symbolic or ceremonial language that does not function as a source of moral authority
- Self-reported intentions or private motivations

**Core Rules Applied**: Direct observable sources only; Scope Purity; Totality judgment across domains; Primary sources preferred.
