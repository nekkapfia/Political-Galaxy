# Guide: How to Produce a Cultural 1A Timeline Document for a Country

This guide explains how to create a high-quality document analysing shifts in a country’s observable sources of moral justification. It tracks the degree to which moral authority is derived from transcendent sources versus mundane/secular sources.

It is designed to produce consistent, well-structured, and analytically rigorous results aligned with the official **Cultural 1A – Foundation** guideline and the broader Empirical Political Slider System.

## 1. Preparation and Scope Definition

**Define the time period**
- Choose a clear starting point that marks a significant baseline (e.g., 1945 for many European countries, independence, or a major regime change).
- Decide whether the document will run to the present day.

**Define the analytical focus clearly**
- The document analyses the **observable sources** governments, parties, and public discourse invoke when justifying why positions or policies are morally right or necessary.
- The central question is: To what extent is moral authority derived from **transcendent sources** versus **mundane/secular sources**?

**Two Source Categories (core definitions)**
- **Transcendent**: Divine command, sacred texts, revelation, heavenly authority, Gaia/mother nature, intrinsic value of nature or future generations, inherent/universal rights that exist independently of current human consensus.
- **Mundane/Secular**: Evidence, science, history/tradition (as empirical inheritance), reason, debate, popular sovereignty, reciprocity among living citizens, pragmatic outcomes for the current population, national interest of living citizens.

**Strict scope boundaries**
Do **not** analyse:
- Enforcement, punishment, or coercion
- Actual policy outcomes or effectiveness
- Rhetoric vs reality gap
- Power dynamics or cultural dominance
- The *content* of specific moral rules (this belongs in other axes)
- Intensity of enforcement (this belongs in Cultural 1B – Dogmatism)

Focus only on the **observable sources invoked** in moral justification and argumentation (manifestos, values statements, policy documents, major speeches, legislative debates, institutional guidance).

## 2. Research and Data Collection

Gather evidence focused on **observable expressions of moral justification sources**. Prioritise primary sources only:

- Official party manifestos (most recent and historical)
- Core values / philosophical statements and party constitutions
- Key policy papers that explain *why* a policy is morally correct
- Major speeches by party leaders and heads of government on values or moral justification
- Legislative debates and court rulings that articulate moral foundations
- Education curricula and institutional guidance that state foundational moral claims

**What to look for:**
- How positions are justified: appeals to divine command, sacred authority, inherent rights, duties to future generations, intrinsic value of nature, universal moral principles (transcendent) versus evidence, science, popular sovereignty, tradition as empirical inheritance, reciprocity, pragmatic outcomes, national interest of living citizens (mundane).
- Patterns of presence, reduction, or absence of each source across domains.

**Tip**: Focus on sections where the party or government explains *why* something should be done, not just *what* should be done.

## 3. Segmenting History into Eras (High Granularity First)

**Critical rule:** Start by creating **more eras than you initially think are necessary**. It is far easier to merge similar eras later than to split an era that has become too broad.

**Good reasons to create a new era:**
- A clear shift in the dominant observable framework for moral justification (transcendent sources gain or lose primacy versus mundane sources)
- Major legislation, court rulings, institutional reforms, or public discourse that significantly expand or reduce the use of transcendent or mundane sources
- Periods where transcendent moral claims (duties to future generations, inherent rights, ecological justice) become more or less prominent across multiple domains
- Sustained changes in how governments and parties justify positions (e.g., popular sovereignty as ultimate authority, evidence-based pragmatism, or appeals to higher moral duties)
- Measurable changes in patterns of moral argumentation (frequency of transcendent vs mundane language in manifestos, speeches, and policy documents)

Aim for relatively short, coherent periods rather than long eras that contain multiple distinct phases.

## 4. Scoring Each Era

Use the official **Cultural 1A Absence Balance Scale (0–50)**.

**Step-by-step scoring:**
1. Determine which source is reduced or absent (Transcendent or Mundane).
2. Assess the **degree** of absence using totality judgment:
   - Breadth across domains (law, education, governance, social policy)
   - Consistency of omission
   - Primacy of the remaining source
3. Assign a band (0–50) based on the rubric descriptions.
4. Add the directional suffix:
   - **T** = Transcendent source is dominant (Mundane is reduced/absent)
   - **M** = Mundane source is dominant (Transcendent is reduced/absent)

**Practical Scoring Tips:**
- Real-world parties and eras rarely score above 48 or below 4.
- Look for patterns across multiple documents rather than isolated quotes.
- When both sources are clearly present and integrated, scores usually fall between 3–20.
- Score based on what parties and governments *actually write and say*, not what they are popularly associated with.

**Convert to Final Score**
After assigning the original score:

**Final Score = 50 + (Original Score × Multiplier)**

Where:
- T = × +1
- M = × −1

**Examples:**
- 36T → 50 + 36 = **86**
- 14M → 50 − 14 = **36**
- 47M → 50 − 47 = **3**

This conversion creates a directional scale where:
- Higher positive scores = stronger transcendent foundation
- Lower scores = stronger mundane foundation
- Scores near 50 = greater balance between both sources

## 5. Writing the Era Sections

Use a consistent structure for every era. Maintain similar depth and analytical quality across all eras.

### Recommended Structure

**Heading format (use exactly this style):**  
## **StartYear–EndYear: Era Name – Final Score: XX**

**Intro paragraph** (3 to 5 sentences)  
Summarise the period’s core identity and the overall character of moral justification.

**Subheading:**  
### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

- Bullet point 1
- Bullet point 2
- Bullet point 3
- Bullet point 4 (optional)

**Analysis paragraphs** (1–2 paragraphs)  
Explain the main sources actually invoked, supported by direct quotes or references where possible. Clearly distinguish between transcendent and mundane elements.

**Key Shift sentence** at the end of every era (this is mandatory). After the Key Shift sentence, add **one blank line**, followed by a single "---" horizontal rule break before the next era heading.

**Rules for Era Sections:**
- Keep eras reasonably concise but with sufficient analytical depth.
- Every single era **must** have a **Key Shift** sentence at the end.
- Immediately after the Key Shift sentence, insert **one blank line**, followed by a single "---" horizontal rule break before the next era heading.
- Focus on observable sources invoked in justification rather than outcomes or enforcement.
- Use 3–5 bullets maximum.
- Vary phrasing to avoid formulaic repetition across eras.

## 6. Refining the Eras

After completing a first draft:

- Review the full set of eras and ask:
  - Can any eras be merged without losing important nuance?
  - Does any era contain distinct phases that would be better separated?
  - Are there periods where content could be better redistributed between adjacent eras?
- Merge eras that show very similar dominant frameworks for moral justification sources.
- Keep eras that represent meaningful shifts in the balance between transcendent and mundane sources.
- Aim for the smallest number of eras that still preserves the important turning points.

## 7. Writing the Political Parties Section

Include a Political Parties section at the end of the document.  
**Parties must be arranged in strict alphabetical order** both in the summary table and in the individual subsection headings.

**Introductory Summary**  
Immediately after the party summary table, include a brief (3–5 sentence) analytical summary describing the broad state of current politics in the country with specific reference to the balance between transcendent and mundane sources of moral authority. This summary should bridge the historical eras section and the party analysis, highlighting the dominant patterns and positioning of major political actors. It should be written in the same empirical, non-moralising style as the rest of the document. This should be followed by a "---" break before the first individual political party subsection.

**Structure for each party:**
- Brief Overview (2–4 sentences)
- Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis (with bullets and quotes where possible)
- Why This Produces a Final Score of XX
  - Immediately beneath this heading, include a short explanatory paragraph (2–4 sentences) that justifies the assigned score band by direct reference to the observable patterns described in the Expanded Analysis above it.

Base this section on current party manifestos, values statements, and public justification patterns. Focus on what each party actually uses as sources of moral legitimacy.

## 8. Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

**Best Practices:**
- Always prioritise primary sources over secondary commentary.
- Score based on what parties and governments *actually write*, not what they are popularly associated with.
- Apply totality judgment — one strong quote does not override a consistent pattern.
- Keep sections relatively consistent in length and depth across eras and parties.
- Use direct quotes from party materials and government documents where possible.
- Stay strictly within the Cultural 1A scope.

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid:**
- Confusing Cultural 1A with enforcement or policy outcomes.
- Scoring based on a party’s historical reputation rather than current documents.
- Treating ceremonial or symbolic references as substantive moral sources.
- Assuming religious language automatically equals transcendent authority (it must be used as moral justification).
- Over-scoring balance when one source clearly carries more weight in justification.
- Allowing later eras to become thinner or more generic than earlier eras.

## 9. Final Assembly and Quality Control

**Include these elements in the final document:**
- Opening summary paragraph (no main title above it) — identify overall trajectory and main drivers of change in the balance between transcendent and mundane sources
- Era Summary Table (with Final Scores and short source-of-authority descriptions)
- All individual era sections (in chronological order), each ending with its Key Shift sentence, followed by **one blank line** and then a single "---" horizontal rule break
- Two consecutive "---" breaks immediately after the final era section and before the # Political Parties heading
- # Political Parties heading
- Political Parties intro paragraph (with bridging reference to the eras)
- Table of parties (parties listed in strict alphabetical order)
- All individual party sections (in strict alphabetical order), each with a short explanatory paragraph directly under the "Why This Produces a Final Score of XX" heading
- Optional: Final Score Conversion section at the bottom if the document is being used as a standalone reference

**Final Output Checklist**
Before finalising a country document, verify:

- [ ] All eras and parties scored using the official 0–50 rubric
- [ ] Directional suffix (T/M) correctly applied
- [ ] Final Scores correctly calculated
- [ ] Each era section follows the consistent structure and ends with a Key Shift sentence + "---"
- [ ] Analysis stays within Cultural 1A scope
- [ ] Quotes are taken from primary party documents and government sources
- [ ] Table of converted scores is included at the top
- [ ] Parties are in strict alphabetical order
- [ ] Every party has a short explanatory paragraph under its score justification
- [ ] Later eras receive the same analytical depth as earlier eras
- [ ] Repetitive phrasing has been minimised

## 10. Recommended Number of Eras

| Time Span       | Recommended Number of Eras | Guidance |
|-----------------|----------------------------|----------|
| 10–20 years     | 2–4                        | Avoid over-fragmentation |
| 25–40 years     | 4–6                        | Good balance |
| 40–60 years     | 6–9                        | Common for post-war Europe |
| 70–80+ years    | 10–14                      | As done with Britain |

## Summary Workflow

1. Define the time period and starting point.
2. Research observable sources of moral justification (primary sources only).
3. Create an initial high-granularity set of eras.
4. Score each era using the official Cultural 1A 0–50 scale + T/M suffix and convert to Final Score.
5. Write the era sections to a consistent standard (including Key Shift sentences followed by a single "---" break after each).
6. Review and refine the eras (merge where appropriate).
7. Write the Political Parties section, ensuring strict alphabetical order and a short explanatory paragraph under every "Why This Produces a Final Score of XX" heading.
8. Write the opening summary paragraph (make it analytical).
9. Insert two consecutive "---" breaks between the final era and the # Political Parties heading.
10. Review the full document against the quality standards and Final Output Checklist.
11. Edit for repetition, consistency of depth, and analytical clarity.

This process produces consistent, comparable, and defensible analysis across different countries and political systems while preserving the unique analytical requirements of Cultural 1A – Foundation.
