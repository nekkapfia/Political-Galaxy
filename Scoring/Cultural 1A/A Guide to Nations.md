# Guide: How to Produce a Cultural 1A Timeline Document for a Country

This guide explains how to create a document like the ones we produced for Britain and Germany. It covers researching, segmenting history, scoring eras, and writing in a consistent, high-quality style.

## 1. Preparation and Scope Definition

**Define the starting point**
- Choose a logical starting year (e.g., end of WWII for many European countries, or a major regime change).
- For Germany we started in 1998 (Schröder government). For Britain we started in 1945.

**Clarify the focus**
- Focus exclusively on **observable sources of moral justification** used by governments, political parties, and public discourse.
- Stay strictly within the Cultural 1A scope (do **not** analyse outcomes, enforcement, or “real intentions”).

**Decide on scope**
- Will you include a contemporary political parties section at the end? (Recommended — it provides useful context.)

## 2. Research and Data Collection

**Primary sources to gather:**
- Party manifestos and Grundsatzprogramme (foundational programmes)
- Major government policy documents and white papers
- Key speeches by heads of government and party leaders
- Parliamentary debates (especially on major reforms or crises)
- Historical analyses of political rhetoric and justification

**What to look for:**
- How governments and parties explain *why* a policy or position is right or necessary.
- Recurring themes: national interest, justice, responsibility to future generations, popular sovereignty, evidence, tradition, human dignity, etc.

## 3. Segmenting History into Eras

This is one of the most important steps.

**Principles for good segmentation:**
- Each era should have a relatively consistent character in terms of transcendent vs mundane sources.
- Look for clear shifts in how moral authority is constructed.
- Avoid making eras too short (unless there is a very sharp break).
- For a 30–40 year period, **4–6 eras** is usually ideal.
- For an 80+ year period, **8–12 eras** works well.

**Common patterns to look for:**
- Major ideological shifts (e.g. Thatcher 1979, Schröder 1998)
- Periods of crisis that change justification style
- Long periods of technocratic/pragmatic governance
- Times when transcendent rhetoric increases or decreases noticeably

## 4. Scoring Each Era

Use the official **Cultural 1A Absence Balance Scale (0–50)**.

**Steps:**
1. Determine which source (transcendent or mundane) is reduced or absent.
2. Assess the **degree** of absence using totality judgment (breadth, consistency, and primacy).
3. Assign an original score (0–50) + directional suffix (**T** or **M**).
4. Convert to Final Score: `Final Score = 50 + (Original Score × Multiplier)`

**Tips:**
- Be consistent in how you apply the rubric across eras.
- When in doubt, be slightly conservative with high scores (very few real-world cases reach 48–49).
- Document your reasoning clearly in the “Why This Produces a Final Score” section.

## 5. Writing the Era Sections

Use this consistent structure for every era:

### Recommended Structure

**Era X: Year–Year – Name**  
**(Final Score: XX)**

**Brief Overview** (3–5 sentences)  
Give context and the overall character of the period.

**Moral Justification Sources**  
Explain what sources were actually used, with specific examples and (where possible) quotes or references to key policies/speeches. Distinguish clearly between transcendent and mundane elements.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of XX**  
Explain your scoring decision with reference to the rubric. Be specific about which source is reduced and to what degree.

**Writing Tips:**
- Aim for similar length and depth across eras.
- Use specific examples rather than general statements.
- Vary your phrasing to avoid repetition.
- Be precise and evidence-based.

## 6. Writing the Political Parties Section

At the end of the document, include a section on current major political parties.

**Structure:**
- Table with Final Scores + short “Source of Moral Authority” summary.
- Individual party entries using the same format:
  - Brief Overview
  - Moral Justification Sources (Expanded Analysis)
  - Why This Produces a Final Score of XX

**Tips:**
- Base this section on current party manifestos and values statements.
- Keep the same level of analytical depth as the historical eras.

## 7. Final Assembly and Polish

**Add these elements:**
- Clear title
- Table of converted Final Scores at the top
- Short introduction (optional but recommended)
- Conclusion / Overall Trends section (highly recommended)

**Quality checklist:**
- Are all eras written to a similar depth?
- Is scoring reasoning clear and tied to the rubric?
- Is there unnecessary repetition in phrasing?
- Do the era sections flow logically from one to the next?
- Does the parties section match the quality of the era sections?

## 8. Recommended Number of Eras by Time Period

| Time Span       | Recommended Eras | Notes |
|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| 10–20 years     | 2–4              | Avoid over-fragmentation |
| 25–40 years     | 4–6              | Good balance |
| 40–60 years     | 6–9              | Common for post-war Europe |
| 70–80+ years    | 10–14            | As done with Britain |

## Summary Workflow

1. Define time period and starting point
2. Research primary sources
3. Segment history into coherent eras
4. Score each era on Cultural 1A
5. Write era sections using the standard structure
6. Write political parties section
7. Add introduction + conclusion
8. Review for consistency, depth, and repetition

This process produces consistent, comparable, and defensible analysis across different countries and political systems.
