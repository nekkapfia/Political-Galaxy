# Cultural 1A – Foundation: Guide to Analysing, Scoring, and Writing Political Party Sections

This guide provides a clear, repeatable process for analysing the political parties of any country on the **Cultural 1A – Foundation** axis. It covers how to gather evidence, identify moral sources, apply the scoring rubric, convert scores, and produce consistent written sections.

## 1. Understand the Scope (Strictly Enforced)

Cultural 1A measures **only** the observable sources parties invoke when justifying why their positions are morally right or necessary.

**What to measure:**
- Sources used in manifestos, values statements, policy documents, and public moral reasoning.
- How parties explain *why* something is good, just, necessary, or obligatory.

**What NOT to measure:**
- Enforcement, punishment, or coercion
- Actual policy outcomes or effectiveness
- Rhetoric vs reality gap
- Power dynamics or cultural dominance
- The *content* of specific moral rules (this belongs in other axes)
- Intensity of enforcement (this belongs in Cultural 1B – Dogmatism)

**Two Source Categories:**
- **Transcendent**: Divine command, sacred texts, revelation, heavenly authority, Gaia/mother nature, intrinsic value of nature or future generations, inherent/universal rights that exist independently of current human consensus.
- **Mundane/Secular**: Evidence, science, history/tradition (as empirical inheritance), reason, debate, popular sovereignty, reciprocity among living citizens, pragmatic outcomes for the current population.

## 2. Data Collection

Gather primary sources only:
- Official party manifestos (most recent general election)
- Core values / philosophical statements
- Party constitutions or foundational documents
- Key policy papers that explain *why* a policy is morally correct
- Major speeches by party leaders on values or moral justification

**Tip**: Focus on sections where the party explains *why* something should be done, not just *what* should be done.

## 3. Analysis Process

For each party, ask:

1. What sources does the party actually cite when justifying moral positions?
2. Is one source dominant, secondary, or absent?
3. In which domains is each source used or omitted?
4. Does the party treat any source as capable of overriding current public opinion or empirical convenience?

**Key Questions to Answer:**
- Does the party appeal to duties to future generations or non-human entities that can override present interests?
- Does it ground rights in inherent/universal principles or in democratic will and reciprocity?
- Does it justify tradition as sacred/revelatory or as empirically successful inheritance?
- Does it treat popular sovereignty as the ultimate source of legitimacy?

## 4. Scoring Methodology

Use the official **Cultural 1A Absence Balance Scale** (0–50).

**Step-by-step scoring:**

1. Determine which source is reduced or absent (Transcendent or Mundane).
2. Assess the **degree** of absence using totality judgment:
   - Breadth across domains (law, education, governance, social policy)
   - Consistency of omission
   - Primacy of the remaining source
3. Assign a band (0–50) based on the rubric descriptions.
4. Add the directional suffix:
   - **T** = Transcendent source is dominant (Mundane is reduced/absent)
   - **M** = Mundane source is dominant (Transcendent is reduced/absent)

**Practical Scoring Tips:**
- Real-world parties rarely score above 48 or below 4.
- Look for patterns across multiple documents rather than isolated quotes.
- When both sources are clearly present and integrated, scores usually fall between 3–20.

## 5. Convert to Final Score

After assigning the original score:

**Final Score = 50 + (Original Score × Multiplier)**

Where:
- T = × +1
- M = × −1

**Examples:**
- 36T → 50 + 36 = **86**
- 14M → 50 − 14 = **36**
- 47M → 50 − 47 = **3**

This conversion creates a directional scale where:
- Higher positive scores = stronger transcendent foundation
- Lower scores = stronger mundane foundation
- Scores near 50 = greater balance between both sources

## 6. Writing the Party Section

Use a consistent structure for every party:

### Recommended Structure

**Party Name – Final Score: XX**

**Brief Overview** (2–4 sentences)  
Summarise the party’s core identity and positioning.

**Moral Justification Sources**  
Explain the main sources the party actually invokes, supported by direct quotes where possible. Clearly distinguish between transcendent and mundane elements.

**Why This Produces a Final Score of XX**  
Explain the scoring decision using the rubric language. Reference:
- Which source is reduced/absent
- The degree of absence
- The band it falls into
- Key patterns across documents

**Tone Guidelines:**
- Be precise and evidence-based.
- Use direct quotes from party materials.
- Avoid speculation about “true intentions” or outcomes.
- Stay strictly within the Cultural 1A scope.

## 7. Best Practices and Common Pitfalls

**Best Practices:**
- Always prioritise primary sources over secondary commentary.
- Score based on what parties *actually write*, not what they are popularly associated with.
- Apply totality judgment — one strong quote does not override a consistent pattern.
- Keep sections relatively consistent in length and depth across parties.

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid:**
- Confusing Cultural 1A with enforcement or policy outcomes.
- Scoring based on a party’s historical reputation rather than current documents.
- Treating ceremonial or symbolic references as substantive moral sources.
- Assuming religious language automatically equals transcendent authority (it must be used as moral justification).
- Over-scoring balance when one source clearly carries more weight in justification.

## 8. Final Output Checklist

Before finalising a country document, verify:

- [ ] All parties scored using the official 0–50 rubric
- [ ] Directional suffix (T/M) correctly applied
- [ ] Final Scores correctly calculated
- [ ] Each party section follows the consistent structure
- [ ] Analysis stays within Cultural 1A scope
- [ ] Quotes are taken from primary party documents
- [ ] Table of converted scores is included at the top

This process produces consistent, comparable, and defensible analysis across different countries and political systems.
