# Guide: Defining Eras, Calculating Scores, and Structuring the Document for Cultural 1A (Britain)

This guide outlines the methodology developed for analysing and documenting eras on the Cultural 1A slider for Britain.

## 1. How to Work Out the Era Breaks

### Core Principles

- Start from a clear baseline (e.g. the post-war high transcendent period).
- Focus on **observable change** in the origin of moral grammar rather than political rhetoric.
- Use the **Rollercoaster / Gradient Model**: Identify breaks by looking for changes in the **rate or direction** of movement.
- Distinguish clearly between two types of eras:

| Era Type     | Definition                                      | Characteristics                     | Internal Structure                     |
|--------------|--------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Stable**   | Period with little to no meaningful directional movement | Flat or minor fluctuation only     | Single unified block                   |
| **Transition** | Period of clear, sustained movement toward one of the poles | Noticeable change in score         | Split into **Cultural** then **Legal** phases |

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Establish a starting baseline**  
   Begin from the end of the previous stable or locked period.

2. **Identify clusters of formal/legal change**  
   These usually mark the **Legal Shift Phase** of a Transition era.

3. **Work backwards to identify the Cultural Shift Phase**  
   Cultural change almost always precedes and enables legal change.

4. **Apply the Gradient Test**
   - Sharp, concentrated movement → Likely a **Transition** era.
   - Very slow or flat movement → Likely a **Stable** era.
   - Clear change in the *rate* of movement → Consider a break point.

5. **Look for natural breakpoints**
   - End of major legislative clusters
   - Significant shifts in the political environment
   - Noticeable slowing or acceleration of cultural change

6. **Avoid over-fragmentation**
   - Only create a new era when there is a **meaningful change in gradient**.
   - Minor variations in speed within a slow transition do not usually justify splitting.

## 2. How to Calculate Era Scores

### Core Rules

- All scores must be **whole numbers**.
- Score each of the six domains of Cultural 1A:
  1. Law
  2. Education Curricula
  3. Media Narratives
  4. Public Rituals
  5. Institutional Governance
  6. Daily Cultural Reflexes

- Combine **domain-level scoring** with **holistic assessment**.

### Process

1. Score each domain at the **end** of the era (and at the start for Transition eras).
2. For Transition eras, calculate:
   - **Change over Era** = Current Era End Score − Previous Era End Score
3. Apply holistic judgment:
   - What was the overall character of the era?
   - Was movement consistent across domains?
   - What was the predominant pattern?
4. Final scoring rules:
   - **Stable eras**: Score remains largely constant.
   - **Transition eras**: Use the **final score** of the era.

## 3. How to Structure the Document (Era Sections)

### Recommended Layout

Use the following consistent structure:

1. **Master Era Overview Table** (placed at the very top)
2. **For each individual era**, follow this order:

   - Era Header (with Type and Period)
   - **Overall Character** (1–2 sentences)
   - **Domain Overview Table** (placed near the top)
   - **Cultural Shift Phase** + **Legal Shift Phase** (Transition eras only)
   - **Key Drivers** (bullet points)
   - **Summary** (longer analytical section)

### Table Formats

**Stable Eras:**
| Domain | Score | Domain Summary |

**Transition Eras:**
| Domain | Score at end | Change over Era | Domain Summary |

### Domain Summary Guidelines

- Keep entries concise but informative (1–2 sentences).
- Focus on what actually changed or how the domain behaved during the era.
- Move deeper analysis into the main **Summary** section (especially for Stable eras).

### Summary Section Guidelines

- **Stable eras**: Aim for **two paragraphs**:
  - Paragraph 1: Overall state and main characteristics.
  - Paragraph 2: Analytical reflection on the nature of the stability.

- **Transition eras**: Do **not** write one combined summary. Write separate descriptions for the **Cultural Shift Phase** and **Legal Shift Phase**.

## Summary of Best Practices

| Element                    | Recommendation                                              | Reason |
|---------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------|--------|
| Era Breaks                | Use gradient/rate of change + clusters of formal reform    | Prevents arbitrary or overly fragmented splits |
| Transition Structure      | Always split into Cultural Phase → Legal Phase             | Reflects real-world causal sequence |
| Scoring                   | Combine domain scores with holistic assessment             | More robust than using one method alone |
| Tables                    | Place near the top of each era                             | Improves readability and scannability |
| Stable Era Summaries      | Two paragraphs with slightly expanded Domain Summaries     | Prevents Stable eras from feeling thin |
| Master Table              | Use Score range (X-Y) for Transition eras                  | Gives immediate visual overview of movement |
| Repetition                | Minimise overlap between Character / Key Drivers / Phases  | Keeps the document clean and professional |

---

This methodology produces consistent, analytically rigorous, and readable era sections while remaining faithful to the empirical principles of the Cultural 1A slider.
