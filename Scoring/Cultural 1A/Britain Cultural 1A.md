## Era 1 (Stable): Post-War Christian Baseline: 1945–1958
**Era Score: 85**

**Overall Character**  
A high transcendent baseline in which Christian-derived moral grammar remained dominant across law, education, public rituals, and daily life.

**Domain Overview**

| Domain                    | Score | Domain Summary |
|---------------------------|-------|----------------|
| **Law**                   | 85    | Strongly rooted in transcendent sources, with fault-based divorce, criminalisation of male homosexuality, and active blasphemy laws |
| **Education Curricula**   | 90    | Mandatory religious instruction and daily collective worship dominant across schools |
| **Media Narratives**      | 78    | Predominantly Christian moral framing of national identity and social order |
| **Public Rituals**        | 80    | Religious elements central to state occasions and civic ceremonies |
| **Institutional Governance** | 82 | Significant church influence in family policy, education, and welfare |
| **Daily Cultural Reflexes** | 75 | Traditional Christian-derived expectations around family, duty, and conduct |

**Summary**  
This period represents the high transcendent baseline of the early post-war era. Moral grammar remained strongly anchored in Christian-derived sources across all domains with only minor gradual softening. While some pragmatic secular influences existed in welfare administration and governance, they remained secondary and did not challenge the dominant religious framework.

The era was characterised by strong cultural and institutional continuity following the Second World War. Christian moral assumptions continued to shape both formal institutions and everyday social expectations, creating a relatively cohesive moral environment.

## Era 2 (Transition): First Wave of Liberalisation: 1958–1972
**Final Score: 57**

**Overall Character**  
A sharp and sustained transition from a high transcendent baseline to a significantly more secular moral framework, driven first by cultural change and then by rapid legal reform.

**Domain Overview**

| Domain                    | Score at end | Change over Era | Domain Summary |
|---------------------------|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Law**                   | 55           | **−30**         | Major legislative reforms moved family and personal conduct law away from transcendent sources. The Sexual Offences Act 1967, Abortion Act 1967, and Divorce Reform Act 1969 decriminalised certain sexual acts, legalised abortion, and introduced no-fault divorce principles. |
| **Education Curricula**   | 68           | **−22**         | Religious instruction remained compulsory, but expanding secular educational priorities and curriculum reform began to reduce its previous dominance in shaping moral formation. |
| **Media Narratives**      | 60           | **−17**         | Media coverage of social and moral issues became noticeably more secular, with greater emphasis on individual rights and criticism of traditional religious authority. |
| **Public Rituals**        | 68           | **−12**         | Religious elements in national ceremonies remained visible but began losing some of their former cultural centrality and moral authority, becoming more symbolic than prescriptive. |
| **Institutional Governance** | 58        | **−24**         | Policy-making increasingly incorporated secular and utilitarian justifications, particularly in areas of personal conduct and family policy. |
| **Daily Cultural Reflexes** | 57         | **−17**         | Social expectations around sexuality, family structure, and personal morality began shifting noticeably away from traditional Christian norms toward more individualised and secular values. |

**Cultural Shift Phase (1958–1966)**  
This phase marked the beginning of sustained cultural liberalisation. Changing attitudes toward authority, sexuality, and family life, combined with the rise of youth culture and more secular media framing, gradually weakened the previously dominant transcendent moral grammar. While formal institutions such as law and education remained relatively resistant, everyday cultural reflexes and public discourse began moving noticeably toward more immanent sources.

**Legal Shift Phase (1967–1972)**  
This phase saw an unusually rapid and concentrated wave of legislative reform. The Sexual Offences Act 1967, Abortion Act 1967, and Divorce Reform Act 1969 directly altered the source of moral authority in law, moving family structure and personal conduct toward secular frameworks. These legal changes formalised and accelerated the cultural shifts of the preceding years.

**Key Drivers**  
- Gradual cultural liberalisation in the late 1950s and early 1960s  
- Rapid legislative reform between 1967 and 1969

## Era 3 (Stable): Post-Liberalisation Plateau: 1973–1990
**Era Score: 57**

**Overall Character**  
A long period of relative stability in which the origin of moral grammar remained at the new lower level established in Era 2, with only very gradual further movement.

**Domain Overview**

| Domain                    | Score | Domain Summary |
|---------------------------|-------|----------------|
| **Law**                   | 57    | The major reforms of the late 1960s were consolidated. There was limited further legislative movement in core moral grammar areas, although some equality measures were introduced in the 1970s. |
| **Education Curricula**   | 70    | Religious education remained compulsory but faced increasing competition from secular educational priorities and broader curriculum development. |
| **Media Narratives**      | 65    | Media continued a gradual shift toward secular perspectives on social and moral issues, although traditional viewpoints remained present in public discourse. |
| **Public Rituals**        | 68    | Religious elements in national and civic life persisted but continued a slow decline in cultural centrality and moral authority, becoming increasingly symbolic rather than directive. |
| **Institutional Governance** | 62 | Political resistance to further liberalisation became more visible from the late 1970s onward, though it had limited success in reversing earlier changes. |
| **Daily Cultural Reflexes** | 68 | Social expectations around family and personal conduct continued a slow liberalisation, though at a much reduced pace compared to the late 1960s. |

**Summary**  
This long period saw the system settle into a new, lower baseline following the intense reforms of Era 2. The origin of moral grammar remained relatively stable, with only gradual further movement. Political resistance to secularisation became more visible, particularly during the Thatcher years, but was not strong enough to significantly alter the overall score or reverse the direction of travel.

The era was characterised by consolidation rather than major directional change. While cultural and media shifts continued at a slow pace, formal institutions largely maintained the framework established in the late 1960s.

## Era 4 (Transition): Second Wave of Secularisation: 1990–2006
**Final Score: 46**

**Overall Character**  
A long, gradual transition in which cultural liberalisation continued steadily before being formalised and accelerated through legislative and institutional change.

**Domain Overview**

| Domain                    | Score at end | Change over Era | Domain Summary |
|---------------------------|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Law**                   | 46           | **−11**         | Further embedding of secular and rights-based frameworks through equality legislation and relationship recognition. The Civil Partnership Act 2004 and related equality laws introduced legal recognition of same-sex relationships and strengthened anti-discrimination protections. |
| **Education Curricula**   | 55           | **−15**         | Gradual shift toward multi-faith and inclusive approaches, with a declining emphasis on Christian primacy in moral education. |
| **Media Narratives**      | 48           | **−17**         | Continued strengthening of secular framing of moral and social issues, with increasing focus on individual rights and diversity. |
| **Public Rituals**        | 52           | **−16**         | Religious elements remained present in some national ceremonies but were largely ceremonial rather than carrying substantive moral authority in public life. Their role had shifted from actively reinforcing transcendent moral norms to serving more as cultural tradition and heritage. |
| **Institutional Governance** | 48        | **−14**         | Stronger embedding of secular rights-based reasoning in policy-making and institutional decision-making, particularly through human rights and equality frameworks. |
| **Daily Cultural Reflexes** | 47         | **−21**         | Continued liberalisation of social expectations around family, sexuality, and personal relationships, becoming more normalised across society. |

**Cultural Shift Phase (1990–2000)**  
This phase was marked by the steady continuation of cultural liberalisation. Public attitudes toward family, sexuality, and personal morality moved further away from traditional transcendent sources. Media narratives and daily cultural reflexes became increasingly secular as political resistance from the previous era lost momentum.

**Legal Shift Phase (2000–2006)**  
This phase saw the formal codification of earlier cultural changes through significant legislative and institutional reform. Key developments, including the Civil Partnership Act 2004 and broader equality legislation, embedded secular and rights-based frameworks more deeply into law and governance.

**Key Drivers**  
- Continued cultural liberalisation throughout the 1990s  
- Major legislative codification in the early-to-mid 2000s (especially Civil Partnership Act 2004 and equality legislation)

## Era 5 (Stable): Contemporary Secular Stabilisation: 2007–2026
**Era Score: 46**

**Overall Character**  
A long period of stability in which the secular moral framework established by the end of Era 4 became consolidated and normalised across British public and institutional life.

**Domain Overview**

| Domain                    | Score | Domain Summary |
|---------------------------|-------|----------------|
| **Law**                   | 46    | Secular frameworks were firmly established across most areas of personal and family law. This was expressed through no-fault divorce principles, legal recognition of same-sex relationships (via civil partnerships and later same-sex marriage), and comprehensive equality and anti-discrimination legislation that prioritised individual rights over religious moral norms. |
| **Education Curricula**   | 50    | Multi-faith and inclusive approaches became dominant. Religious education shifted away from functioning primarily as a vehicle for transmitting Christian moral values toward a broader, more comparative study of religions and ethics. |
| **Media Narratives**      | 47    | Strongly secular framing of moral and social issues became the norm across most mainstream outlets, with emphasis on individual rights, diversity, and personal autonomy rather than traditional religious authority. |
| **Public Rituals**        | 52    | Religious elements remained present in some national ceremonies but were largely ceremonial rather than carrying substantive moral authority in public life. Their role had shifted from actively reinforcing transcendent moral norms to serving more as cultural tradition and heritage. |
| **Institutional Governance** | 46 | Secular rights-based frameworks became dominant in policy-making and institutional decision-making. This was expressed through human rights legislation, equality duties on public bodies, and the reduced role of religious considerations in areas such as family policy and education governance. |
| **Daily Cultural Reflexes** | 48 | Strongly secular expectations around family, relationships, and personal conduct became normalised. Traditional Christian-derived norms around marriage, sexuality, and family structure were largely replaced by individual choice and rights-based understandings. |

**Summary**  
This long period represents the consolidation of the current secular baseline. The origin of moral grammar remained stable at this lower level across all domains. While cultural and political debates continued (particularly around gender, education, and the role of faith in public life), there was no major new directional shift in the observable source of moral norms.

The era was characterised by the normalisation and embedding of secular frameworks that had been established in previous periods. Legal and institutional changes from the early 2000s became fully integrated into everyday life and governance.
