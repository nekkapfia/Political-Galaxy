# Guide to Scoring Political Parties on Cultural 1A

## 1. Core Definition

This slider measures the **proportion of moral legitimacy** derived from transcendent sources compared to the proportion derived from secular logic and reasoning.

It does **not** assess:
- The moral content of a policy
- Whether a policy is right or wrong
- The consequences or outcomes of a policy

It measures **only** the basis on which moral authority is claimed.

---

## 2. Question Structure (5 Pairs)

Questions are organised as **5 Pairs (A and B)**. Each pair examines a different dimension of how a party derives and defends moral legitimacy.

| Pair | A (First Question) | B (Second Question) | Purpose of the Pair |
|------|--------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Pair 1** | Uses secular reasoning as justification? | Uses transcendent source as justification? | Primary source of moral legitimacy |
| **Pair 2** | Tolerates secular counter-arguments? | Rejects secular counter-arguments using transcendent arguments? | Handling of secular opposition |
| **Pair 3** | Tolerates transcendent counter-arguments? | Rejects transcendent counter-arguments using secular reasoning? | Handling of transcendent opposition |
| **Pair 4** | Defends/accommodates transcendent element using secular reasoning? | Defends/accommodates secular element using transcendent source? | Framing of accommodations |
| **Pair 5** | Has carve-outs justified by transcendent/religious reasons? | Has carve-outs justified by secular reasons? | Nature of exemptions and special treatment |

---

## 3. Data Collection Rules

- Select **6–8 policies** that are prominent and representative of the party’s platform.
- Focus on policies that touch on moral, cultural, or value-based questions (e.g. gender, family, religion in public life, education, immigration, equality).
- For each policy, answer every question with a clear **Yes** or **No**.
- Default rule: If genuinely uncertain, answer **No** for the more assertive claim in the pair.

---

## 4. Scoring Protocol (Parity Swap Method)

Follow this process strictly and in order:

### Step 1: Score the Correct Table
Score the table using the answers exactly as provided.

### Step 2: Apply the Parity Rule

- **If the initial score is below 50** (bottom half):
  - Report this score as the final score.
  - Do **not** perform a parity swap.

- **If the initial score is above 50** (top half):
  - Perform a **parity swap** (swap all A and B answers within each pair across the entire table).
  - Score the parity-swapped table.
  - The final reported score = **100 − (Parity Swapped Score)**.

This rule ensures that mirrored tables produce consistent, inverse results and removes scorer discretion.

---

## 5. Weighting of Pairs (for Consistency)

Not all pairs carry equal weight when determining the final score:

| Pair | Relative Weight | Reason |
|------|------------------|--------|
| **Pair 1** | Very High | Directly measures the primary source of moral legitimacy |
| **Pair 2** | High | Shows how the party responds to secular challenges |
| **Pair 3** | Medium-High | Shows openness to transcendent challenges |
| **Pair 4** | Medium | Reveals preferred framing of accommodations |
| **Pair 5** | Medium-Low | Important but usually less decisive than justification and tolerance |

When patterns conflict between pairs, give greater weight to **Pair 1** and **Pair 2**.

---

## 6. Handling Ambiguous or Mixed Answers

- If a policy shows mixed signals within a pair, default to the answer that reflects the **more dominant pattern** across the party’s other policies.
- If the entire table is borderline, score it conservatively (closer to the centre of the band) rather than pushing toward an extreme.
- Document any borderline decisions in your notes.

---

## 7. Writing the Party Section

Use this consistent structure:

**Party Name**  
**Score: XX**

**Key Patterns**
- Bullet points (4–6) summarising the dominant signals from the 5 pairs.

**Narrative Summary**
- 4–6 sentences explaining the overall balance of moral legitimacy.
- Mention the role of carve-outs and how the party handles opposition.
- Avoid referencing specific question numbers.

**Example Structure**
- Strong and consistent use of [secular/transcendent] reasoning for policy justification.
- Creates carve-outs justified by [secular/transcendent] reasons.
- Generally [tolerant/hostile] toward [secular/transcendent] counter-arguments.
- Overall moral legitimacy is primarily derived from [secular/transcendent] sources.

---

## 8. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

- Do not score based on whether you agree with the party’s policies.
- Do not assume that defending “heritage” or “tradition” automatically counts as transcendent justification.
- Avoid letting one very strong policy overly influence the overall score.
- Do not manually adjust scores to create symmetry — use the parity swap protocol instead.
- Do not treat all pairs as equal in importance (see Section 5).

---

## 9. Documentation Standards

For every party scored, record:

- The full Yes/No table (with Pair A/B columns)
- The initial score before any parity swap
- Whether a parity swap was applied and why
- The final reported score and band
- Any borderline decisions and the reasoning used

This allows other scorers to replicate or review the work.
