# Cultural 2B: Xenophobia vs Xenophilia

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

## Cultural 2B Definition

This guideline measures the observable permeability of the boundary between “us” and “not us” — the degree of difficulty, cost, cultural resistance, and institutional friction involved in crossing into the in-group (through immigration, naturalisation, assimilation, intermarriage, or cultural adoption) versus the ease and openness of boundary dissolution. 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in immigration law, border control, citizenship requirements, assimilation policies, media narratives, public rituals, education curricula, and statistical outcomes of integration success/failure) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Extremely high permeability costs and cultural/institutional resistance to crossing the “us” boundary. This allows observable variation within different closed systems (different mechanisms of boundary defence while retaining total rejection of easy entry).

- **100** = Absolute closure: crossing the boundary is effectively impossible; maximum legal, cultural, and social friction against any outsider integration.
- **95–99** = Near-absolute closure: only the most exceptional, lengthy, and rigorously vetted individuals are permitted entry with near-total assimilation demands.
- **90–94** = Extreme boundary defence: very high barriers in law, language, culture, and daily practice with strong enforcement against dilution.

### 80–89 (High – Strict boundary maintenance with minimal entry)
- Immigration is tightly restricted with rigorous vetting, long residency requirements, and mandatory deep cultural assimilation.
- Citizenship is granted only after proven integration (language, values, loyalty tests).
- Intermarriage and cultural adoption face strong social and institutional discouragement.
- Media and education reinforce clear “us vs them” distinctions and the value of preserving group distinctiveness.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Predominantly closed with limited managed entry)
- Controlled immigration exists but with high barriers, selective criteria, and strong assimilation pressure.
- Naturalisation requires demonstrable cultural adoption and loyalty.
- Public rituals and education emphasise in-group cohesion and wariness of outsiders.
- Statistical outcomes show low rates of successful integration and high boundary maintenance.

### 60–69 (Mid – Moderate permeability with noticeable friction)
- Immigration and naturalisation are possible but involve significant costs, delays, and assimilation requirements.
- Some group mixing occurs, yet clear cultural and institutional preferences for in-group remain.
- Media narratives balance openness rhetoric with observable defence of group boundaries.
- Integration outcomes show moderate success rates alongside persistent friction.

### 51–59 (Mid–Lower – Noticeable increase in permeability)
- Barriers to entry decrease; immigration volumes rise with reduced vetting and assimilation demands.
- Citizenship becomes more accessible with shorter residency periods and lighter cultural requirements.
- Education and media increasingly frame boundary crossing as positive or inevitable.
- Observable softening of “us vs them” distinctions in public discourse and daily practice.

### 41–50 (Low–Mid – Multiple significant pathways across the boundary)
- Multiple legal and cultural routes facilitate entry and integration (easier visas, family reunification, reduced assimilation tests).
- Group mixing accelerates in education, employment, and family formation.
- Public rituals and media promote diversity and boundary dissolution as virtues.
- Statistical outcomes reflect higher rates of demographic and cultural change.

### 31–40 (Low – High number and accessibility of boundary crossing)
- Permeability is high; immigration and cultural adoption face minimal institutional or social resistance.
- Assimilation is de-emphasised or discouraged in favour of multiculturalism.
- Education curricula and media actively celebrate the erosion of traditional “us” boundaries.
- Daily cultural reflexes treat in-group preservation as problematic or outdated.

### 21–30 (Very Low – Very high permeability and low friction)
- Borders and cultural boundaries are functionally open with little effective resistance.
- Group identity dissolves rapidly through high volumes of entry and minimal assimilation pressure.
- Institutional and cultural mechanisms actively facilitate boundary blurring or dissolution.

### 11–20 (Extreme Low – Near-total openness)
- Almost no observable friction remains for crossing into the in-group.
- National or cultural identity becomes largely symbolic or optional.
- Policies and daily norms treat any defence of boundaries as illegitimate.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute openness / boundary dissolution) — Compressed zone
Complete dissolution of the “us” vs “not us” boundary with zero institutional or cultural resistance to entry or mixing. This allows observable variation within different universalist systems (different mechanisms of openness while retaining total rejection of group boundaries).

- **6–10** = Extreme openness: any remaining boundary defence is actively pathologised and sanctioned.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute openness: the original in-group identity is effectively erased through policy and culture.
- **0** = Absolute openness: no distinguishable “us” remains; every individual is treated as interchangeable with zero boundary enforcement.

**End of Cultural 2B: Xenophobia**
