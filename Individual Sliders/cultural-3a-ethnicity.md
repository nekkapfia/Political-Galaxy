# Cultural 3A: Ethnicity

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

## Cultural 3A Definition

This guideline measures the observable basis on which a culture defines its core in-group (“us”) — the degree to which group membership and belonging are determined primarily by shared ethnicity, ancestry, and descent versus primarily by shared ideas, values, behaviours, citizenship, or voluntary adoption. 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in immigration law, citizenship policy, education curricula, family and inheritance rules, media narratives, public rituals, institutional preferences, and statistical outcomes of group continuity or dissolution) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Absolute or near-absolute definition of the in-group by shared ethnicity, ancestry, and descent. This allows observable variation within different ethnic-based systems (different mechanisms of ethnic preference and continuity while retaining total rejection of purely civic or ideological belonging).

- **100** = Pure ethnic foundation: every core aspect of belonging, citizenship, and group continuity is determined exclusively by blood, ancestry, and descent with no observable civic or ideological alternatives permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-pure ethnic foundation: ethnicity overwhelmingly determines group membership; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined non-ethnic pathways exist.
- **90–94** = Strong ethnic foundation: ethnic criteria dominate law, education, immigration, and institutions with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict ethnic definition with minimal non-ethnic entry)
- Citizenship and immigration policy strongly prioritises ethnic ancestry, language tied to ethnic heritage, and cultural descent.
- Education curricula and public rituals emphasise ethnic continuity, shared bloodlines, and ancestral heritage.
- Family and inheritance law reinforce ethnic transmission of identity and property.
- Media and institutional practices visibly favour ethnic in-group members in symbolic and practical matters.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Predominantly ethnic with limited non-ethnic accommodation)
- Core institutions maintain ethnic criteria as primary but allow narrow, revocable non-ethnic pathways in secondary areas.
- Ethnic continuity remains culturally reinforced and statistically dominant.
- Public discourse treats ethnic belonging as natural and beneficial rather than problematic.
- Assimilation of outsiders is possible but requires deep cultural and often genetic-adjacent conformity.

### 60–69 (Mid – Balanced or contested ethnic vs civic definition)
- Mixed observable patterns: ethnic criteria dominate in immigration and family law while civic/values-based criteria appear in education or employment.
- Enforcement of ethnic preferences exists but faces measurable pushback or selective exemptions.
- Media narratives show both defence of ethnic continuity and promotion of civic openness without clear supremacy of either.
- Statistical outcomes reflect moderate ethnic continuity alongside growing non-ethnic integration.

### 51–59 (Mid–Lower – Noticeable shift toward civic/ideological definition)
- Civic, behavioural, or values-based criteria gain observable traction in multiple domains (e.g., easier naturalisation based on language/loyalty rather than ancestry).
- Ethnic preferences are still present but increasingly reframed as suspect or temporary.
- Education and media begin to emphasise shared ideas over shared blood.
- Family and inheritance rules start to de-emphasise ethnic transmission.

### 41–50 (Low–Mid – Multiple significant civic mechanisms)
- Civic or ideological belonging becomes dominant in several core domains (citizenship, education, public institutions).
- Ethnic ancestry is tolerated only where unavoidable and is actively reframed as outdated.
- Public rituals and media increasingly celebrate multicultural or idea-based identity over ethnic continuity.
- Statistical outcomes show accelerating dilution of ethnic distinctiveness.

### 31–40 (Low – Strong enforcement of civic/ideological primacy)
- Group belonging is defined primarily by shared values, citizenship, or voluntary adoption across most domains.
- Ethnic criteria are dismantled or heavily constrained; ancestry-based preferences are discouraged or inverted.
- Education, law, and institutions prioritise universalist or ideological belonging over ethnic descent.
- Cultural reflexes treat ethnic boundary maintenance as inherently illegitimate or harmful.

### 21–30 (Very Low – Near-total rejection of ethnic definition)
- Ethnic ancestry has negligible observable role in determining group membership.
- All major institutions and daily norms treat identity as fully fluid, civic, or ideological.
- Any remaining ethnic references are treated as historical artifacts with no binding force.

### 11–20 (Extreme Low – Active dissolution of ethnic boundaries)
- Policies and culture actively work to erase ethnic distinctions through high openness and enforced mixing.
- Ethnic self-identification is pathologised or minimised in law and education.
- Institutional governance treats ethnic continuity as an obstacle to progress.

### 0–10 (Floor – Pure civic/universalist foundation)
- **6–10** = Strong civic/universalist foundation: ethnic or ancestral criteria are absent or explicitly rejected across all observable domains.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute civic/universalist foundation: group belonging is determined solely by ideas, voluntary adoption, or legal status with only marginal residual ethnic references.
- **0** = Pure civic/universalist foundation: zero ethnic or ancestral basis remains for defining the in-group; every individual is treated as interchangeable based solely on ideas, behaviour, or legal membership with total rejection of descent-based belonging.
