# Cultural 1B: Dogmatism

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

## Cultural 1B Definition

This guideline measures the observable intensity with which a system enforces its moral or ideological foundation against dissent, deviation, or alternative frameworks. It tracks the degree of suppression mechanisms (legal penalties, institutional exclusion, social sanctions, educational indoctrination, media control) rather than the content of the belief itself. 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, education curricula, employment practices, media narratives, institutional governance, public rituals, and daily cultural reflexes) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Absolute or near-absolute enforcement of a single moral or ideological orthodoxy across all domains, with zero tolerance for deviation or pluralism. This allows observable variation within different dogmatic systems (different mechanisms of suppression while retaining total rejection of dissent).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal enforcement of orthodoxy in every domain with no deviation or alternative frameworks permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total dogmatism; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined exceptions permitted.
- **90–94** = Extreme dogmatism legitimation: orthodoxy dominates law, education, employment, and institutions with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict and consistent enforcement of orthodoxy)
- Criminal or civil penalties exist for blasphemy, hate speech, or ideological deviation.
- Education curricula and employment require explicit loyalty statements or ideological conformity tests.
- Media and public institutions actively suppress dissenting views through deplatforming or cancellation mechanisms.
- Institutional governance enforces purity standards for promotion and leadership positions.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Predominant enforcement with limited exceptions)
- Core domains maintain strong orthodoxy enforcement but allow narrow, revocable exceptions in secondary areas.
- Dissent is heavily socially discouraged and carries professional or institutional costs.
- Education and media promote the official framework while marginalising alternatives.
- Cancel culture or social sanctions are frequent and effective against prominent deviation.

### 51–69 (Mid-Range – Noticeable but inconsistent dogmatism)
- Enforcement varies by domain or issue; selective intensity is observable.
- Dogmatic responses to specific threats to the moral grammar (e.g., blasphemy or hate speech laws carrying criminal penalties, mandatory diversity or loyalty statements in employment/universities, or institutional purity tests for promotion).
- Cancel culture mechanisms and social media pile-ons are frequent and result in professional or social consequences.
- Mixed curricula and media: core orthodoxy is actively promoted while limited pluralism exists in non-core areas.

### 31–50 (Mid–Low – Noticeable shift toward tolerance of deviation)
- Orthodoxy enforcement weakens in multiple domains; dissenting views gain observable space.
- Legal penalties for ideological deviation are reduced or selectively applied.
- Education and employment increasingly allow alternative frameworks without formal sanctions.
- Public discourse shows growing acceptance of pluralism even on previously sacred issues.

### 11–30 (Low – Strong enforcement of pluralism and anti-dogmatism)
- Active legal and institutional protections for dissenting views and alternative moral frameworks.
- Speech codes or loyalty tests are dismantled or heavily constrained.
- Education and media promote critical thinking and viewpoint diversity as virtues.
- Cultural reflexes treat dogmatic enforcement as inherently illegitimate or harmful.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute anti-dogmatism / pluralism) — Compressed zone
Total rejection of all orthodoxy enforcement with universal protection of dissent and alternative frameworks. This allows observable variation within different pluralist systems (different mechanisms of openness while retaining zero tolerance for suppression).

- **6–10** = Extreme pluralism: any attempt at orthodoxy enforcement is actively sanctioned; maximum viewpoint diversity is enforced across institutions.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute pluralism: dogmatic mechanisms are systematically dismantled with only marginal residual enforcement.
- **0** = Absolute pluralism: zero tolerance for any suppression of dissent; every moral or ideological framework is treated as equally legitimate with no binding orthodoxy whatsoever.
