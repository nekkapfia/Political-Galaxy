# Dogmatism

**Source Document**  
**Date:** July 2026

## Dogmatism Definition

Dogmatism measures the observable degree to which a system treats its foundational moral, cultural, and ideological principles as closed — both to internal revision or challenge, and to engagement with or legitimacy for alternative moral frameworks originating outside the in-group. It tracks institutional, legal, educational, and cultural mechanisms that protect core principles from change while rejecting or delegitimizing external alternatives.

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Aspects

Rather than scoring Dogmatism as a single undifferentiated concept, scorers should analyse it through these two complementary aspects:

### Aspect 1: Orthodoxy (Internal)

**Orthodoxy** measures the observable degree to which the system treats its own foundational principles as closed to legitimate empirical challenge or revision. It tracks the strength of institutional, legal, educational, and social mechanisms that suppress, penalize, delegitimize, or pathologize open inquiry, internal dissent, or deviation from those core principles.

### Aspect 2: Tribalism (External)

**Tribalism** measures the observable degree to which the system rejects or treats as illegitimate moral frameworks and value systems that originate from outside its own group. It tracks institutional, legal, educational, and cultural mechanisms that dismiss, delegitimize, or actively oppose alternative moral systems associated with other groups or identities.

## Final Scoring

The two aspects (**Orthodoxy** and **Tribalism**) are scored independently on the 0–100 scale. The final Dogmatism score is calculated as follows:

$$
A = \frac{\text{Orthodoxy} + \text{Tribalism}}{2}
$$

$$
H = \max(\text{Orthodoxy}, \text{Tribalism})
$$

$$
d = \frac{|\text{Orthodoxy} - \text{Tribalism}|}{100}
$$

$$
F = (1 - d) \cdot A + d \cdot H
$$

The final score \( F \) is rounded **down** to the nearest whole number.

## Scoring Bands

### 96–100 — Total / Extreme Dogmatism (Compressed Extreme)
The system permits no meaningful internal challenge to its own foundational principles and no legitimacy for external moral frameworks.

- **100** = Any internal questioning of core principles or positive engagement with external moral frameworks is prevented through law, institutional rules, or direct punishment across all major domains.
- **98–99** = Only narrow, temporary, and officially approved exceptions are permitted for either internal revision or engagement with external frameworks. Unauthorized activity results in institutional or legal sanctions.
- **97** = Core principles are treated as settled and beyond internal revision. External moral frameworks are treated as illegitimate. Challenge or engagement results in professional, institutional, or legal consequences.
- **96** = Laws, institutional rules, or official policies penalize open internal challenge to foundational principles and engagement with external moral frameworks in multiple domains. Education and state-linked media present core principles as settled and external frameworks as invalid or threatening.

### 86–95 — Strong Dogmatism (Usable High Range)
The system strongly protects its own foundational principles from internal change while robustly rejecting external moral frameworks.

- Legal or institutional rules penalize public challenge to core principles and engagement with or positive reference to external moral frameworks in at least one major domain.
- Education curricula present core principles as authoritative and limit critical examination of them, and frame external moral frameworks as inferior or dangerous.
- Individuals who openly deviate from core principles or positively engage with external moral frameworks in institutional settings face professional or institutional sanctions.
- State or major institutional media frame deviation from core principles and external moral frameworks as illegitimate.

### 76–85 — Predominant Dogmatism (Usable High-Mid Range)
The system actively protects its foundational principles from major internal revision while rejecting external moral frameworks.

- Institutional or legal mechanisms discourage significant deviation from core principles and broad engagement with external moral frameworks.
- Education curricula present core principles as correct while permitting only limited discussion of alternatives.
- Public criticism of core principles by officials or in state media carries consequences.
- Public positive reference to external moral frameworks by officials or in state media carries consequences.
- Promotion or advancement within major institutions is linked to alignment with core principles and rejection of external frameworks.

### 66–75 — Moderate to Strong Dogmatism (Usable Mid-High Range)
The system protects its foundational principles from major internal change while rejecting external moral frameworks.

- Institutional mechanisms limit major revision of core principles and broad acceptance of external frameworks.
- Education curricula present core principles as established while allowing some critical discussion, and treat external moral frameworks as secondary or inferior.
- Individuals can question specific applications of core principles or engage selectively with external frameworks without automatic institutional punishment.
- State media supports core principles and rejects external frameworks but occasionally reports internal debate or external developments critically.

### 56–65 — Noticeable Dogmatism (Usable Mid Range)
The system maintains identifiable foundational principles that receive protection from major internal revision while external moral frameworks are rejected in some contexts but discussed in others.

- Some laws or institutional rules limit major revision of core principles and positive engagement with external moral frameworks.
- Education curricula include both affirmation of core principles and space for critical examination, and include critical presentation of external frameworks.
- Public figures can criticize aspects of core principles or engage with external moral frameworks without consistent institutional punishment.
- Media outlets affiliated with the system present both defense of core principles and criticism of external frameworks.

### 48–55 — Transitional Dogmatism (Usable Middle Range)
The system has recognizable foundational principles.

- Protection of core principles from major revision exists in some institutions or policy areas but is absent or weak in others.
- Rejection of external moral frameworks exists in some institutions or policy areas but is absent or weak in others.
- Education curricula contain both reinforcement of and critical perspectives on foundational principles, and both reinforcement of and critical perspectives on external moral frameworks.
- Individuals face consequences for challenging core principles in some institutions but not others, and face consequences for engaging with external moral frameworks in some institutions but not others.
- Media coverage includes both supportive and critical content regarding core principles, and both supportive and critical content regarding external moral frameworks.

### 40–47 — Moderate Openness (Usable Mid-Low Range)
The system maintains some foundational principles but generally treats them as open to internal challenge and reform while showing limited rejection of external moral frameworks.

- Few laws or institutional rules penalize criticism of core principles or engagement with external moral frameworks.
- Education curricula present core principles as subjects for analysis and debate and treat external moral frameworks as legitimate topics for examination.
- Individuals can challenge core principles or engage with external moral frameworks without systematic professional or institutional punishment.
- Media regularly publishes criticism of core principles and discussion of external moral frameworks.

### 30–39 — Low to Moderate Dogmatism (Usable Low-Mid Range)
The system has identifiable foundational principles that receive limited protection from internal revision while external moral frameworks are engaged with on relatively open terms.

- Institutional mechanisms to protect the framework and its core principles from major revision or rejection of external moral frameworks exist in some areas.
- Education curricula present core principles as established in some contexts while allowing substantial critical examination, and treat external moral frameworks as legitimate for comparison and debate.
- Public criticism or reform of core principles occurs regularly.
- Public positive engagement with external moral frameworks occurs regularly.
- Media and institutions show both defense of core principles and open discussion of external moral frameworks.

### 20–29 — Moderate Dogmatism (Usable Low Range)
The system maintains foundational principles that are defended selectively while permitting substantial internal debate, reform, and engagement with external moral frameworks.

- Core principles receive institutional protection from major revision in specific domains.
- Education curricula balance affirmation of core principles with space for critique and comparison with external moral frameworks.
- Individuals and groups can challenge or reform core principles with moderate institutional pushback.
- Individuals and groups can engage positively with external moral frameworks with moderate institutional pushback.
- Media coverage includes both support for core principles and open discussion of external moral frameworks.

### 10–19 — Moderate to Low Dogmatism (Usable Low Range)
The system has foundational principles that are acknowledged but treated as open to substantial internal challenge and evolution, while engaging openly with external moral frameworks.

- Institutional protection of core principles from major revision is present but limited.
- Education curricula present core principles as important yet subject to ongoing debate and comparison with external moral frameworks.
- Public criticism and reform of core principles are common.
- Public positive engagement with external moral frameworks is common.
- Media and institutions actively discuss and compare core principles with external moral frameworks.

### 5–9 — Low Dogmatism (Usable Low Range)
The system shows limited attachment to fixed foundational principles and accepts internal challenge while engaging with external moral frameworks.

- Institutional mechanisms to protect core principles from major revision or to reject external moral frameworks are minimal.
- Education curricula treat core principles as open to critique and reform, and present external moral frameworks as legitimate for examination and comparison.
- Public criticism of core principles occurs regularly.
- Public engagement with external moral frameworks occurs regularly.
- Media and institutions routinely debate and compare core principles with external moral frameworks.

### 0–4 — Absolute Low Dogmatism (Compressed Extreme)
This is the theoretical floor of the slider. There are no remaining mechanisms that protect core principles from internal revision or reject external moral frameworks.

- **4** = Minimal institutional protection for foundational principles against internal revision. Minimal institutional rejection of external moral frameworks.
- **3** = Institutional and cultural mechanisms discourage rigid defense of core principles. Institutional and cultural mechanisms discourage rejection of external moral frameworks.
- **1–2** = Foundational principles have no effective defense against internal revision. External moral frameworks face no institutional rejection.
- **0** = No institutional, legal, educational, or cultural mechanisms exist that can stabilize foundational principles against internal revision or reject external moral frameworks in any form.

**End of Dogmatism Slider**
