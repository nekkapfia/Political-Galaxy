# Cultural 5A: Tradition vs Progress

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

## Cultural 5A Definition

This slider measures how the system views traditions and the extent to which they attempt to preserve/modernise them. 

It tracks the degree to which the system reveres, enforces and preserves traditional norms, customs, historical precedents, inherited structures, and established practices versus actively updating, modernising or replacing them in favor of new ideals, innovation, adaptation, and future-oriented change. 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, education curricula, media narratives, public rituals, policy priorities, institutional design, cultural output, and daily norms) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Traditions are viewed as sacrosanct, inviolable, sacred, and essential to identity. They are actively revered and protected with any perceived deviations treated with zero tolerance. This allows observable variation within different traditionalist systems (different bases for continuity — religious, ancestral, cultural, or institutional — while retaining total rejection of future-oriented transformation).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal protection and enforcement of traditions in every domain with no criticism or change permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total traditional preservation; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined progressive accommodations permitted.
- **90–94** = Extreme tradition legitimation: traditional structures dominate law, education, institutions, and culture with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict traditional preservation and enforcement)
- Laws and institutions explicitly recognize, protect, and restore traditional norms, customs, historical precedents, and inherited structures (e.g., clear rules preserving family forms, heritage sites, ancestral practices).
- Education curricula prioritize traditional history, classics, and continuity with the past with zero deconstruction or innovation mandates.
- Media and public rituals openly celebrate heritage, historical continuity, and traditional achievement.
- Statistical outcomes show strong correlation between traditional norms and cultural transmission with active enforcement against progressive disruption.

### 70–79 (Mid-High – Predominantly traditional with limited exceptions)
- Core domains maintain tradition as primary but allow narrow, revocable progressive policies in secondary areas.
- Traditional structures remain visible and culturally reinforced; disruption of continuity is limited to specific issues only.
- Family and inheritance policy preserves traditional transmission of norms and practices.
- Public discourse and institutions treat historical continuity as natural or beneficial rather than inherently problematic.

### 51–69 (Mid-Range – Balanced or contested tradition vs progress)
- Mixed observable patterns: tradition legitimated in some core domains (e.g., law, family policy) while progressive pressures appear in others (e.g., education curricula).
- Enforcement of traditional continuity exists but faces measurable pushback or selective exemptions; progressive change policies are present but not dominant.
- Media narratives show both celebration of heritage and calls for reform without clear supremacy of either.
- Statistical outcomes reflect moderate correlation between tradition and cultural transmission, with some observable disruption interventions.

### 31–50 (Mid–Low – Noticeable shift toward progressive transformation)
- Progressive policies gain observable dominance in multiple domains (e.g., mandatory curriculum decolonisation, legal redefinition of family structures, heritage deconstruction or renaming campaigns).
- Traditional structures are tolerated only where politically unavoidable and are actively reframed as outdated or problematic.
- Public rituals and media increasingly emphasize novelty, innovation, and future-oriented social engineering over historical continuity.
- Family and inheritance rules begin to incorporate disruption mechanisms (e.g., redefinition of marriage, inheritance taxes aimed at breaking generational wealth transmission).

### 11–30 (Low – Strong enforcement of anti-tradition / progressivism)
- Traditions and Historical norms are almost entirely viewed as regressive or barbaric and actively rejected/replaced across most domains: explicit bans or heavy penalties on traditional practices, mandatory progressive re-engineering.
- Traditional structures are dismantled or heavily constrained wherever possible; historical symbols are actively discouraged or inverted.
- Education, employment, law, and institutions prioritize future-oriented change and social engineering over continuity.
- Cultural reflexes and daily norms treat any observable adherence to tradition as inherently illegitimate or harmful.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute enforced progressivism) — Compressed zone
Traditions are viewed as universally Primitive and Immoral. Total suppression of tradition making practicing of them a crime, regardless of historical continuity, customs, or inherited structures. This allows observable variation within different radical progressivist systems (different mechanisms of dismantling while retaining zero tolerance for any traditional continuity).

- **6–10** = Extreme enforced progressivism: tradition treated as inherently illegitimate; widespread dismantling policies, innovation mandates, and cultural erasure dominate most core domains with extreme sanctions for any deviation.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute enforced progressivism: mandatory progressive transformation enforced across nearly all domains with only marginal exceptions and heavy monitoring.
- **0** = Absolute enforced progressivism: zero tolerance for any tradition whatsoever; all norms, institutions, and historical continuity forcibly dismantled with total monitoring and punishment of any deviation.
