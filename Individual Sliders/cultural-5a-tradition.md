# Cultural 5A: Tradition vs Progress

## Core Definition

This slider measures how the system views traditions and the extent to which they attempt to preserve/modernise them. 

It tracks the degree to which the system reveres, enforces and preserves traditional norms, customs, historical precedents, inherited structures, and established practices versus actively updating, modernising or replacing them in favor of new ideals, innovation, adaptation, and future-oriented change. 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, education curricula, media narratives, public rituals, policy priorities, institutional design, cultural output, and daily norms) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

---

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Traditions are treated as requiring strong institutional protection and continuity across all domains, with any deviations subject to prohibition or severe restriction. This allows observable variation within different traditionalist systems (different bases for continuity — religious, ancestral, cultural, or institutional — while retaining total rejection of future-oriented transformation that conflicts with established norms).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal protection and enforcement of traditions in every domain with no criticism or change permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total traditional preservation; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined progressive accommodations permitted.
- **90–94** = Extreme tradition legitimation: traditional structures dominate law, education, institutions, and culture with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict traditional preservation and enforcement)
- Laws and institutions explicitly recognize, protect, and restore traditional norms, customs, historical precedents, and inherited structures (e.g., clear rules preserving family forms, heritage sites, ancestral practices).
- Education curricula prioritize traditional history, classics, and continuity with the past with zero deconstruction or innovation mandates.
- Media and public rituals openly celebrate heritage, historical continuity, and traditional achievement.
- Statistical outcomes show strong correlation between traditional norms and cultural transmission with active enforcement against progressive disruption.

### 70–79 (Mid-High – Predominantly traditional with limited exceptions)
- Core domains maintain tradition as primary but allow narrow, revocable progressive policies in secondary areas.
- Traditional structures remain visible and culturally reinforced; disruption of continuity is limited to specific issues only.
- Family and inheritance policy preserves traditional transmission of norms and practices.
- Public discourse and institutions treat historical continuity as the established baseline rather than a target for reform.

### 60–69 (Mid – Tradition dominant in core domains with strong progressive pressures in education, media, and secondary policy areas)
- Tradition remains the default framework in law, family policy, and major institutions, but progressive re-engineering is visibly institutionalized in education curricula, public media, and some regulatory domains.
- Enforcement of traditional continuity is consistent but faces routine ideological challenge and selective exemptions or carve-outs.
- Media narratives and cultural output show competing frames: heritage celebration alongside calls for modernization and reform of established norms.
- Statistical outcomes reflect strong traditional transmission in core demographics alongside measurable disruption interventions in targeted sectors (e.g., education, family formation).

### 51–59 (Mid-Lower – Contested balance; progressive frameworks gain significant traction in core domains while tradition retains selective institutional protection)
- Progressive policies and reframing of tradition as requiring reform achieve dominance in several core domains (e.g., education content, some family law reforms, public symbolism) but encounter organized institutional or legal resistance in others.
- Traditional structures are preserved in key areas (e.g., certain inheritance or cultural recognition rules) but are actively contested and incrementally narrowed through policy or institutional practice.
- Media and public discourse feature sustained debate with neither tradition-preservation nor progressive transformation achieving clear supremacy across all domains; both approaches receive institutional legitimacy in different contexts.
- Observable policy oscillation or compromise outcomes between continuity and transformation in core areas.

### 41–50 (Low-Mid – Progressive transformation dominant in most core domains; tradition tolerated only where unavoidable and subject to requirements for reform or justification)
- Progressive social engineering and future-oriented change constitute the prevailing pattern across law, education, media, and institutional practice in most core domains.
- Traditional norms and structures are tolerated only where politically or practically unavoidable; they are routinely subject to requirements for reform, justification, or alignment with progressive frameworks in official policy and institutional communications.
- Public rituals, education curricula, and media content emphasize novelty, innovation, and adaptation of historical practices as the primary direction; continuity with the past is presented as requiring active management or updating.
- Family and inheritance rules incorporate mechanisms that alter or limit generational transmission of traditional norms (e.g., redefinition of family structures, wealth redistribution policies affecting inheritance patterns).

### 31–40 (Low – Strong enforcement of progressivism; traditional structures actively dismantled, restricted, or subject to institutional pressure and sanctions in core domains)
- Explicit policies and institutional mechanisms target the reduction or elimination of traditional practices, symbols, and transmission mechanisms across most major domains.
- Historical norms and symbols are systematically subject to critique, reform requirements, replacement, or restrictions; adherence to traditional practices faces institutional, legal, regulatory, or professional consequences in many contexts.
- Education curricula, media narratives, and institutional communications treat traditional continuity as requiring reform or alignment with progressive objectives; future-oriented social engineering is presented as the primary operational path.
- Observable reduction in institutional and public spaces where traditional norms operate without requirements for justification, reform, or adaptation; cultural output shows measurable shift away from un-modified traditional transmission.

### 21–30 (Low – Very strong anti-tradition enforcement; historical norms and symbols actively suppressed, restricted, or subject to erasure campaigns in core public and institutional domains)
- Traditional practices face widespread regulatory burdens, institutional restrictions, or targeted campaigns for reform or replacement in core areas (law, education, public institutions, employment norms).
- Historical symbols, narratives, and institutions are actively discouraged, renamed, repurposed, or subjected to requirements for alignment with progressive frameworks through policy or institutional action.
- Daily norms, institutional culture, and professional standards require observable adherence to tradition to be accompanied by justification, reform commitments, or adaptation to progressive objectives.
- Progressive transformation constitutes the near-default institutional and policy approach; residual traditional elements persist primarily in private spheres or under explicit exemptions with ongoing oversight.

### 11–20 (Very Low – Near-absolute progressivist enforcement in practice; tradition subject to prohibition, severe restriction, or requirements for complete alignment with progressive frameworks across nearly all observable domains)
- Almost all institutional and cultural mechanisms prioritize the dismantling, restriction, or reform of traditional continuity; any public or official expression of traditional norms is subject to significant professional, legal, regulatory, or institutional consequences unless accompanied by explicit alignment with progressive objectives.
- Education, media, law, and governance operate under a dominant future-oriented framework with minimal institutional space for unmodified traditional continuity.
- Statistical and observable outcomes show near-complete displacement or transformation of traditional transmission patterns by engineered progressive alternatives in core domains.
- Institutional rules, professional standards, and public communications require traditional identity, heritage practices, or transmission mechanisms to be adapted, reformed, or aligned with system objectives; unmodified traditional continuity faces institutional marginalization or prohibition in most public contexts.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute enforced progressivism) — Compressed zone
Traditions are treated as incompatible with system requirements. Institutional rules prohibit or severely restrict traditional practices and transmission, regardless of historical continuity, customs, or inherited structures. This allows observable variation within different progressivist systems (different mechanisms of reform, restriction, or replacement while retaining zero tolerance for unmodified traditional continuity).

- **6–10** = Extreme enforced progressivism: tradition treated as incompatible with institutional rules; widespread dismantling policies, innovation mandates, and requirements for alignment with progressive frameworks dominate most core domains with severe sanctions (legal, financial, professional, or institutional) for unmodified traditional practices.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute enforced progressivism: mandatory progressive transformation and alignment requirements enforced across nearly all domains with only marginal, explicitly justified exemptions under heavy monitoring and oversight.
- **0** = Absolute enforced progressivism: zero tolerance for any unmodified traditional continuity whatsoever; all norms, institutions, and historical transmission mechanisms are required to be reformed, restricted, or replaced in accordance with progressive frameworks, with total institutional prohibition and monitoring of deviation.
