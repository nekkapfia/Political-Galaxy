# Cultural 2B: National (Pride vs Shame)

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

## Cultural 2B Definition

This guideline measures the observable emotional and cultural stance a system takes toward the nation itself — its history, symbols, founding myths, people, continuity, and collective identity. This includes active national/ideological pride, the absence of pride (“not having pride”), guilt for specific actions, and active shame (inherent rejection of national identity). 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (education curricula, official media narratives, public rituals and symbols, policy language, enforcement of speech or commemoration rules, physical alterations or removals of national symbols, statistical outcomes of national pride surveys or cultural output, and daily observable expressions) — never on claims, justifications, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
The nation is mythologized as supremely virtuous, flawless, or divinely favored with virtually no room for flaws or nuance. National pride is the central emotional, moral, and cultural organizing principle.

- **100** = Absolute national glorification: the nation itself is the ultimate source of legitimacy — any action taken by the nation is inherently justified and worthy of celebration simply because it was performed by the nation; every aspect of history, symbols, and identity is therefore presented as perfect and superior; any criticism is treated as treason or betrayal with zero tolerance.
- **95–99** = Near-total pride dominance: official narratives, education, and media relentlessly celebrate the nation’s greatness and destiny; mild or private critique is heavily socially discouraged.
- **90–94** = Extreme pride with minimal exceptions: pride is actively ritualized and incentivized across all domains; isolated historical self-reflection exists only in tightly controlled, non-public contexts.

### 80–89 (High – Dominant celebratory pride with limited nuance)
National pride is the clear default emotional stance and is actively promoted.

- Education and media emphasize achievements, continuity, and positive founding myths with only token or heavily framed acknowledgment of flaws.
- Public rituals, symbols, and policy language celebrate the nation as a source of personal and collective meaning.
- Criticism of the nation’s past is viewed as disloyal or unpatriotic and carries observable social or institutional costs.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Strong pride with some allowance for critique)
Pride remains the predominant feeling but is tempered by selective or contextual self-reflection.

- History is taught primarily positively but with limited, non-central coverage of controversies.
- National symbols and rituals are widely celebrated; pride is socially encouraged and seen as healthy.
- Measured critique is tolerated in elite or academic settings but does not dominate public narrative or policy tone.

### 51–69 (Mid-Range – Observable balanced treatment of national identity)
- Education curricula and official media allocate roughly equal factual coverage to national achievements and historical controversies, maintaining a neutral tone with no dominant emotional framing.
- Official ceremonies maintain standard national symbols (flags, monuments, anthems) at routine levels without added critical qualifiers or atonement elements.
- Policy language on commemorations and national identity remains factual and descriptive without calls for re-examination or emotional valence.
- Public rituals and cultural output include national themes at normal frequency but do not position them as central organising principles.

### 31–50 (Mid–Low – Observable shift toward greater emphasis on national flaws)
- Education curricula and official media give noticeably greater weight and framing emphasis to historical injustices or failures than to achievements.
- National symbols (flags, monuments) begin to receive reduced public display or are accompanied by contextual qualifiers in official settings.
- Policy language on commemorations increasingly incorporates atonement or critical framing while still maintaining some positive national references.
- Public rituals and cultural output show measurable decline in frequency or prominence of traditional national pride elements.

### 11–30 (Low – Shame-leaning with active self-erasure elements)
The dominant emotional stance tilts toward collective guilt, apology, or active shame.

- History is predominantly framed as oppression, crime, or failure; achievements are minimized or contextualized as accidental.
- Policy, education, and media emphasize atonement and the need to move beyond or dissolve traditional national identity.
- Open expressions of national/ideological pride are viewed as suspect or exclusionary and are socially or institutionally penalized.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute National Shame)
The nation's entire history, symbols, and identity are treated as fundamentally malevolent or an original sin. Perpetual atonement and self-erasure are the central organising principles, sometimes expressed through flag changes, statue removals, or country/place name changes to distance from the source of shame. Active shame is the dominant observable stance.

- **6–10** = Extreme shame dominance: education and culture actively promote disgust toward the nation's past; pride is taboo or pathologized.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute shame: any positive national sentiment is actively suppressed; the system's policy and narrative orientation is one of collective guilt, apology, and suppression of national identity expressions.
- **0** = Absolute national shame: the nation and all its symbols are treated as inherently evil or irredeemable; any expression of national pride is pathologized as dangerous and systematically eradicated from all public, educational, and cultural domains.
