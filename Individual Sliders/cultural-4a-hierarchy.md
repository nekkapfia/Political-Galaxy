# Cultural 4A: Hierarchy

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

## Cultural 4A Definition

This guideline measures the observable stance a system takes toward social and power hierarchies — the degree to which it actively enforces, legitimizes, naturalizes, or promotes ranked structures of authority, status, resource allocation, and outcomes based on merit, effort, tradition, competence, or inherited position versus actively dismantling or suppressing such hierarchies in favor of enforced egalitarianism (mandated equal outcomes, suppression of differential status, and elimination of ranked distinctions). 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, education curricula, employment practices, family policy, media narratives, institutional decision-making, public rituals, and statistical outcomes of status/resource distribution) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Absolute or near-absolute enforcement and legitimation of social/power hierarchies across all domains, with zero tolerance for egalitarian leveling. This allows observable variation within different hierarchical systems (different bases for rank — merit, tradition, competence, or inherited position — while retaining total rejection of equal-outcome mandates).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal enforcement of ranked hierarchy in every domain with no egalitarian exceptions or rhetoric permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total hierarchy enforcement; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined egalitarian accommodations permitted.
- **90–94** = Extreme hierarchy legitimation: ranked structures dominate law, education, employment, and institutions with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict merit/traditional hierarchy enforcement)
- Law and institutions explicitly recognize and protect natural hierarchies based on merit, competence, and responsibility.
- Education and employment systems strongly reward individual achievement and competence.
- Public rituals and media celebrate excellence, leadership, and earned status.

### 70–79 (Mid-High – Predominantly hierarchical with limited egalitarian policies)
- Hierarchies are generally accepted and supported, with only narrow exceptions for protected classes or temporary measures.

### 51–69 (Mid-Range – Mixed hierarchy vs egalitarianism)
- Observable tension or balance between acceptance of hierarchies and egalitarian interventions.

### 31–50 (Mid-Low – Noticeable shift toward enforced egalitarianism)
- Policies increasingly aim to reduce or eliminate status differences through redistribution, quotas, or suppression of excellence.

### 11–30 (Low – Strong enforcement of egalitarianism)
- Active dismantling of hierarchies; merit-based outcomes are heavily overridden in favor of equal results.
- Public discourse frames natural hierarchies as oppressive.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute enforced egalitarianism)
- All observable hierarchies are systematically suppressed or inverted in favor of mandated equal outcomes across domains.
