# Cultural 4A: Hierarchy

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

## Cultural 4A Definition

This guideline measures the observable stance a system takes toward social and power hierarchies — the degree to which it actively enforces, legitimizes, naturalizes, or promotes ranked structures of authority, status, resource allocation, and outcomes based on merit, effort, tradition, competence, or inherited position versus actively dismantling or suppressing such hierarchies in favor of enforced egalitarianism (mandated equal outcomes, suppression of differential status, and elimination of ranked distinctions). 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, education curricula, employment practices, family policy, media narratives, institutional decision-making, public rituals, and statistical outcomes of status/resource distribution) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Absolute or near-absolute enforcement and legitimation of social/power hierarchies across all domains, with zero tolerance for egalitarian leveling. This allows observable variation within different hierarchical systems (different bases for rank — merit, tradition, competence, or inherited position — while retaining total rejection of equal-outcome mandates).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal enforcement of ranked hierarchy in every domain with no egalitarian exceptions or rhetoric permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total hierarchy enforcement; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined egalitarian accommodations permitted.
- **90–94** = Extreme hierarchy legitimation: ranked structures dominate law, education, employment, and institutions with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict merit/traditional hierarchy enforcement)
- Laws and institutions explicitly recognize and protect ranked authority structures (e.g., clear chains of command, inheritance rules, merit-based selection with no quotas).
- Education and employment decisions prioritize observable competence, effort, or traditional rank with zero outcome-equalization mandates.
- Media and public rituals openly celebrate hierarchical achievement and status differentiation.
- Statistical outcomes show strong correlation between merit/effort and status/resource allocation with active enforcement against leveling.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Predominantly hierarchical with limited exceptions)
- Core domains maintain merit or traditional rank as primary but allow narrow, revocable egalitarian policies in secondary areas.
- Authority structures remain visible and culturally reinforced; suppression of hierarchy is limited to specific issues only.
- Family and inheritance policy preserves ranked transmission of status/wealth.
- Public discourse and institutions treat hierarchy as natural or beneficial rather than inherently problematic.

### 60–69 (Mid – Moderate hierarchy with noticeable egalitarian exceptions)
Ranked structures and merit/traditional authority remain the default in most core domains, yet egalitarian policies create visible friction and selective exemptions.

- Merit-based selection and competence criteria are prioritised in law, military, and institutional governance.
- Education admissions and public-sector employment apply limited outcome-equalization measures or diversity targets.
- Status symbols and differential rewards are openly recognised but occasionally qualified by anti-bias rules or equity considerations.
- Media narratives celebrate hierarchical achievement and excellence alongside criticism of “unfair” rank, without either achieving clear dominance.
- Statistical outcomes continue to show moderate-to-strong correlation between competence/effort and status/resource allocation, with some observable leveling interventions that do not override the predominant pattern.

### 51–59 (Mid–Lower – Noticeable shift toward egalitarian enforcement)
- Egalitarian policies gain observable dominance in multiple domains (e.g., mandatory diversity quotas in employment/education, legal restrictions on merit-based selection).
- Hierarchical structures are tolerated only where unavoidable and are actively reframed as suspect or temporary.
- Public rituals and media increasingly emphasize equal outcomes over ranked achievement.
- Family and inheritance rules begin to incorporate leveling mechanisms (e.g., wealth redistribution mandates).

### 41–50 (Low–Mid – Multiple significant egalitarian mechanisms)
- Group outcome equalization appears in several key domains alongside weakening merit-based structures.
- Law and institutions apply differential treatment aimed at reducing ranked distinctions.
- Media and education promote narratives of fairness through reduced hierarchy.
- Statistical outcomes reflect growing compression of status and resource distribution.

### 31–40 (Low – Strong enforcement of anti-hierarchy / egalitarianism)
- Suppression of hierarchies becomes primary across most domains: explicit bans or heavy penalties on merit-based differentiation, mandatory equal-outcome rules.
- Authority structures are dismantled or heavily constrained wherever possible; status symbols are actively discouraged or inverted.
- Education, employment, and law prioritize group outcome equalization over individual competence or traditional rank.
- Cultural reflexes and daily norms treat any observable hierarchy as inherently illegitimate or harmful.

### 11–30 (Very Low – Near-total egalitarian enforcement)
- Ranked distinctions are heavily suppressed in law, institutions, and daily practice.
- Merit, tradition, or competence-based authority faces systematic dismantling.
- Public discourse and education frame all hierarchy as oppressive or unjust.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute enforced egalitarianism) — Compressed zone
Total suppression of all hierarchy with universal enforcement of equal outcomes regardless of merit, effort, tradition, or competence. This allows observable variation within different radical egalitarian systems (different mechanisms of leveling while retaining zero tolerance for any ranked differentiation).

- **6–10** = Extreme enforced egalitarianism: hierarchy treated as inherently illegitimate: widespread quotas, leveling policies, and cultural inversion of status dominate most core domains with extreme sanctions for any deviation.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute enforced egalitarianism: mandatory equal outcomes enforced across nearly all domains with only marginal exceptions and heavy monitoring.
- **0** = Absolute enforced egalitarianism: zero tolerance for any hierarchy whatsoever; all status, authority, resource allocation, and outcomes forcibly leveled with total monitoring and punishment of any deviation.
