# Reform UK – Cultural 1A (Foundation)

**Score: 4**

Reform UK presents itself as the party of common-sense restoration. It argues that Britain has been failed by weak leadership, uncontrolled immigration, and institutional capture, and that the solution is to put the interests of British citizens first.

### Moral Justification

* Immigration and border control are justified by the right of the British people to decide who joins their political community and by the concrete costs imposed on housing, wages, and services.
* Policy is repeatedly framed through “putting the British people first” and the principle that charity begins at home.
* Elite and international constraints are criticised as overrides of democratic will.
* Cultural and national identity are defended as the inheritance and cohesion of the existing population rather than as sacred or transcendent authorities.
* Quantifiable benefits to current citizens (taxpayer savings, reduced pressure on public services, higher wages) are central to justification.

The language of Reform UK is demos-centred, reciprocal, and anti-elite. Virtually every major claim is grounded in the concrete interests and democratic will of living British citizens. Immigration, institutions, and cultural questions are all evaluated through the lens of what serves or harms the existing political community. Higher-law, universalist, or sacred claims are almost entirely absent; even cultural defence is framed as the practical inheritance and cohesion of the current population. The party presents itself as the straightforward vehicle for the neglected interests of ordinary British citizens against institutions and elites that have ceased to prioritise them.

### Why this produces a final score of 4
The transcendent source is almost entirely absent across core domains. All major claims are grounded in popular sovereignty, citizen interest, reciprocity, and national self-preservation, producing an extremely strong mundane lean only marginally less pure than Restore Britain.
