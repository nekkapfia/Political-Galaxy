# Conservative Party – Core 1A (Personal Autonomy)

**Score: 68**

## Brief Overview

The Conservative Party’s Core 1A position reflects a managed retention of baseline speech, online, and public-order restrictions while resisting further expansion of identity-based or election-period controls. The score of 68 sits in the higher end of the modern British party spectrum, driven by opposition to new layers of regulation rather than wholesale repeal of existing frameworks such as the Online Safety Act 2023 or Public Order Act provisions.

## Expanded Analysis

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

- Retention of the Online Safety Act 2023 framework primarily framed around child protection and illegal content, without proposed expansion into broader “harmful but legal” adult speech categories beyond the current baseline.
- Continued operation of Public Order Act 1986 and Communications Act 2003 offences relating to threatening, abusive or insulting words and behaviour.
- Support for under-16 social-media age limits and age-verification measures.
- Opposition to new election-period social-media duties or additional anti-hostility definitional expansions.
- Maintenance of existing hate-speech and non-crime hate incident recording practices without significant widening.

Under the system’s strict negative-only weighting rules, the absence of further restrictive proposals does not generate positive credit. The score therefore reflects the cumulative weight of the retained baseline rather than any liberalising direction of travel.

### Differential Treatment

No permanent Endos/Xenos legal divergence is applied for the Conservative Party under current Core 1A rules. Mainstream parties receive a single holistic score unless explicit statutory differentials exist.

## Why This Produces a Final Score of 68

The 68 placement arises from:

1. Retention of a non-trivial suite of speech and online regulation (Online Safety Act baseline + Public Order / Communications Act offences) which prevents scores in the high 70s–80s.
2. Explicit resistance to the progressive identity package expansions (self-ID institutional effects, mandatory EDI, expanded hostility definitions) that pull Labour, Green, SNP and Liberal Democrat scores into the high 20s–low 50s range.
3. No credit for rhetorical free-speech commitments or future repeal promises; only observable current and proposed policy is scored.

The result is a relatively high (within the contemporary UK party distribution) but still restricted score that sits clearly above the progressive cluster while remaining well below pure non-intervention positions.
