# Conservative Party – Cultural 1A (Foundation)

**Score: 22**

The Conservative Party presents itself as the party of national recovery, secure borders, economic competence, and the defence of British institutions and culture. Its core aim is to restore public confidence in the state’s ability to control immigration, maintain order, and deliver competent government after a period of perceived failure and internal division.

### Moral Justification

* Immigration and borders are justified by the right of the British people to decide who enters the country and the duty of government to prioritise the security, housing, and wages of existing citizens.
* Economic policy is framed through competence, work, incentives, and the need to deliver measurable improvements in living standards for British families.
* Cultural and national identity issues are defended as the protection of a shared inheritance and social cohesion that belongs to the current political community.
* Institutional continuity is presented as pragmatic stewardship of arrangements that have historically served the country, rather than as sacred tradition.
* Elite and international constraints are frequently criticised when they are seen to override the democratic will or concrete interests of British voters.

The party’s language centres on the interests and democratic will of the existing British political community. It consistently presents government as having a primary duty to the security, prosperity, and cultural continuity of those who already constitute the demos. At the same time it retains a residual claim to governing experience and institutional competence, positioning itself as the responsible alternative both to the left and to more radical forces on its right. The overall self-presentation is therefore one of restored control and national self-respect achieved through pragmatic, citizen-focused government rather than through higher moral or transformative projects.

### Why this produces a final score of 22
Mundane sources (popular sovereignty, citizen interest, institutional pragmatism, and empirical tradition) are clearly primary. Transcendent sources are marginal. Residual institutional and competence language prevents the score from falling as low as Reform UK or Restore Britain.

The populist direction has gained ground, but the party as a whole still retains more institutional and competence language than Reform UK or Restore Britain.

## Kemi Badenoch, National / Populist Conservatives (Primary Faction)

This current argues that the Conservative Party must prioritise the democratic will and concrete interests of existing British citizens above institutional caution, international opinion, or liberal constraints. Its core aim is a clearer focus on borders, national identity, and accountability to voters.

### Moral Justification

* Border control and immigration reduction are justified as the fundamental right of the British people to determine the composition of their own political community.
* National identity and cultural continuity are presented as goods that belong to the living population and must be actively defended.
* Elite, legal, and international moral claims are criticised when they appear to constrain or override democratic preference.
* Policy legitimacy is repeatedly grounded in “what the British people voted for” and the duty to deliver it.

The language of this faction is more confrontational and demos-centred than that of the wider Conservative Party. It consistently frames institutions, courts, international bodies, and internal party moderates as potential obstacles to the clear expression of popular will on immigration and identity. The faction presents itself as the authentic voice of voters who feel that successive governments, including Conservative ones, have failed to prioritise their concrete interests and cultural continuity. In doing so it shifts the party’s centre of gravity further toward pure popular-sovereignty justifications and away from residual institutional or One-Nation softening.

### Why this produces a final score of 17
The transcendent source is marginal. Moral authority is derived overwhelmingly from popular sovereignty and the concrete interests of living British citizens, producing a strong mundane lean only modestly above pure Reform/Restore levels.

## James Cleverly, One-Nation / Institutional Conservatives (Secondary Faction)

This current defends a tradition of responsible, pragmatic Conservatism that seeks to govern for the whole nation, maintain institutional stability, and avoid populist rupture. Its aim is competence, social cohesion, and the preservation of effective governing institutions.

### Moral Justification

* Institutions and governing conventions are justified as empirically successful arrangements that deliver stability and competent administration.
* National interest is framed more inclusively, with residual emphasis on social cohesion across classes and regions.
* Competence and delivery are treated as primary sources of political legitimacy.
* Radical or populist approaches are criticised as risks to the practical capacity to govern.

The language of this faction remains managerial and stewardship-oriented. It presents governing institutions and long-standing practices as practical inheritances that have demonstrably served the country, and it treats abrupt populist rupture as a threat to the state’s capacity to deliver stability and competence. The faction positions itself as the serious, governing wing of Conservatism, warning that excessive deference to immediate popular pressure risks undermining the very instruments required for effective national recovery. This keeps a measurable residual weight on institutional pragmatism even as the wider party moves in a more populist direction.

### Why this produces a final score of 29
Mundane sources dominate, but the stronger residual weight given to institutional competence and a more inclusive national framing produces a modestly higher score than the party’s populist wing.

## Mel Stride, Economic Liberal / Thatcherite Residual (Secondary Faction)

This current continues the post-1979 emphasis on individual responsibility, free markets, work, and limited government. Its core aim is to maximise prosperity and personal agency through competitive markets and reduced state interference.

### Moral Justification

* Economic policy is justified through incentives, personal responsibility, and the observable results of market competition versus state direction.
* Limited government is presented as both a practical necessity and a protection of individual agency.
* Work and contribution are treated as central moral and social goods.
* Rights language appears, but is usually tied to consent, property, and non-interference rather than to higher-law claims.

The language of this faction is individualist and outcome-focused. It frames prosperity and personal agency as the products of competitive markets, work, and restrained government rather than of collective moral projects or state direction. While rights language is present, it is operationalised through consent, property, and non-interference, keeping the justificatory centre of gravity firmly on mundane sources. The faction presents itself as the defender of economic freedom and personal responsibility within a Conservative Party that has become more preoccupied with national identity and borders.

### Why this produces a final score of 25
Mundane sources (individual agency, market outcomes, consent) are primary. Natural-rights language provides only a modest overlay and does not organise the overall justification.

## Miriam Cates, Socially Conservative Current (Secondary Faction)

This current places greater emphasis on traditional moral norms, cultural continuity, and, in some cases, religiously informed understandings of family and social order. Its aim is to defend the moral and cultural preconditions of a stable society.

### Moral Justification

* Social and cultural issues are frequently justified by reference to established moral norms and the conditions required for human flourishing and family stability.
* Cultural and religious inheritance is treated as a substantive good that should shape public life.
* Rapid social change is criticised as corrosive of the shared moral framework that sustains community.
* These claims are still usually advanced alongside, rather than in open rejection of, democratic and national-interest arguments.

The language of this faction introduces a clearer moral-traditional register than other Conservative currents. It treats established norms around family, culture, and social order as substantive goods that underwrite a stable society, and it is willing to invoke religious or civilisational inheritance more explicitly than the party mainstream. At the same time these claims remain embedded within a broader democratic and national-interest framework rather than being advanced as independent higher-law requirements that can simply override the demos. The result is the strongest transcendent element inside the contemporary Conservative Party, while still remaining well short of a fully religious or natural-law politics.

### Why this produces a final score of 34
This faction shows the strongest transcendent element within the Conservative Party because moral-traditional and occasionally religious sources are given greater independent weight. They remain secondary to popular sovereignty framing, keeping the score in the low-to-mid 30s.
