# Greens

### Greens

The Greens present ecological breakdown and intergenerational injustice as the defining moral crises of the age. Their core aim is systemic economic and social transformation to protect the living world and future generations.

### Moral Justification

* Climate and ecological policy is justified as a binding duty to the planet, to non-human nature, and to people not yet born.
* Present democratic majorities and short-term economic preferences are treated as subordinate to these longer-term and non-human claims.
* Economic arrangements are criticised in moral terms for their destructive impact on the natural world and on the poor.
* Scientific evidence is used extensively but functions mainly as support for a prior ethical imperative rather than as the ultimate source of authority.

The language of the Greens is strongly duty-based and future-oriented. Ecological and intergenerational obligations are treated as moral requirements that existing majorities and economic interests have no right to ignore. Scientific findings are deployed as diagnostic evidence for the scale of the crisis, but the ultimate source of authority remains the ethical claim of the natural world and of future people. The party presents itself as the political expression of an overriding moral obligation that conventional democratic and economic decision-making has systematically failed to honour.

### Why this produces a final score of 81
The transcendent source is primary and organising. Present democratic will and economic interests of living citizens are regularly subordinated to ecological and intergenerational claims, producing one of the strongest transcendent profiles in the British system.
