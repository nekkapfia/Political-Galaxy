# Labour Party – Core 1A (Personal Autonomy)

**Score: 52**

## Brief Overview

The Labour Party’s Core 1A position is defined by defence and modest expansion of the existing Online Safety Act framework, retention of hate-speech and public-order offences, and the introduction of additional election-period speech duties and anti-hostility definitions. The resulting score of 52 places it firmly in the progressive cluster of modern British parties.

## Expanded Analysis

### Core 1A Relevant Policies / Elements

- Defence and proposed extension of the Online Safety Act 2023, including duties that reach beyond the narrowest child-protection and illegal-content categories.
- Introduction of election-period social-media duties and additional definitional work around “anti-Muslim hostility”.
- Retention of Public Order Act 1986, Communications Act 2003, and existing hate-speech provisions.
- Support for child-protection age limits and verification measures.
- Institutional and cultural reinforcement of non-crime hate incident recording practices.

Under negative-only weighting, the cumulative weight of these retained and expanded elements produces a clear downward pull relative to parties that resist further layering of speech regulation.

### Differential Treatment

A modest Endos/Xenos distinction (approximately 52 / 48) is noted in some assessments due to identity-specific scrutiny pressures, but the primary published score remains the holistic 52.

## Why This Produces a Final Score of 52

1. Active maintenance and expansion of the Online Safety Act and related speech frameworks prevent scores above the mid-50s.
2. Alignment with the broader progressive identity package (EDI, self-ID institutional effects, hostility definitions) that characterises the 28–52 range for Labour, Green, SNP and Liberal Democrat positions.
3. No positive credit is available for any rhetorical commitments to free speech or future reviews; only the observable policy set is scored.

The 52 score is therefore a direct mechanical outcome of the retained baseline plus the additional definitional and election-period layers introduced or defended by the party in government.
