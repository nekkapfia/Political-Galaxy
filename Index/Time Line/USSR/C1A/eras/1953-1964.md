## **1953–1964: Khrushchev Thaw – Final Score: 61**

De-Stalinisation, the Secret Speech, and the emphasis on catching up with the West in living standards and technology introduced a significant pragmatic and popular-welfare element while retaining formal Marxist-Leninist orthodoxy.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* The crimes of the Stalin era were partially acknowledged, reducing the claim of continuous Party infallibility.
* Raising living standards, housing, and consumer goods became more central legitimating goals.
* Peaceful coexistence and technological competition with the West elevated performance criteria.
* Ideology remained the official language of authority, but its intensity and its capacity to justify unlimited coercion declined.
* Popular opinion and elite consensus began to matter more in practice, even if not in formal doctrine.

The transcendent source remained primary in official doctrine but lost intensity. Mundane sources (performance, popular welfare, stability) gained substantial ground.

**Key Shift:** This era marked the first major reduction in the intensity of transcendent ideological justification while preserving the formal monopoly of Marxism-Leninism.

### Why this produces a final score of 61
Transcendent sources were still formally dominant but significantly moderated. Mundane performance and welfare considerations became more organising in practice, producing a mixed-to-transcendent profile.
