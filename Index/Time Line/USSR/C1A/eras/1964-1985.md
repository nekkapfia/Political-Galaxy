## **1964–1985: Brezhnev Stability – Final Score: 42**

The long Brezhnev period institutionalised a system in which ideology remained obligatory rhetoric while actual legitimacy rested increasingly on stability, great-power status, and modest improvements in living standards.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Developed socialism and the leading role of the Party continued to be asserted as historical and moral truths.
* In practice, stability, predictability, and the avoidance of both Stalinist terror and Khrushchevian upheaval became central.
* Great-power status, military parity, and the international position of the USSR supplied important legitimating resources.
* Economic performance and consumer satisfaction mattered, yet systemic reform was avoided.
* Ideology functioned more as a ritual language of authority than as a living source of radical mobilisation.

Mundane sources (stability, performance, great-power interest) became clearly dominant in practice. Transcendent ideological claims persisted as formal orthodoxy but lost much of their mobilising and justificatory force.

**Key Shift:** This era completed the transition from high-intensity ideological justification to a more pragmatic, stability-oriented authoritarianism still clothed in Marxist-Leninist language.

### Why this produces a final score of 42
Mundane sources were primary in the actual functioning of the system. Residual transcendent ideology kept the score from falling into the strongly mundane range.
