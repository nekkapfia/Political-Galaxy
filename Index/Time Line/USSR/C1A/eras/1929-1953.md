## **1929–1953: Stalinist Transformation and High Stalinism – Final Score: 86**

Forced collectivisation, industrialisation, the Great Terror, and the postwar reassertion of ideological control marked the highest intensity of transcendent justification in Soviet history.

### Moral Justification Sources – Expanded Analysis

* Historical necessity and the correct Party line justified catastrophic human costs, including famine, mass deportation, and the Gulag.
* Class struggle was continuously re-invented to identify new internal enemies; loyalty to Stalin and the line became a moral test.
* Industrialisation and military strength were framed as proofs of ideological correctness and as preparation for the final historical victory of socialism.
* Individual rights, legal regularity, and popular consent were treated as irrelevant or as bourgeois survivals.
* The cult of Stalin fused personal authority with the claim to unique insight into historical laws.

The transcendent source reached its Soviet peak. Performance mattered enormously, but it was interpreted through and subordinated to the ideological framework; failure was attributed to sabotage or insufficient vigilance rather than to the framework itself.

**Key Shift:** This era pushed the logic of historical necessity and Party infallibility to its most extreme domestic expression.

### Why this produces a final score of 86
Transcendent sources were overwhelmingly dominant. Mundane considerations of popular welfare, consent, or even basic competence were systematically overridden by ideological requirements.
