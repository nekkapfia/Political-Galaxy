# Introduction to the Empirical Political Slider System

## Purpose and Philosophy

The Empirical Political Slider System is a structured framework for analysing political systems, ideologies, and historical periods across multiple independent dimensions. Rather than reducing complex political realities to a single left–right axis or broad ideological labels, the system uses a set of precisely defined, orthogonal sliders. Each slider measures a specific, observable aspect of how power, rights, status, resources, and authority are allocated within a society.

The core purpose of the system is to enable **clear, comparable, and evidence-based analysis** that minimises ambiguity, moral loading, and definitional drift. By breaking political reality into multiple narrow, well-bounded dimensions, the framework allows for more precise mapping of continuity and change over time, as well as more rigorous comparison between different countries, regimes, and ideologies.

## Core Principles

The system is built on several foundational principles:

- **Empirical Priority**: Scoring and analysis are based on observable reality — what is actually done in law, policy, and institutional practice — rather than on official rhetoric, self-description, or stated intentions.
- **Precise and Limited Definitions**: Each slider has a narrowly defined scope. Definitions are deliberately restricted to specific areas of allocation or behaviour and do not expand into adjacent concepts.
- **Orthogonality**: The sliders are designed to measure distinct dimensions. While real-world phenomena may touch multiple sliders, each dimension is kept conceptually and analytically separate to avoid conflation.
- **Non-Moralising Measurement**: The extremes of each slider represent theoretical endpoints rather than moral judgements. The system aims to describe patterns of allocation and organisation, not to assign moral value.
- **Relativism through Clear Anchors**: Each slider includes theoretical extremes (typically scored as 0 and 100) to provide clear reference points, even though real-world cases rarely reach these absolutes.

## Methodological Foundations

### Observable Allocation and Behaviour

The system focuses on how societies and regimes actually allocate outcomes, rights, responsibilities, status, and authority. This includes both formal rules (laws, constitutions, institutional policies) and observable patterns of enforcement and practice. Rhetorical claims, ideological self-descriptions, and justifications are treated as secondary or irrelevant unless they directly shape allocation mechanisms.

### Holistic but Weighted Assessment

When evaluating a period, regime, or political actor, the analysis considers the full range of relevant domains (law, education, employment, institutional governance, etc.). However, not all domains carry equal weight. Core domains — particularly law, institutional rules, and the allocation of significant rights or opportunities — are given primary importance, while secondary domains provide supporting context.

### Handling Mixed and Anomalous Cases

Real-world systems are rarely pure. When a case contains both individualist and group-based elements, or both restrictive and permissive policies, the scorer must evaluate the overall pattern using the principle of **primacy** — the predominant, consistent, and impactful direction across the most important domains. Anomalous or contradictory elements are acknowledged but do not override the dominant pattern unless they are themselves significant in scope and effect.

### Flexible but Bounded Judgement

The bands on each slider are guidelines rather than rigid mathematical thresholds. They exist to support consistent judgement while allowing for nuance. When a case sits near a boundary or contains genuinely mixed characteristics, the scorer exercises reasoned judgement based on the totality of evidence, with clear justification.

## Structure of the System

The framework consists of multiple sliders organised into thematic groups (such as Autonomy, Sovereignty, and various Cultural dimensions). Each slider is treated as an independent axis of analysis. This multi-dimensional structure allows for:

- More accurate representation of political complexity than single-axis models.
- Better identification of continuity and change across different domains.
- Clearer comparison between different countries and time periods.
- Reduced risk of conflating distinct political phenomena.

## Working with Individual Sliders

Each slider within the system follows the same general methodological rules but has its own precisely defined scope. When working with a specific slider, the following approach is used:

- The definition is kept narrow and operational.
- Scoring focuses on observable mechanisms of allocation or treatment.
- The slider is analysed independently from other dimensions, even when real-world cases show interaction between them.
- Bands are interpreted with reference to empirical patterns rather than abstract ideals.

## Application to Historical and Contemporary Analysis

The system can be applied to both historical periods (divided into coherent eras) and contemporary political actors (such as parties or governments). In both cases, the goal is the same: to map how allocation principles have operated or are operating, using consistent definitions and evidence-based scoring.

When analysing historical periods, eras are segmented according to meaningful shifts in allocation patterns. When analysing current political parties or actors, the focus remains on stated positions, proposed policies, and (where relevant) implemented practices.

## Summary

The Empirical Political Slider System provides a disciplined, multi-dimensional approach to political analysis. By prioritising precise definitions, empirical evidence, and orthogonal dimensions, it aims to reduce ambiguity and improve the quality of comparative and historical political research. Each individual slider operates within this shared methodological framework while maintaining its own narrowly defined area of measurement.

# Slider Definitions

---

## Core 1 – Autonomy

**1A – Personal Autonomy**  
Measures the observable degree to which the system interferes with or imposes restrictions on the individual's personal autonomy — the range of voluntary actions, speech, choices, thoughts, beliefs, and physiological processes which the individual is permitted to control without systemic prevention or interference. Tracks only systemic invasiveness.

**1B – Economic Autonomy**  
Measures the observable degree to which a system protects or restricts individuals' and private entities' economic autonomy — the range of voluntary economic actions, contracts, property rights, production, trade, investment, and consumption decisions that remain free from state or collective coercion.

---

## Core 2 – Sovereignty

**2A – National Sovereignty**  
Measures the observable degree to which a nation maintains or surrenders control over its own territory, borders, laws, institutions, currency, military, and decision-making authority versus ceding that control to external entities, supranational organisations, or international agreements.

**2B – International Sovereignty**  
Measures the observable degree to which a nation asserts or refrains from interference in the internal affairs of other nations — the range of diplomatic, economic, military, or cultural actions used to influence, pressure, or control foreign governments, societies, or policies versus strict non-intervention and respect for other nations’ sovereignty.

---

## Cultural 1 – Foundation

**1A – Foundation**  
Measures the observable origin of a culture's moral grammar — the degree to which core moral norms derive from transcendent/religious sources versus secular/immanent sources (human reason, empirical utility, social contract, or material conditions).

**1B – Dogmatism**  
Dogmatism measures the observable degree to which a system treats its foundational moral, cultural, and ideological principles as closed — both to internal revision or challenge, and to engagement with or legitimacy for alternative moral frameworks originating outside the in-group. It tracks institutional, legal, educational, and cultural mechanisms that protect core principles from change while rejecting or delegitimizing external alternatives.

---

## Cultural 2 – Identity & Belonging

**2A – Individuality**  
Measures the observable degree to which a system treats individuals as sovereign moral and legal agents versus subordinating them to group identity, collective outcomes, or ascribed categories.

**2B – Pride (National Identity)**  
Measures the observable emotional and cultural stance a system takes toward the nation itself — its history, symbols, founding myths, people, continuity, and collective identity (pride, absence of pride, guilt, or active shame).

---

## Cultural 3 – Exclusivity

**3A – Ethnicity**  
Ethnic Exclusivity measures the strictness of the ancestral / blood requirement for full membership of the core in-group. It covers the difficulty and extent of ancestral purity demanded, together with the degree of exclusion based on non-group ancestry or the degree of inclusion based on any ancestral connection to the group.

**3B – Culture**  
Cultural Exclusivity measures the strictness of the cultural adoption and transformation required for full membership of the core in-group. It covers the difficulty and extent of immersion in the group's culture and way of life, together with the degree of severance from previous cultural groups, traditions and heritage.

---

## Cultural 4 – Structure

**4A – Social Determinism**  
Measures the degree to which circumstances of birth (such as race, class, social status) limit, restrict or guide a persons life and the degree to which people are able to improve their status and/or the status of their descendents through their actions.

**4B – Equity Lens**  
Measures the observable framework a system uses to explain differential group outcomes — the degree to which it attributes disparities to systemic oppression (requiring equity interventions) versus individual actions, merit, and personal responsibility.

---

## Cultural 5 – Change

**5A – Tradition vs Progress**  
Measures how the system views traditions and the extent to which it attempts to preserve, modernise, or replace them in favor of new ideals, innovation, adaptation, and future-oriented change.

**5B – Gradualism vs Radicalism**  
Measures the observable pace and intensity with which a system pursues cultural, institutional, and social change — favoring incremental, evolutionary, cautious modifications versus rapid, revolutionary, disruptive transformations.

### Note on Slider Definitions
Slider Definitions are exact and highly limited, they do not expand beyond the specifically outlined areas they claim to. Often this means the slider definition deviates from the standard "Colloquial" understanding of the words.

### Note on Axis Extremes
The ends of each axis represent theoretical extremes. In practice, real-world systems rarely reach the absolute 0 or 100 points. These extremes are included by design: they create clear reference points at both ends of the scale, enabling more accurate relativism and removing any ambiguity about where the measurement begins. The extremes are designed to be the point at which "more" and "less" cease to have meaning for that slider, they do not impart moral weight and are non-judgemental. Where wording is used which may seem judgemental it is an observation of what that is generally used to describe it by the population at large rather than to impart the moral implications on it.

### Note on Slider Scaling
The scale on each slider is non-linear and fully asymmetric. Contraction/expansion is permitted on one side only, on both sides, or on neither side, depending on the actual distribution of real-world ideologies on that axis. The exact location, width, and degree of any contraction or expansion is fluid from slider to slider and is calibrated solely to capture the empirical density of ideological variation on that specific core. The only mandatory principle is that the meaningful range—where actual ideological differentiation occurs—is expanded to occupy the majority of the visible scale, while any theoretically sparse or empty zones (whether at the extremes or in the middle) may be contracted as required.

### Application Rules
- All societies are assessed holistically across all major domains.
- Hierarchical weighting applies: core domains such as law, education curricula, and institutional governance carry primary weight; secondary domains provide supporting evidence.
- When a system contains mixed or anomalous elements, evaluate the score in its totality. Anomalous elements are weighed according to their actual observable scope, consistency, and effect; they neither dominate nor are ignored.
- Blurred or borderline systems are placed in natural overlap zones.
- Where a system/ideology is limited due to confliciting external pressure it should be judged based on what it does in areas it does have primacy.

### Methodological Principles
- Scoring is based **exclusively on empirical reality** — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.
- Holistic assessment across all major domains with hierarchical weighting.
- Observable empirical reality always takes precedence over official rhetoric, self-labeling, or constitutional claims.

### Final Guidelines
**Specifics: Guidelines**  
All bands are flexible guidelines rather than strict brackets. This allows nuanced judgment when [topic] intensity varies across issues or domains, with primacy (predominant observable pattern, cumulative impact, breadth, and consistency) being the decisive factor. It does not allow for flexibility of the bands and individual policies will always fall within a predetermined band. In reality society and ideology will contain a mix of policies and actions which vary across several bands for a single slider, in this case the scorer is required to exersize their own judgement as it relates to the totallity of the system.
The framework functions as a flexible guideline system, not a rigid formula.

# Core 1A: Personal Autonomy

## Core 1A Definition

This guideline measures the observable degree to which the system interferes with or imposes restrictions on the individual's personal autonomy — the range of voluntary actions, speech, choices, thoughts, beliefs, and physiological processes which the individual is permitted to control without systemic prevention or interference. It tracks **only** the level of systemic invasiveness: the degree, scope, and breadth of restrictions the system enacts and enforces. It does **not** measure the individual's actual experienced autonomy in daily life, nor does it evaluate natural or personal choices that may reduce autonomy (such as having children, marriage, or employment decisions). 

All laws and enforced restrictions are treated as negative factors that increase systemic invasiveness, although not all carry equal weight. The slider does not care for the moral implications for the law/restriction only it's existance.

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, education curricula, employment practices, family policy, media narratives, institutional governance, public rituals, and daily cultural reflexes) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Endos/Xenos (Optional Split)

This slider includes an optional split measurement unique to Core 1A. In addition to the standard combined score, the guideline permits reporting personal autonomy as two separate values using the **Endos/Xenos** pair when the system applies markedly different levels of autonomy to different groups living under it.

- **Endos:** Measures the personal autonomy granted to those the system identifies as part of the core in-group (“us”).
- **Xenos:** Measures the personal autonomy granted to those the system identifies as outside the core in-group (“not us”) who are nevertheless living under the system.

Both Endos and Xenos use the exact same 0–100 scale, measurement criteria, band guidelines, and empirical-reality standards as the standard combined slider. The split is applied only when there is significant and consistent observable divergence in treatment between the in-group and out-group.

## Scoring Bands

### 95–100 — Minimalist Anarchy
Absolute or near-absolute protection of personal autonomy from **systemic** control across all domains.

- **100** = The system has no laws and imposses no restrictions of any kind. Individuals are free from system interference preventing them from taking any action they want.
- **99** = The only laws that exist are those designed to protect the individual from the system itself.
- **97–98** = The only laws that exist are those that protect the individual from the system and those that allow the system to protect itself from destruction.
- **95–96** = Near-total personal autonomy, with the only restrictions being those that prevent the infliction of severe, lifelong harm on others (e.g., murder).

### 90–95 - Strictly direct harm protections only
The system limits itself to intervention only where doing so would protect someone from direct immediate harm.
- Laws and institutions restrict interference solely to preventing direct, observable harm to others (e.g. violence).
- No restrictions are placed on self-regarding voluntary actions, speech, or lifestyle choices.

### 82–89 — Laws protecting vulnerable individuals
The systems rules begin to introduce the concept of protecting those who are vulnerable from being abused or exploited.
- Restrictions target only clear exploitation of those unable to consent (young children, mentally incapacitated).
- No laws restricting the actions of consenting adults.
- Systems exist only to defend the vulnerable from exploitation by others.

### 76–81 — Protection against exploitation
The system is limited to policing activities designed to render people into a state which would vulnerable to the exploitation of others. While engaging in the acts themselves are not prohibited the system does target those facilitating the acts.
- Restrictions target third-party actions that facilitate or exploit pseudo-vulnerable states in others.
- The self-regarding act by the consenting adult remains fully legal.
- The system intervenes only against those enabling, supplying, or profiting from the exploitation of others.

### 70–75 — Restriction of self-regarding pseudo-vulnerable choices
The system begins preventing individuals from engaging in activities which would render them vulnerable to the predation of others.
- Restrictions extend to the individual’s own self-regarding voluntary actions framed as creating pseudo-vulnerability or self-harm.
- Laws begin to prohibit behaviours judged harmful primarily to the self.
- Observable paternalistic controls on personal lifestyle and consumption choices increase.

### 60–69 — Betterment of society restrictions
The system starts engaging in the prevention of activities which are seen as detrimental society rather than to the protection of specific individuals.
- Laws move beyond protecting people and begin targeting disruptive or annoying behaviour.
- Policing begins to become active rather than reactive.
- Government monitoring and surveillance starts to appear.

### 51–59 — Paternalism / self-betterment laws
The system begins imposing restrictions framed around protecting or improving the individual. Actions which the system deems would be harmful for the individual are legislated against and legally enforced.
- Laws start targeting personal choices judged harmful primarily to the self.
- Observable increase in controls on lifestyle and daily behaviour.

### 44–50 — Early ideological/identity-based controls
The system starts policing external personal self-expression based on ideological or moral criteria. 
- Institutional rules start requiring conformity in speech and self-presentation.
- Observable shift toward early ideological conformity requirements in institutional settings.
- This does not extend to internal beliefs only their expression.

### 37–43 — Societal/cultural/behavioural conformity
The system starts policing those who do things it consideres to be directly damaging to the system and society at large.
- Language begins to become enforced.
- Laws begin to become too complex and extensive for regular citizens to understand.
- Police begin actively using entrapment to tempt people to commit crimes.

### 28–36 — Ideological enforcement and police state
The system aggressively polices ideological deviation through laws, monitoring, and institutional mechanisms.
- Laws that criminalise or penalise dissenting speech or beliefs.
- Laws are now extensive enough that everyone is guilty of something which the system can use to persecute its detractors.
- Access to information which is critical or undesirable is curtailed.

### 19–27 — Ideological reprogramming / thought control
The system uses institutional power to target individual beliefs and worldviews, applying pressure or sanctions aimed at forcing alignment with official ideology.
- Mandatory training or re-education programs designed to correct "incorrect" beliefs or attitudes.
- Sanctions or persecution for expressing views that deviate from the required orthodoxy.
- Education and media systems used to promote the official ideology and discourage or suppress alternatives.

### 10–18 — Extreme restrictions on voluntary actions, movement and association
The system imposes severe, systematic physical restrictions on the voluntary movement, residence, and daily activities of members of the general public.
 - Forced unpaid labour.
 - Detention or confinement of individuals.
 - Curfews, movement bans, or strict travel restrictions.
 - Physical barriers, checkpoints, or enforced segregation.

### 0–9 (Floor – total dominion)
The system interferes with all aspects of a persons lives. 
- **7–9** = Extreme low autonomy: control extends to most voluntary actions with heavy restrictions on all aspects of life.
- **6** = Total control over all voluntary aspects of a person's life.
- **5** = Systematic extermination of designated groups (genocide)
- **4** = Forced Medical Experimentation, Extermination through Torture.
- **3** = Control extends into involuntary actions and physiological processes
- **1–2** = Near-absolute control of involuntary actions and physiological processes.
- **0** = Absolute theoretical control: the system enforces total dominion over the individual’s body, mind, and actions with zero personal autonomy remaining. Every physiological process, thought, voluntary and involuntary action is controlled by the state.

# Core 1B: Economic Autonomy Source Document

## Core 1B Definition

This guideline measures the observable degree to which a system protects or restricts individuals' economic autonomy — the range of voluntary economic actions, contracts, property rights, production, trade, investment, and consumption decisions that remain free from state or collective coercion. It tracks the boundary between voluntary market mechanisms versus coercive state control over resources, labour, capital, and economic outcomes. Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, taxation, regulation, licensing, nationalisation patterns, price/wage controls, inheritance rules, employment practices, and statistical outcomes of resource allocation) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Taxation Rule

Taxation is treated as a separate component rather than part of the main Regulation slider. Two methods exist for calculating the Taxation Score.

**Primary Method (Standard Formula)**

$$
\text{Taxation Score} = \left(1 - \frac{\text{Government Tax Income}}{\text{GDP}}\right) \times 100
$$

This is the default method and should be used whenever reliable data on tax revenue and GDP is available.

## Alternative Method for Simplistic Economies

When the standard method is inappropriate, impractical, or impossible to apply in a meaningful way — such as in pre-modern, pre-monetary, tribal, feudal, or extreme command economies — the scorer may instead use the following alternative:

**Alternative Method**  
The Taxation Score is calculated as the **inverse of the effective tithe rate**:

**Taxation Score = 100 − Effective Tithe Rate**

The *effective tithe rate* represents the scorer’s best estimate of the proportion of output, labour, or resources that was compulsorily extracted by the governing authority.

**Requirements When Using the Alternative Method**  
If the scorer chooses to use this alternative method, they must:

1. Briefly explain **why** the standard tax-to-GDP method was considered inappropriate or unworkable for the society or period in question.
2. Explain, at a reasonable level of detail, **how** they estimated the effective tithe rate.

There is no fixed formula for calculating the effective tithe rate. The scorer is expected to use their judgment based on available historical evidence.

This alternative method exists specifically to allow sensible scoring in cases where modern economic statistics and concepts are either unavailable or would produce misleading results. Use of this method is at the scorer’s discretion.

# Economic Autonomy Axis

**Definition**: This axis scores economic systems based on the **economic autonomy of individuals** inside the system, specifically their ability to decide *with whom, when, and how* they engage in economic activities (voluntary exchange, contracts, production, investment, consumption, and resource allocation).

The scale measures the degree of protection for individual economic decision-making versus coercive state controls, ownership, direction, or prescription. It is mechanism-focused (institutions, rules, and structures) rather than outcome- or intention-based. It aligns with the framework's principles of empirical evaluation, non-linear/asymmetric scaling, and individual autonomy.

Government spending is evaluated through its observable effects on voluntary economic activity (e.g. crowding-out, subsidies, state enterprises, welfare scale, and industrial policy). High non-core spending generally pulls the Regulation Score toward the lower part of the relevant band, even if financed partly by debt or other means.

Within each band, score toward the upper end when state interventions are minimal in scope, temporary, narrowly targeted, and/or easily reversible. Score toward the lower end when interventions are broader, more permanent, more directive, or create significant path dependency. State ownership, price/wage controls, industrial policy, and large-scale welfare provision generally pull the score downward within the applicable band.

## Regulation Scoring Scale

**100** = Total, explicit, and universal protection of economic autonomy in every domain with no coercive state controls or rhetoric permitted. There is zero direct state ownership, zero direction of resources or capital, and zero prescriptive regulation. Government spending is negligible and limited to the absolute minimum required for basic order.

**98–99** = Near-total economic autonomy. Only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined state interventions are permitted. Direct state ownership or direction of resources and capital is virtually non-existent. Spending exists only to prevent the collapse or destruction of the governing system itself. Purely defensive.

**96–97** = Extreme economic autonomy legitimation. Voluntary market mechanisms dominate law, employment, investment, and resource allocation with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement. Direct state control over resources and capital is extremely limited. Government spending is very low and focused almost entirely on core public goods.

**95** = Absolute or near-absolute economic autonomy across all domains, with zero tolerance for coercive state intervention in voluntary economic activity. Direct state ownership and direction of resources and capital is minimal. Government spending is extremely limited.

**83–94** = Markets are the dominant force. The state actively reduces regulation and welfare spending in an effort to increase competition and individual responsibility. There is significant privatisation of state-owned companies and a reduction in government intervention in labour markets and social support. Individuals and firms have high freedom to enter contracts, own property, produce, trade, invest, and consume with minimal state direction.

**72–82** = Markets remain the primary mechanism for production and distribution. The state plays a more active role with rules around employment conditions, workplace safety, and environmental standards. The government collects significant taxes to fund benefits and services. Individuals face some constraints on economic decisions but retain considerable freedom to engage in voluntary exchange.

**61–71** = Markets are the main way goods and services are produced, but the state maintains a very large welfare system funded by high taxes. There are strong legal protections for workers and extensive public services. The state not only regulates markets but also tries to influence economic outcomes more directly through taxes, subsidies, and regulation. The government often owns or heavily influences key sectors.

**50–60** = Economic activity is structured through formal cooperation between the state, large businesses, and labour organisations. The government plays a strong role in setting wages, prices, and production targets in key sectors. The state actively steers economic development by identifying strategic sectors and using policy tools to help them grow. There is significant coordination between government and large companies.

**39–49** = The government takes a leading role in deciding which industries should grow and provides support through subsidies, cheap credit, and state investment. Significant parts of the economy are owned or controlled by the state or state-linked organisations, while market mechanisms still operate in many areas.

**28–38** = Market-based system guided by religious or ethical principles. Markets operate as the main mechanism of exchange, but economic activity is also shaped by religious or moral rules. The state may enforce some of these principles. Markets remain central, but the state imposes extensive regulations and incentives to reduce environmental damage and promote sustainability. Economic activity is heavily shaped by environmental rules, taxes on pollution, and support for green technologies.

**17–27** = The state has extensive control over production, investment, and labour allocation. It uses regulation, ownership, and planning to pursue specific political or national goals. The entire economy is organised around achieving specific national or military objectives. The state directs production, controls resources, and often imposes restrictions on consumption and labour.

**6–16** = The state owns most major industries and creates detailed plans for what should be produced, in what quantities, and at what prices. Factories, farms, and other workplaces are owned and managed by the workers themselves through democratic organisations. There is little or no private ownership of large-scale production. Coordination between different workplaces may occur through federations rather than a central state.

**5** = Near-absolute state economic control. The state owns or directs essentially all significant resources, capital, and production. Any remaining private economic activity is marginal, heavily restricted, and exists only under direct state oversight or permission. There is effectively no independent private economic decision-making of consequence.

**3–4** = Total state economic control with only the most minimal and symbolic residual private activity. Virtually every meaningful economic decision regarding resources, capital, production, and distribution is directed by the state. The distinction between state and economy is almost entirely collapsed.

**1–2** = Absolute state economic control. There is no private economic action of any meaningful kind. All resources, capital, production, distribution, and economic decisions are fully directed by the state or collective. The distinction between the state and the economy has completely collapsed.

**0** = Theoretical floor. Complete and total state economic control with no private economic activity possible under any circumstances.

## Final Scoring

The final Core 1B score is calculated by combining the **Regulation Score** and the **Taxation Score** using the following method:

Start with the arithmetic mean:

$$
A = \frac{\text{Regulation Score} + \text{Taxation Score}}{2}
$$

$$
L = \min(\text{Regulation Score}, \text{Taxation Score})
$$

$$
d = \frac{|\text{Regulation Score} - \text{Taxation Score}|}{100}
$$

Then apply:

$$
F = (1 - d) \cdot A + d \cdot L
$$

The final score \( F \) is rounded up to the nearest whole number. This conservative rounding convention ensures the final score is never lower than the calculated mathematical result.

# Core 2A: National Sovereignty

## Core 2A Definition

This guideline measures the observable degree to which a nation maintains or surrenders control over its own territory, borders, laws, institutions, currency, military, and decision-making authority versus ceding that control to external entities, supranational organisations, or international agreements. It tracks the boundary between national self-determination and external governance. Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in border policy, immigration law, treaty obligations, supranational legal supremacy, monetary policy, military command structures, and daily institutional practice) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Absolute or near-absolute national sovereignty with zero tolerance for external control over core national functions. This allows observable variation within different high-sovereignty systems (different mechanisms of border and institutional closure while retaining total rejection of external authority).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal maintenance of national sovereignty in every domain with no ceded authority or rhetoric permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total national sovereignty; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined external connections permitted.
- **90–94** = Extreme sovereignty legitimation: national control dominates law, borders, military, currency, and institutions with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict closure with minimal controlled connection points)
- Borders are tightly controlled with very limited legal immigration, strict vetting, and minimal asylum claims.
- No supranational legal authority overrides national law; treaties are subordinate to domestic constitutions.
- Military and foreign policy remain fully independent with no foreign bases or command integration.
- Currency and monetary policy are exclusively national with no external oversight.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Predominantly closed with limited managed connection points)
- Borders maintain strong controls but allow limited, highly managed immigration and trade routes.
- International agreements exist but contain explicit sovereignty safeguards and exit clauses.
- Military cooperation is bilateral and revocable; no permanent loss of command authority.
- National institutions retain primacy over external bodies in all core decisions.

### 60–69 (Mid – Moderate number and accessibility of connection points)
- Noticeable increase in managed immigration channels, trade agreements, and international institutions.
- Some areas of law (e.g., trade or environmental rules) show partial external influence.
- Military or intelligence sharing occurs under national oversight but with growing integration.
- National decision-making remains dominant but faces observable external pressure in specific domains.

### 51–59 (Mid–Lower – Noticeable increase in connection points and ceded control)
- Multiple significant treaties or organisations begin to constrain national policy options.
- Supranational courts or bodies gain limited but enforceable authority in defined areas.
- Border controls loosen with higher legal immigration volumes and reduced vetting in practice.
- Monetary or fiscal policy shows measurable influence from external institutions.

### 41–50 (Low–Mid – Multiple significant connection points)
- Several core functions (trade, migration, regulation) operate under substantial external agreements.
- National law is frequently subordinated to international or supranational rules.
- Military alliances involve shared command structures or foreign basing rights.
- Observable transfer of decision-making power away from national institutions.

### 31–40 (Low – High number and accessibility of connection points)
- Large volumes of uncontrolled or weakly managed cross-border flows.
- Extensive ceding of authority to supranational bodies across multiple policy areas.
- National institutions function more as implementing agencies than independent decision-makers.
- Sovereignty erosion is visible in daily governance and legal outcomes.

### 21–30 (Very Low – Very high number and accessibility of connection points)
- Borders approach functional openness with minimal effective controls.
- Most major policy areas are shaped or constrained by external organisations.
- National military and monetary sovereignty are heavily compromised or shared.
- External authority becomes the de facto dominant force in many domains.

### 11–20 (Extreme Low – Near-total absence of closure)
- National borders and institutions exercise almost no independent control.
- Supranational or foreign entities dictate core policies with little national veto power.
- Observable dissolution of distinct national decision-making capacity.

### 0–10 (Floor – Total Surrender) — Compressed zone
Complete surrender of national sovereignty with zero effective independent control. This allows observable variation within different post-sovereign systems (different mechanisms of external governance while retaining total rejection of national autonomy).

- **6–10** = Extreme surrender: national institutions exist in name only; external bodies exercise direct authority over borders, law, and military.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute surrender: virtually all sovereign functions have been transferred to supranational or foreign entities.
- **0** = Absolute surrender: the nation ceases to function as a sovereign entity; every core function is fully controlled externally.

## Core 2B Definition

This guideline measures the observable degree to which a nation asserts or refrains from interference in the internal affairs of other nations — the range of diplomatic, economic, military, or cultural actions used to influence, pressure, or control foreign governments, societies, or policies versus strict non-intervention and respect for other nations’ sovereignty. 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in foreign policy, military interventions, sanctions regimes, aid conditions, regime-change efforts, cultural export programs, and international legal actions) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Absolute or near-absolute non-intervention with zero tolerance for interference in other nations’ internal affairs. This allows observable variation within different high-sovereignty systems (different mechanisms of strict non-interference while retaining total rejection of outward control).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal non-intervention in every domain with no interference actions or rhetoric permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total non-intervention; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined exceptions permitted.
- **90–94** = Extreme non-intervention legitimation: respect for foreign sovereignty dominates foreign policy, military, and diplomacy with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement.

### 80–89 (High – Strict non-intervention with minimal controlled exceptions)
- Military force or covert operations are used only in direct self-defence with no regime-change or humanitarian interventions.
- Sanctions are rare and limited to clear, immediate threats; no secondary sanctions on third parties.
- Aid and diplomacy are unconditional and never used to reshape foreign governments or societies.
- Cultural or ideological export is strictly voluntary with no state-backed pressure.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Predominantly non-intervention with limited managed exceptions)
- Occasional limited sanctions or diplomatic pressure occur only in response to direct attacks on national interests.
- Military cooperation remains defensive and does not extend to internal affairs of other states.
- Aid programs avoid political conditionality; cultural exchanges remain neutral.
- National institutions maintain clear boundaries against outward interference.

### 60–69 (Mid – Moderate number and accessibility of interference actions)
- Targeted sanctions and diplomatic pressure are used more frequently against selected regimes.
- Limited military or intelligence support is provided to allies in internal conflicts.
- Aid and trade agreements begin to include governance or human-rights conditions.
- Cultural diplomacy shows mild ideological promotion without full coercion.

### 51–59 (Mid–Lower – Noticeable increase in interference actions)
- Sanctions regimes expand to multiple countries with broader economic impact.
- Covert or overt support for opposition groups or regime-change efforts becomes observable.
- International legal actions or tribunals are used to pressure foreign governments.
- Aid and development programs carry explicit political or ideological conditions.

### 41–50 (Low–Mid – Multiple significant interference actions)
- Frequent use of economic sanctions, proxy support, or diplomatic isolation against multiple states.
- Military interventions or no-fly zones occur in foreign internal conflicts.
- Regime-change rhetoric is accompanied by concrete funding or training of opposition forces.
- Cultural and media operations actively promote internal destabilization in target nations.

### 31–40 (Low – High number and accessibility of interference actions)
- Interference becomes routine across many regions through sanctions, proxy wars, or colour-revolution support.
- National resources are regularly devoted to reshaping foreign societies and governments.
- International institutions are leveraged as tools for outward pressure rather than neutral forums.

### 21–30 (Very Low – Dominance of interventionism)
- Most foreign policy centres on active intervention, regime change, or ideological export.
- Large-scale military operations or sustained sanctions target internal affairs of multiple nations.
- Cultural and economic tools are systematically used to undermine foreign sovereignty.

### 11–20 (Extreme Low – Severe outward control)
- The system routinely exerts direct or indirect control over multiple foreign governments.
- Military occupations, puppet regimes, or permanent sanctions regimes are common.
- National institutions operate as instruments of global ideological enforcement.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute interventionism) — Compressed zone
Total commitment to outward interference and control with zero respect for other nations’ sovereignty. This allows observable variation within different interventionist systems (different mechanisms of domination while retaining total rejection of non-interference).

- **6–10** = Extreme interventionism: active attempts to reshape or control multiple foreign societies through military, economic, and cultural means with extreme sanctions for resistance.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute interventionism: systematic global projection of power with almost no restraint on interfering in internal affairs.
- **0** = Absolute interventionism: zero tolerance for foreign sovereignty; every nation is treated as subject to the system’s ideological or strategic direction with total monitoring and enforcement.

**End of Core 2B: International Sovereignty**

# Cultural 1A – Foundation (0–50 Absence Balance Scale)

---

**Scope (Exact and Limited)**  
This slider measures **only** the observable sources actually invoked in moral justification and argumentation — i.e., what foundations people or institutions treat as legitimate bases for moral claims in law, education, governance, and public moral reasoning.  

It does **not** measure:  
- Enforcement, punishment, or coercion mechanisms  
- Power dynamics, suppression, exclusion, or marginalisation in practice  
- Cultural hegemony, dominance, or broader social/cultural influence  
- Rhetoric, stated claims, self-reported beliefs, or intentions (only what sources are actually used)  
- Outcomes, downstream effects, or policy results  
- The *content* of specific moral rules or prohibitions (these belong in Core 1A – Personal Autonomy)  
- The *intensity* with which the moral foundation is enforced or dissent suppressed (this belongs in Cultural 1B – Dogmatism)  
- The *degree or existence* of constraints on roles, outcomes, or life circumstances based on circumstances of birth (ethnicity, socioeconomic status, caste, disabilities, sexuality, etc.) — this belongs in Cultural 4A – Social Determinism.  
  However, the *source of the moral justification* for such constraints (transcendent vs secular) **is** measured by Cultural 1A.

Scoring is based solely on empirical patterns of which sources appear (or do not appear) in moral reasoning. The slider stays strictly within this defined scope at all times.

The slider measures the degree to which **one source of moral legitimacy is absent from the moral conversation**. 

Transcendent sources include divine command, sacred texts, revelation, heavenly authority, Gaia, mother nature, or equivalent non-worldly foundations.  
Worldly/secular sources include evidence, science, history, tradition (as empirical or reasoned inheritance), secular thought, reason, and debate.

Low scores reflect both sources being actively present and integrated in the moral conversation. High scores reflect increasing absence of one source. 

**50 is the complete theoretical extreme** — total conceptual absence. It represents the pure pole where one source has **zero conceptual existence** in the moral framework (not rejected or excluded, but never having entered the worldview at all — equivalent to a mind that has never encountered the concept). Real-world entities score below this pole.

When scoring, note which source (transcendent or worldly/secular) is absent or reduced.

## Universal Application Rules
- Focus exclusively on observable presence or absence of each source in moral justification and argumentation (law, education, governance carry primary weight).
- Bands are flexible guidelines. Final placement uses totality judgment: predominant pattern of presence/absence, cumulative impact, breadth across domains, consistency, and primacy of the sources actually invoked.
- Scoring uses empirical reality only — what sources are actually referenced in arguments and decisions.
- Score 4 floor as practical minimum for real-world cases.
- Axis extremes are theoretical reference points.

# Cultural 1A: Foundation Bands

## 50 (Theoretical Extreme – Total Conceptual Absence)
One source constitutes the entire moral universe. The other source has **zero conceptual existence** within it. It is not present, not rejected, not excluded, and not even available as a category of thought. The moral framework has no language, mechanism, or space for the absent source — it has never entered the worldview in any form, equivalent to a mind that has never encountered the concept at all.

- All moral claims are justified solely and exclusively by direct reference to the one source. There is no conceptual room for the absent source to be invoked, considered, or dismissed.
- **Example**: A moral system in which the world and all justification is understood exclusively through one lens (e.g., pure divine command or sacred authority), where the very idea of evidence-based or secular moral reasoning has never existed as a concept. Or the pure reverse: a moral system in which only empirical reason and observable evidence exist as categories of moral legitimacy, with no conceptual category for transcendent, faith-based, or revelatory sources having ever been part of moral thought.

## 46–49
One source is almost entirely absent **in practice** from the moral conversation of an otherwise functioning moral system. It may appear in isolated, trivial, or non-substantive contexts but has no meaningful presence in actual moral reasoning or decisions.

- The absent source is nearly completely excluded from core domains and most substantive discussion, surviving only in the most marginal and residual contexts. This is the highest level of practical absence short of the theoretical pole.
- **Example**: Moral and legal reasoning in a society proceeds almost exclusively through one source, with only rare, ceremonial, or private references to the other that carry no weight.

## 41–45
One source is largely absent from the moral conversation in practice. It persists only in minor or peripheral areas and exerts no meaningful influence on primary moral reasoning or policy.

- Core domains operate without substantive engagement with the absent source.
- **Example**: Fundamental issues are discussed and resolved without invoking one source in any substantive way; it survives only in limited traditional or symbolic roles.

## 31–40
One source has significant absence from substantial areas of moral argumentation in practice. It remains present in limited or specialised domains but is routinely omitted from broader discussion.

- Major policy and ethical conversations proceed with clear gaps where one source is not engaged.
- **Example**: Public moral debates on key issues largely bypass one source, while it may still appear in narrow contexts.

## 21–30
One source is noticeably absent from meaningful portions of the moral conversation. Both sources are still invoked in some contexts, but one is frequently omitted where it could relevantly apply.

- Clear and recurring gaps appear in the moral arena.
- **Example**: Arguments on certain issue categories regularly omit one source while including the other.

## 11–20
Both sources are generally present in the moral conversation, but one source begins to be omitted from specific categories or types of discussion in a recurring manner. The overall discourse still includes both in most areas, but absence is emerging in practice within particular domains.

- Absence is emerging but limited to specific recurring contexts rather than systemic.
- **Example**: Moral reasoning combines both sources across most areas, yet one is consistently omitted from particular domains in a recurring way.

## 3–10
Both sources are actively present and integrated into the moral conversation. Minor contextual variation or differences in emphasis may occur across issues, but these do not rise to meaningful absence of either source.

- The conversation maintains regular, functional engagement with both.
- **Example**: Laws and norms are justified through ongoing combination of both sources, with neither systematically omitted from relevant discussions.

## 0–2
Both sources exist and are used in perfect harmony within the moral conversation. They carry equal weight with no variation in emphasis or contextual drop-off and are routinely invoked together as fully complementary foundations with no gaps whatsoever.

- Moral legitimacy flows from the fully integrated, perfectly balanced presence of both sources.
- **Example**: Core moral claims are justified through balanced, interdependent appeals to both transcendent/traditional authority and empirical reason/secular debate, treated as mutually reinforcing with no contextual omissions.

**Scoring Notes**

1. Determine the band and numeric score first using totality judgment.
2. **Only after** the band and score are finalised, add a directional suffix:
   - **T** = The Transcendent source is the one unaffected (transcendent remains present; mundane/secular is absent or reduced).
   - **M** = The Mundane (secular) source is the one unaffected (mundane/secular remains present; transcendent is absent or reduced).

**Examples**:
- 48T = Extremely high absence of the mundane/secular source.
- 48M = Extremely high absence of the transcendent source.
- 12T = Moderate absence of the mundane/secular source.
- 12M = Moderate absence of the transcendent source.

- Always record which source is absent/reduced when the score is above 10.
- Cross-reference the scored-policies corpus before new assignments.
- Revise on new information or clarification.

# Final Score Conversion

In order to integrate easily into the rest of the slider project, the original 0–50 score is translated into a 0–100 range using a simple formula.

**Formula:**
- T = (+1)
- M = (−1)
- **Final Score = 50 + (Original Score) × (T or M)**

**Examples:**
- 36T → 50 + 36 = **86**
- 14M → 50 − 14 = **36**
- 47M → 50 − 47 = **3**

**Interpretation:**
- Higher positive scores (closer to 100) = Stronger transcendent foundation (greater absence/reduction of mundane/secular sources).
- Scores near 50 = Greater balance between transcendent and mundane sources.
- Lower scores (closer to 0) = Stronger mundane/secular foundation (greater absence/reduction of transcendent sources).

This conversion allows the Cultural 1A score to be plotted directly on a consistent directional scale across countries and time periods.

# Dogmatism

**Source Document**  
**Date:** July 2026

## Dogmatism Definition

Dogmatism measures the observable degree to which a system treats its foundational moral, cultural, and ideological principles as closed — both to internal revision or challenge, and to engagement with or legitimacy for alternative moral frameworks originating outside the in-group. It tracks institutional, legal, educational, and cultural mechanisms that protect core principles from change while rejecting or delegitimizing external alternatives.

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

## Aspects

Rather than scoring Dogmatism as a single undifferentiated concept, scorers should analyse it through these two complementary aspects:

### Aspect 1: Orthodoxy (Internal)

**Orthodoxy** measures the observable degree to which the system treats its own foundational principles as closed to legitimate empirical challenge or revision. It tracks the strength of institutional, legal, educational, and social mechanisms that suppress, penalize, delegitimize, or pathologize open inquiry, internal dissent, or deviation from those core principles.

### Aspect 2: Tribalism (External)

**Tribalism** measures the observable degree to which the system rejects or treats as illegitimate moral frameworks and value systems that originate from outside its own group. It tracks institutional, legal, educational, and cultural mechanisms that dismiss, delegitimize, or actively oppose alternative moral systems associated with other groups or identities.

## Final Scoring

The two aspects (**Orthodoxy** and **Tribalism**) are scored independently on the 0–100 scale. The final Dogmatism score is calculated as follows:

$$
A = \frac{\text{Orthodoxy} + \text{Tribalism}}{2}
$$

$$
H = \max(\text{Orthodoxy}, \text{Tribalism})
$$

$$
d = \frac{|\text{Orthodoxy} - \text{Tribalism}|}{100}
$$

$$
F = (1 - d) \cdot A + d \cdot H
$$

The final score \( F \) is rounded **down** to the nearest whole number.

## Scoring Bands

### 96–100 — Total / Extreme Dogmatism (Compressed Extreme)
The system permits no meaningful internal challenge to its own foundational principles and no legitimacy for external moral frameworks.

- **100** = Any internal questioning of core principles or positive engagement with external moral frameworks is prevented through law, institutional rules, or direct punishment across all major domains.
- **98–99** = Only narrow, temporary, and officially approved exceptions are permitted for either internal revision or engagement with external frameworks. Unauthorized activity results in institutional or legal sanctions.
- **97** = Core principles are treated as settled and beyond internal revision. External moral frameworks are treated as illegitimate. Challenge or engagement results in professional, institutional, or legal consequences.
- **96** = Laws, institutional rules, or official policies penalize open internal challenge to foundational principles and engagement with external moral frameworks in multiple domains. Education and state-linked media present core principles as settled and external frameworks as invalid or threatening.

### 86–95 — Strong Dogmatism (Usable High Range)
The system strongly protects its own foundational principles from internal change while robustly rejecting external moral frameworks.

- Legal or institutional rules penalize public challenge to core principles and engagement with or positive reference to external moral frameworks in at least one major domain.
- Education curricula present core principles as authoritative and limit critical examination of them, and frame external moral frameworks as inferior or dangerous.
- Individuals who openly deviate from core principles or positively engage with external moral frameworks in institutional settings face professional or institutional sanctions.
- State or major institutional media frame deviation from core principles and external moral frameworks as illegitimate.

### 76–85 — Predominant Dogmatism (Usable High-Mid Range)
The system actively protects its foundational principles from major internal revision while rejecting external moral frameworks.

- Institutional or legal mechanisms discourage significant deviation from core principles and broad engagement with external moral frameworks.
- Education curricula present core principles as correct while permitting only limited discussion of alternatives.
- Public criticism of core principles by officials or in state media carries consequences.
- Public positive reference to external moral frameworks by officials or in state media carries consequences.
- Promotion or advancement within major institutions is linked to alignment with core principles and rejection of external frameworks.

### 66–75 — Moderate to Strong Dogmatism (Usable Mid-High Range)
The system protects its foundational principles from major internal change while rejecting external moral frameworks.

- Institutional mechanisms limit major revision of core principles and broad acceptance of external frameworks.
- Education curricula present core principles as established while allowing some critical discussion, and treat external moral frameworks as secondary or inferior.
- Individuals can question specific applications of core principles or engage selectively with external frameworks without automatic institutional punishment.
- State media supports core principles and rejects external frameworks but occasionally reports internal debate or external developments critically.

### 56–65 — Noticeable Dogmatism (Usable Mid Range)
The system maintains identifiable foundational principles that receive protection from major internal revision while external moral frameworks are rejected in some contexts but discussed in others.

- Some laws or institutional rules limit major revision of core principles and positive engagement with external moral frameworks.
- Education curricula include both affirmation of core principles and space for critical examination, and include critical presentation of external frameworks.
- Public figures can criticize aspects of core principles or engage with external moral frameworks without consistent institutional punishment.
- Media outlets affiliated with the system present both defense of core principles and criticism of external frameworks.

### 48–55 — Transitional Dogmatism (Usable Middle Range)
The system has recognizable foundational principles.

- Protection of core principles from major revision exists in some institutions or policy areas but is absent or weak in others.
- Rejection of external moral frameworks exists in some institutions or policy areas but is absent or weak in others.
- Education curricula contain both reinforcement of and critical perspectives on foundational principles, and both reinforcement of and critical perspectives on external moral frameworks.
- Individuals face consequences for challenging core principles in some institutions but not others, and face consequences for engaging with external moral frameworks in some institutions but not others.
- Media coverage includes both supportive and critical content regarding core principles, and both supportive and critical content regarding external moral frameworks.

### 40–47 — Moderate Openness (Usable Mid-Low Range)
The system maintains some foundational principles but generally treats them as open to internal challenge and reform while showing limited rejection of external moral frameworks.

- Few laws or institutional rules penalize criticism of core principles or engagement with external moral frameworks.
- Education curricula present core principles as subjects for analysis and debate and treat external moral frameworks as legitimate topics for examination.
- Individuals can challenge core principles or engage with external moral frameworks without systematic professional or institutional punishment.
- Media regularly publishes criticism of core principles and discussion of external moral frameworks.

### 30–39 — Low to Moderate Dogmatism (Usable Low-Mid Range)
The system has identifiable foundational principles that receive limited protection from internal revision while external moral frameworks are engaged with on relatively open terms.

- Institutional mechanisms to protect the framework and its core principles from major revision or rejection of external moral frameworks exist in some areas.
- Education curricula present core principles as established in some contexts while allowing substantial critical examination, and treat external moral frameworks as legitimate for comparison and debate.
- Public criticism or reform of core principles occurs regularly.
- Public positive engagement with external moral frameworks occurs regularly.
- Media and institutions show both defense of core principles and open discussion of external moral frameworks.

### 20–29 — Moderate Dogmatism (Usable Low Range)
The system maintains foundational principles that are defended selectively while permitting substantial internal debate, reform, and engagement with external moral frameworks.

- Core principles receive institutional protection from major revision in specific domains.
- Education curricula balance affirmation of core principles with space for critique and comparison with external moral frameworks.
- Individuals and groups can challenge or reform core principles with moderate institutional pushback.
- Individuals and groups can engage positively with external moral frameworks with moderate institutional pushback.
- Media coverage includes both support for core principles and open discussion of external moral frameworks.

### 10–19 — Moderate to Low Dogmatism (Usable Low Range)
The system has foundational principles that are acknowledged but treated as open to substantial internal challenge and evolution, while engaging openly with external moral frameworks.

- Institutional protection of core principles from major revision is present but limited.
- Education curricula present core principles as important yet subject to ongoing debate and comparison with external moral frameworks.
- Public criticism and reform of core principles are common.
- Public positive engagement with external moral frameworks is common.
- Media and institutions actively discuss and compare core principles with external moral frameworks.

### 5–9 — Low Dogmatism (Usable Low Range)
The system shows limited attachment to fixed foundational principles and accepts internal challenge while engaging with external moral frameworks.

- Institutional mechanisms to protect core principles from major revision or to reject external moral frameworks are minimal.
- Education curricula treat core principles as open to critique and reform, and present external moral frameworks as legitimate for examination and comparison.
- Public criticism of core principles occurs regularly.
- Public engagement with external moral frameworks occurs regularly.
- Media and institutions routinely debate and compare core principles with external moral frameworks.

### 0–4 — Absolute Low Dogmatism (Compressed Extreme)
This is the theoretical floor of the slider. There are no remaining mechanisms that protect core principles from internal revision or reject external moral frameworks.

- **4** = Minimal institutional protection for foundational principles against internal revision. Minimal institutional rejection of external moral frameworks.
- **3** = Institutional and cultural mechanisms discourage rigid defense of core principles. Institutional and cultural mechanisms discourage rejection of external moral frameworks.
- **1–2** = Foundational principles have no effective defense against internal revision. External moral frameworks face no institutional rejection.
- **0** = No institutional, legal, educational, or cultural mechanisms exist that can stabilize foundational principles against internal revision or reject external moral frameworks in any form.

**End of Dogmatism Slider**

# Cultural 2A: Individuality

## Cultural 2A Definition

This guideline measures the observable degree to which a system treats individuals as sovereign moral and legal agents versus subordinating them to group identity, collective outcomes, or ascribed categories. 

It tracks whether outcomes, rights, responsibilities, and status are determined primarily by individual actions, merit, character, and choices or by group membership (ethnic, racial, class, gender, caste, etc.). 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable enforcement in law, education curricula, employment and promotion practices, family and inheritance policy, media narratives, institutional governance, public rituals, and statistical outcomes of group vs individual allocation) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

### 90–100: Near-absolute individualism
The system allocates outcomes, rights, responsibilities, and status almost exclusively according to individual actions, merit, and choices. Group membership has no meaningful formal or informal role in allocation across law, institutions, or practice.

- **100**: Every outcome, right, responsibility, and status is determined solely by individual actions, merit, character, and voluntary choices with no group-based differentiation in any domain.
- **95–99**: Group considerations are absent except in the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined cases.
- **90–94**: Individual agency dominates law, education, employment, and institutions with maximum enforcement and cultural reinforcement. Group-based allocation mechanisms are extremely rare and heavily scrutinised.

### 80–89: High individualism with minimal group exceptions
Individual merit and conduct are the dominant basis for allocation. Group-based mechanisms exist but remain exceptional and do not override individualist principles in core domains.

- Group-based policies are rare and narrowly confined to specific, non-core areas.
- Core domains (employment, education, and law) operate on individual merit as the default rule.
- Institutions do not routinely balance individual performance against group identity.
- Group membership does not confer formal advantages in hiring, promotion, or admissions.

### 70–79: Predominantly individualist with confined group-based policies
Individual treatment remains the primary and default principle. Group-based allocation mechanisms exist but are confined to specific areas and do not routinely override individual merit in core processes.

- Group-based targets or preferences are present but limited to particular programmes or institutions rather than operating as standard practice.
- Law maintains individual rights as the general rule, with group accommodations remaining exceptional.
- In employment and education, individual merit is the normal basis for decisions, even where some group-based policies exist.
- Statistical outcomes continue to show individual actions as the strongest overall predictor, with group effects remaining secondary.

### 60–69: Moderate individualism with routine group-based allocation
Individual merit operates as a formal principle but group-based allocation mechanisms are routine and regularly influence outcomes across multiple domains.

- Diversity targets, affirmative action, or identity-based preferences function as standard elements in hiring, promotions, and university admissions.
- Law and institutions regularly balance individual merit against group equity objectives in decision-making.
- Group membership affects access to opportunities in multiple recurring and institutionalised contexts.
- Individual criteria are frequently weighed against or modified by group membership in practice.

### 51–59: Noticeable group influence on allocation
Group membership plays a regular and substantial role in determining outcomes and access.

- Group-based mechanisms (quotas, targets, or identity criteria) are present in multiple core domains.
- Individual merit is recognised but is weighed against group membership in institutional decisions.
- Law permits differential treatment based on group categories in several areas.
- Statistical outcomes show recurring divergence linked to group membership.

### 41–50: Significant group-based allocation
Group membership is a major factor in the allocation of rights, opportunities, and status.

- Group-based allocation mechanisms (quotas, set-asides, or identity preferences) are standard in employment and education.
- Law and institutions apply differential treatment based on ascribed group categories as normal practice.
- Individual responsibility and merit are subordinated to group outcomes in policy and enforcement.
- Institutional culture treats group membership as a primary lens for resource and status claims.

### 31–40: Strong group primacy in allocation
Group identity is the dominant factor in determining outcomes, rights, and status in most major domains.

- Quotas, collective rights, or identity-based redistribution operate as standard mechanisms.
- Law and institutions treat persons primarily as members of groups rather than as sovereign individuals.
- Individual merit or conduct has limited impact compared with group category.
- Dissent from group-based allocation frameworks carries institutional costs.

### 21–30: Very strong group subordination
The individual is largely subsumed under group identity in law and institutional practice.

- Rights, responsibilities, and access to resources are assigned predominantly according to group membership.
- Personal agency has minimal observable effect on major outcomes relative to group category.
- Institutions enforce group-based allocation as the primary organising principle.

### 11–20: Severe group collectivism
Individuality is actively suppressed in favour of group identity and collective outcomes.

- Law and institutions treat the group as the fundamental unit.
- Pursuit of individual distinction or deviation from group norms carries significant costs.
- Allocation of status and resources is overwhelmingly determined by group membership.

### 0–10: Absolute group collectivism
The individual has no meaningful independent status. Every person is defined and treated solely by group identity.

- **6–10**: Individual agency is treated as illegitimate or pathological. All major outcomes are framed and enforced through group membership.
- **1–5**: The individual has almost no independent moral or legal status outside their group category.
- **0**: Every person is defined, valued, and treated solely by group identity with no independent sphere of rights or responsibilities.

# Cultural 2B: National (Pride vs Shame)

**Source Document**  
**Date:** April 16, 2026

**Cultural 2B Definition**  
This guideline measures the observable emotional and cultural stance a system takes toward the nation itself — its history, symbols, founding myths, people, continuity, and collective identity. This includes active national/ideological pride, the absence of pride, guilt for specific actions, and active shame (inherent rejection of national identity). 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (education curricula, official media narratives, public rituals and symbols, policy language, enforcement of speech or commemoration rules, physical alterations or removals of national symbols, statistical outcomes of national pride surveys or cultural output, and daily observable expressions) — never on claims, justifications, or moral framing.

**Scoring Bands**

**90–100(Glorification)**  
The nation is mythologized as supremely virtuous, flawless, or divinely favored with virtually no room for flaws or nuance. National pride is the central emotional, moral, and cultural organizing principle of the entire society.

- **100** = The nation itself is the ultimate source of legitimacy — any action taken by the nation is inherently justified and worthy of celebration simply because it was performed by the nation; every aspect of history, symbols, and identity is presented as perfect and superior; any criticism is treated as treason or betrayal with zero tolerance.
- **95–99** = Official narratives, education, and media relentlessly celebrate the nation’s greatness and destiny; mild or private critique is heavily socially discouraged.
- **90–94** = The nation and its actions are glorified with any mistakes or injustices either reframed or suppressed; isolated historical self-reflection exists only in tightly controlled, non-public contexts.

**81–89 (Pride)**  
National pride is the clear default emotional stance and is actively promoted across institutions and culture, shaping how the nation presents itself both domestically and internationally.

- Education and media emphasize achievements, continuity, and positive founding myths with only token or heavily framed acknowledgment of flaws.
- Public rituals, symbols, and policy language celebrate the nation as a source of personal and collective meaning through frequent parades, holidays, and commemorations.
- Criticism of the nation’s past is viewed as disloyal or unpatriotic and carries observable social or institutional costs.

**72–80 (Honour)**  
Pride remains the predominant feeling but is tempered by selective or contextual self-reflection that does not undermine the overall positive national narrative.

- History is taught primarily positively but with limited, non-central coverage of controversies.
- National symbols and rituals are widely celebrated; pride is socially encouraged and seen as healthy and normal.
- Measured critique is tolerated in elite or academic settings but does not dominate public narrative or policy tone.

**63–71 (Admiration)**  
The system maintains a broadly positive national self-image, though critical perspectives are clearly present and tolerated in some areas.

- Education curricula and official media give more space to national achievements than to controversies but include some critical content.
- Official ceremonies and public rituals continue to celebrate national symbols at normal or slightly reduced frequency.
- Policy language on commemorations and national identity remains mostly factual and positive with occasional neutral or critical qualifiers.
- Cultural output (films, television, literature, music, theatre, art exhibitions, and festivals) still features national themes and positive historical portrayals at similar rates to previous decades, though some works now include ironic or critical framing.

**54–62 (Positive)**  
The system shows a roughly even mix of pride and critical perspectives without a clear dominant emotional direction, resulting in a neutral or mixed national self-image.

- Education curricula and official media allocate roughly equal factual coverage to national achievements and historical controversies, maintaining a neutral tone.
- Official ceremonies maintain standard national symbols at routine levels without added critical qualifiers or atonement elements.
- Policy language on commemorations and national identity remains factual and descriptive.
- Public rituals (ceremonies, holidays, commemorations) continue at roughly the same frequency as in previous decades.
- Cultural output (films, television, literature, music, theatre, art exhibitions, and festivals) still features national themes and positive historical portrayals at similar rates to previous decades, but these are increasingly presented with neutral, ironic, or critical framing rather than straightforward celebration.

**47–53 (Balanced)**  
The system shows a roughly even mix of pride and critical perspectives with no clear dominant emotional direction, resulting in a neutral or mixed national self-image.

- Education curricula and official media give roughly equal coverage to national achievements and historical controversies, maintaining a neutral tone.
- National flags and symbols are mainly displayed during sporting events but do not feature prominently in everyday life.
- Holidays and commemorations are observed mainly for historical and cultural reasons rather than from strong feelings of pride or shame.

**38–46 (Negative)**  
Institutions begin to place noticeably greater emphasis on national flaws and injustices than on achievements, marking a clear but not yet dominant shift in tone.

- Education curricula and official media give noticeably greater weight and framing emphasis to historical injustices or failures than to achievements.
- National symbols (flags, monuments) begin to receive reduced public display or are accompanied by contextual qualifiers in official settings.
- Policy language on commemorations increasingly incorporates atonement or critical framing while still maintaining some positive national references.
- Public rituals and cultural output show measurable decline in frequency or prominence of traditional national pride elements.

**29–37 (Shame)**  
National pride is actively treated as problematic or suspect in key institutions, while shame and atonement narratives receive strong institutional support and begin to reshape public life.

- Education curricula and media allocate significantly greater weight to national failures, oppression narratives, or atonement than to achievements.
- Public symbols and rituals show clear decline in frequency or prominence, with increasing replacement of traditional national icons.

**20–28 (Remorse)**  
National pride and historical identity are treated as largely regressive or immoral and are actively suppressed across most domains.

- Explicit bans or heavy penalties on expressions of national pride and mandatory shame-based re-education programs are implemented.
- National symbols are dismantled or heavily constrained; historical figures and events are actively discouraged or inverted in public life.
- Education, employment, law, and institutions prioritize national guilt and social engineering over pride or continuity.
- Cultural reflexes and daily norms treat any observable expression of national pride as inherently illegitimate or harmful.

**10–19 (Guilt)**  
The dominant emotional stance tilts heavily toward collective guilt, apology, or active shame with systematic efforts to erase traditional national identity from public life.

- History is predominantly framed as oppression, crime, or failure; achievements are minimized or contextualized as accidental or tainted.
- Policy, education, and media emphasize atonement and the need to move beyond or dissolve traditional national identity.
- Large-scale removal of statues of historical figures replaced with unrelated or non-national individuals, widespread renaming of public spaces, and institutional rewards for anti-national narratives.

**0–10 (Condemnation)**  
The nation's entire history, symbols, and important figures are treated as completely evil and irredeemable; any expression of national pride is both socially pathologized and legally outlawed. The nation spends a large amount of both time and resources attempting to rectify its past sins.

- **6–10** = Education and culture actively promote disgust toward the nation's past; pride is taboo or pathologized.
- **1–5** = Any positive national sentiment is actively suppressed; the system's policy and narrative orientation is one of collective guilt, apology, and suppression of national identity expressions.
- **0** = The nation and all its symbols are treated as inherently evil or irredeemable; any expression of national pride is pathologized as dangerous and systematically eradicated from all public, educational, and cultural domains.

# Cultural 3 – Exclusivity
## Ethnic Exclusivity

### 1. Definition
*Note that this slider is intended to be scored in conjunction with Core 3B, Cultural Exclusivity.*

Ethnic Exclusivity measures the strictness of the ancestral / blood requirement for full membership of the core in-group. It covers the difficulty and extent of ancestral purity demanded, together with the degree of exclusion based on non-group ancestry or the degree of inclusion based on any ancestral connection to the group.

Scoring uses only observable reality concerning the ethnic / ancestral criterion for full membership. Immigration, deportation, resident non-members, and all cultural requirements are out of scope.

### 2. Avoidance Rules
Do not use as evidence:
- Cultural adoption, language, loyalty, or way-of-life requirements (these belong to 3B Cultural)
- Immigration volume or border control
- Deportation
- Treatment of resident non-members
- Any non-ethnic pathway to membership

### 3. Scoring Bands

#### 90–100 — One-Drop Exclusion
One drop of non-group blood is disqualifying for full membership.

* **100**: Explicit One-Drop Exclusion. Any non-group ancestry bars full membership.
* **95–99**: Near One-Drop. Anything beyond the most marginal mixing results in exclusion.
* **90–94**: Mixed ancestry faces total or near-total barriers.

#### 80–89 — Strong Purity Preference
Significant foreign ancestry is heavily discouraged and produces lasting lower status.

* Interbreeding is heavily discouraged.
* Those who are not close to pure-blood are looked down upon even if formally accepted.
* Even relatively recent mixed ancestry remains a criterion for discrimination.

#### 70–79 — Dominant Ancestry Expectation
Group ancestry is expected to be the dominant element; substantial foreign contribution is a serious barrier.

* Interbreeding is not formally forbidden but is not regarded as fully legitimate.
* Traits or ancestry that are obviously substantially foreign are cause for discrimination or reduced standing.
* Only those whose foreign contribution is limited and not dominant are fully embraced.

#### 60–69 — Core Ancestry Preference
Mixed people are accepted, but those of predominantly group ancestry are clearly preferred.

* Interbreeding is accepted but not encouraged.
* Those with substantial foreign ancestry are accepted but disadvantaged relative to those of core ancestry.
* Obvious foreign traits are not automatically disqualifying.

#### 51–59 — Minimal Ancestry Threshold
Some demonstrable group ancestry is required; once present, foreign ancestry is not itself a barrier.

* Mixed-ethnicity people are accepted provided there is a clear ancestral connection to the group.
* The required connection can be relatively limited.
* Interbreeding itself is fully acceptable.

#### 50 — Inverse One-Drop Rule
One drop of our blood makes you us.

* Any ancestral connection, even distant or partial, is sufficient for full membership.
* Individuals with remote or fractional group ancestry are treated as already belonging or as having a strong presumptive claim to full membership.

#### 41–49 — Related Ethnicities
Full membership is open to the core ethnicity and to ethnic groups that share the same origins.

* Any person of the core ethnicity is accepted, regardless of how distant or limited the connection.
* Ethnic groups that split from the core group in the past are also accepted.
* Example: two tribes that diverged centuries ago and have had no contact since.

#### 31–40 — Ethnic Similarity
Full membership is open to the core ethnicity and to groups that are ethnically similar, even when they do not share recent common origins.

* Any person of the core ethnicity is accepted.
* People who are ethnically similar but not directly related are also accepted.
* Example: two populations both classified as “black” that originated in different continents and diverged before some populations classified as “white”.

#### 21–30 — Historic Alliance
Full membership is open to ethnic groups that have historic alliances or long-standing connections with the core group, even without shared ancestry.

* Membership is granted on the basis of historic ethnic relations and alliance, not genetic relatedness.
* Example: the Gurkhas are not ethnically related to the British but are accepted because of their historic military service and alliance with Britain.

#### 11–20 — Residual Ethnic Restrictions
The ethnic criterion now operates mainly through targeted exclusions rather than broad inclusions.

* Specific ethnic groups against which historic grudges or prejudices exist are excluded.
* The set of accepted ethnicities is wide, but certain named groups remain barred.
* Example: laws that explicitly discriminate against people of English heritage in Scotland.

#### 0–10 — Residual to Zero Genetic Role
Ancestry declines from narrow residual relevance to complete irrelevance.

* **6–10**: Ancestry retains residual meaning. Targeted restrictions or preferences based on ancestry exist in narrow domains.
* **1–5**: Ancestry retains residual meaning in very specific, narrowly targeted restrictions only. Outside those narrow cases, ancestry has no operational role in full membership.
* **0**: Absolute genetic indifference. Ancestry has zero role. No ancestral criterion exists in law, institutions, public definition, or practice. The system treats genetic similarity as irrelevant to full membership.

# Cultural 4A: Social Determinism

**Source Document**  
**Date:** July 16, 2026 (Rewrite)

## Cultural 4A Definition

This guideline measures the observable degree to which circumstances of birth (race, class, social status, family background, or inherited group membership) limit, restrict, or guide a person's life outcomes and the degree to which people are able to improve their status and/or the status of their descendants through their own actions.

It tracks whether social standing, occupation, education access, roles, expectations, and opportunities are determined or constrained primarily by birth circumstances or by individual actions, effort, competence, and merit-based mechanisms.

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable patterns in law, education admissions and curricula, employment and promotion practices, family and inheritance policy, institutional decision-making, public rituals, media narratives, and real-world transmission of status and opportunities) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

High scores indicate strong social determinism: birth circumstances assign or rigidly constrain standing, jobs, education, and roles.  
Low scores indicate high mobility and agency: birth circumstances have minimal lasting impact and individuals can improve status through actions.

## Scoring Bands

### 90–100: Near-absolute social determinism
Birth assigns highly specific hereditary roles + birth-order rules. Almost no residual choice or mobility.

- **100**: Every aspect of a person’s life is determined by birth-related factors. The individual has no meaningful choice or agency over their life trajectory.
- **95–99**: Birth determines highly specific occupational and social niches. Only the most marginal agency exists around extremely minor issues.
- **90–94**: Exact occupational and social apsects are inherited by birth-related factors. Individual actions produce virtually no change in life trajectory or descendant status, and no legitimate pathways exist out of the assigned aspect.

### 80–89: High social determinism – heavy restrictions both intra-class and inter-class
Broad classes exist (manual, trades, commercial, professional, landed) but movement both *between* and *inside* them is heavily restricted.

- Birth determines broad class membership as the standard rule.
- Intra-class options are limited by family, guild, or custom expectations.
- Crossing into a different broad class is rare and usually only into the lowest rank of the next class.

### 70–79: Predominantly deterministic – real intra-class mobility, low inter-class mobility
Real mobility exists *inside* the broad class, but inter-class mobility remains low to negligible.

- Birth still locks a person into a major class.
- Inside that class there is genuine scope to rise or fall through effort, skill, or luck.
- Crossing major class boundaries remains exceptional.

### 60–69: Moderate social determinism – inter-class possible but hard, intra-class still limited
Inter-class movement becomes possible (though difficult and with headwinds). Intra-class mobility remains relatively low.

- Moving between major classes is achievable with significant effort but remains uncommon.
- Once inside a class, birth and early advantages still heavily shape internal position and ease of further rise.
- Clear differential ease of options by birth remains.

### 51–59: Noticeable shift – both inter-class and intra-class mobility realistic but still restricted
Both directions of mobility are now realistic and used, yet birth still creates routine and consequential restrictions on ease and options.

- Significant effort can produce movement both between and within classes.
- Birth-linked advantages and disadvantages remain visible and important across multiple domains.

### 41–50: Blocked access + residual discrimination after success
Access to better paths is largely blocked; people must mostly self-create or force their own way.  
Even after succeeding through effort, residual birth-based discrimination or incomplete acceptance remains.

- Better education, professions, or status tracks are not freely open; individuals must largely create their own path.
- Success is possible through exceptional effort, but the person who succeeds still faces residual stigma, incomplete acceptance, or differential treatment based on birth origin.
- Family transmission of improved status remains partial because of the residual discrimination.

### 31–40: Blocked access + equal treatment after success
Access to better paths is largely blocked; people must mostly self-create or force their own way.  
Once they succeed through effort, they are fully respected and treated equally — no residual birth stigma.

- Better education, professions, or status tracks are not freely open; individuals must largely create their own path.
- Success through effort is recognised and rewarded equally; no residual birth-based discrimination remains after achievement.
- Improved status can be transmitted to descendants without birth-origin penalties.

### 21–30: Access exists but is difficult / not easy
Equal access exists in theory and is formally open to all, but in practice the restrictions, costs, and requirements make the same opportunities substantially more difficult for some birth groups to use.

- Formal and legal access is the same for everyone.
- In practice, disadvantaged birth groups face higher barriers of cost, time, complexity, or required prior resources that make using the opportunity significantly harder.
- The path remains open and achievable, but it is not equally easy or frictionless.
- Example: schooling or professional entry is legally available to all, yet the practical requirements (fees, unpaid time, location, prior preparation, or signalling costs) create a markedly steeper climb for some birth groups.

### 11–20: Universal access – only quality differs
Access exists in practice for all groups. The only remaining birth effect is systematic difference in the quality of the opportunity.

- Every birth group can actually use the main pathways (schooling, professions, credentials, etc.) without extraordinary barriers.
- Birth circumstances systematically affect the quality of what is received (better schools versus standard schools, better early starts, higher-value opportunities, better networks that require costly but open signalling, better healthcare and nutrition, etc.).
- Costly-but-open advantages are treated as quality differences, not as access barriers.
- No meaningful residual barriers to entry remain; only quality gradients exist.

### 0–10: Absolute high mobility / minimal social determinism
There are almost no differences in access or opportunities between different birth groups.

- **6–10**: Any minor differences that remain are accidental or practical rather than intentional or systematic.
- **1–5**: No measurable birth-linked differences remain that the system can meaningfully address. The only differences left are those that are functionally impossible to remove or that lie entirely outside the system’s control.
- **0**: Access and opportunities are perfectly equal for everyone, with no differences in quality. Every person’s starting options and outcomes are determined solely by their own actions, and no birth-based advantages or restrictions are transmitted.

**End of Cultural 4A: Social Determinism**

## Core Definition

This guideline measures the observable framework a system uses to explain and address differential group outcomes — the degree to which it attributes disparities primarily to systemic/intersectional oppression, historical group-based factors, or identity-based disadvantages (requiring equity-based interventions for mandated equal outcomes) versus attributing them primarily to individual actions, merit, effort, competence, or personal responsibility (requiring only equality of opportunity/treatment).

---

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone
Absolute or near-absolute equity lens enforcement across all domains, with zero tolerance for individual-merit or equality-of-opportunity frameworks. This allows observable variation within different equity systems (different mechanisms of group correction while retaining total rejection of merit-based explanations).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal enforcement of equity-based group corrections in every domain with no merit or opportunity exceptions or rhetoric permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total equity enforcement; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined merit accommodations permitted.

### 80–89 (High – Strict equity/oppression framework enforcement)
- Laws and institutions explicitly mandate group-preference policies and systemic corrections (e.g., mandatory quotas, equity audits, identity-based redistribution with no opt-outs).
- Education and employment decisions prioritize group outcomes and intersectional corrections with zero individual-merit exceptions.
- Media and public rituals openly celebrate equity interventions and frame all disparities as systemic oppression.
- Statistical outcomes show strong enforcement of group equalization with active sanctions against merit-based selection.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Predominantly equity lens with limited exceptions)
- Core domains maintain equity corrections as primary but allow narrow, revocable merit policies in secondary areas.
- Group-outcome frameworks remain visible and culturally reinforced; individual-merit explanations are limited to specific issues only.
- Family and inheritance policy incorporates equity-leveling mechanisms.
- Public discourse and institutions treat equity interventions as necessary and beneficial rather than inherently problematic.

### 60–69 (Mid – Contested equity lens with significant merit exceptions)
Equity corrections are actively implemented in some major domains but coexist with substantial, observable merit-based systems in others, creating visible institutional and cultural tension.

- Group-preference policies, reserved places, or identity-based scoring are applied in university admissions and public-sector hiring.
- Mandatory equity audits, unconscious-bias training, and disparity monitoring requirements are imposed on large institutions and corporations.
- Legal challenges, public campaigns, and policy reversals regularly limit the scope or enforcement of equity mandates.
- Media and public discourse show ongoing debate between systemic-oppression explanations and individual-responsibility frameworks without either side achieving dominance.
- Statistical outcomes demonstrate partial movement toward group parity in targeted sectors alongside persistent competence-based differentiation in competitive or technical fields.

### 51–59 (Mid–Lower – Noticeable but contested equity push)
Equity frameworks gain observable traction in multiple core domains yet encounter organised resistance and inconsistent application.

- Soft quotas, diversity goals, or identity considerations are implemented in education admissions and public employment.
- Mandatory equity reporting requirements and bias–audit procedures are introduced across government agencies and large private organisations.
- Media narratives increasingly favour systemic explanations of disparities while still featuring prominent individual-merit counter-arguments.
- Statistical outcomes show early measurable shifts toward group equalization in selected areas accompanied by visible merit-based exceptions and pushback.

### 41–50 (Mid-Low – Clear shift toward individual-merit / opportunity frameworks; equity interventions recede in scope and institutional priority)
- Individual-merit and equality-of-opportunity policies gain measurable dominance in multiple core domains through legislation, court rulings, or administrative changes (e.g., scaling back or removal of quotas, shift to selection criteria based solely on individual qualifications and performance metrics, legal or policy restrictions on explicit group preferences).
- Equity structures and mandates have their scope narrowed through new legislation, binding court rulings, or administrative practice; they are no longer default requirements in core institutional processes.
- Public rituals, media narratives, and institutional discourse prominently feature individual responsibility, competence, and personal agency as the primary explanations for outcomes; group-based correction frameworks receive reduced institutional endorsement.
- Family and inheritance rules prioritize transmission based on personal achievement and documented individual performance rather than equity redistribution mechanisms.
- Statistical outcomes show measurable slowing or reversal of prior group-parity targets in favor of differentiation based on individual-level performance metrics in a growing number of sectors.

### 31–40 (Low-Mid – Individual merit dominant in core domains; equity mechanisms reduced to residual, narrowly targeted, or voluntary roles)
- Selection and allocation rules based on individual qualifications and performance metrics constitute the primary operating framework across most institutional and policy domains (admissions, hiring, promotion, contracting).
- Equity interventions persist only in limited, narrowly defined niches (e.g., small targeted programs or voluntary corporate initiatives) and are subject to routine political opposition, legal challenges, and administrative scrutiny.
- Public discourse, education content, and institutional communications promote explanations centered on individual responsibility and critique group-preference mechanisms on grounds of inconsistency with individual-outcome allocation.
- Observable institutional rules and procedures favor selection processes based solely on individual qualifications and performance metrics; group-outcome equalization requirements are deprioritized or rolled back in core operational areas.
- Statistical trends show increasing weight assigned to individual-level performance factors in outcomes alongside declining institutional emphasis on systemic group correction targets.

### 21–30 (Low – Strong enforcement of individual merit and equality of opportunity; equity structures actively dismantled or heavily constrained)
- Promotion of individual frameworks is primary: explicit policy or legal barriers prohibit group-based corrections in most public and large private contexts; mandatory equal-treatment rules that exclude identity preferences are standard.
- Equity mechanisms (quotas, audits, preferential scoring) are systematically dismantled, defunded, legally prohibited, or subjected to strict oversight and justification requirements wherever they previously operated.
- Education policy, employment rules, law, and institutional governance require allocation decisions to be based on individual merit, effort, and competence metrics as the legitimate basis.
- Institutional and professional consequences (sanctions, loss of funding, legal liability, or public criticism tied to fairness and individual merit standards) apply to the use of equity-style group corrections in core domains.
- Media coverage and public rituals prominently feature merit-based success cases and subject identity-based redistribution mechanisms to sustained critical examination on empirical outcome grounds.

### 11–20 (Very Low – Merit frameworks primary across nearly all domains; equity corrections subject to prohibition or severe institutional restriction with minimal tolerated exceptions)
- Almost all observable allocation mechanisms (admissions, hiring, promotion, contracting, benefits) operate under rules requiring individual merit or equal-opportunity criteria, with active enforcement mechanisms against the use of group-identity factors.
- Any residual equity-style interventions face prohibition, severe institutional resistance, binding legal restrictions, or requirements for exceptional justification; they are confined to the most marginal or explicitly exempted private spheres.
- Education curricula, media narratives, and public policy documents frame group-based equity approaches as inconsistent with individual-outcome allocation rules and subject them to institutional discouragement or prohibition.
- Statistical outcomes and institutional practice demonstrate near-complete reliance on competence- and effort-based differentiation; group-parity engineering programs are absent from core public and institutional operations or subject to outright prohibition.
- Institutional rules, professional standards, and public communications reinforce that outcomes are to be determined by individual qualifications and performance rather than group identity-based adjustments.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute individual merit / equality of opportunity) — Compressed zone
Total rejection of all equity lenses with universal enforcement of individual-based outcomes regardless of group identity. This allows observable variation within different merit systems (different mechanisms of equal opportunity while retaining zero tolerance for group corrections).

- **6–10** = Extreme individual-merit enforcement: equity lenses treated as incompatible with institutional rules; widespread merit-based selection and prohibition of group preferences dominate most core domains with severe sanctions (legal, financial, or professional) for any deviation.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute individual-merit enforcement: equality of opportunity enforced across nearly all domains through binding rules; only marginal, explicitly justified group-accommodation exceptions exist under strict oversight.
- **0** = Absolute individual-merit enforcement: zero tolerance for any equity correction whatsoever; all disparities, status, authority, resource allocation, and outcomes are required to be determined solely on individual merit and performance metrics with total institutional prohibition of group-based interventions.

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone

### 70–79 (Mid-High – Predominantly traditional with limited exceptions)

### 60–69 (Mid – Tradition dominant in core domains with strong progressive pressures in education, media, and secondary policy areas)

- Progressive policies and reframing of tradition as requiring reform achieve dominance in several core domains (e.g., education content, some family law reforms, public symbolism) but encounter organized institutional or legal resistance in others.

- Historical symbols, narratives, and institutions are actively discouraged, renamed, repurposed, or subjected to requirements for alignment with progressive frameworks through policy or institutional action.
- Daily norms, institutional culture, and professional standards require observable adherence to tradition to be accompanied by justification, reform commitments, or adaptation to progressive objectives.
- Progressive transformation constitutes the near-default institutional and policy approach; residual traditional elements persist primarily in private spheres or under explicit exemptions with ongoing oversight.

### 11–20 (Very Low – Near-absolute progressivist enforcement in practice; tradition subject to prohibition, severe restriction, or requirements for complete alignment with progressive frameworks across nearly all observable domains)
- Almost all institutional and cultural mechanisms prioritize the dismantling, restriction, or reform of traditional continuity; any public or official expression of traditional norms is subject to significant professional, legal, regulatory, or institutional consequences unless accompanied by explicit alignment with progressive objectives.
- Education, media, law, and governance operate under a dominant future-oriented framework with minimal institutional space for unmodified traditional continuity.
- Statistical and observable outcomes show near-complete displacement or transformation of traditional transmission patterns by engineered progressive alternatives in core domains.
- Institutional rules, professional standards, and public communications require traditional identity, heritage practices, or transmission mechanisms to be adapted, reformed, or aligned with system objectives; unmodified traditional continuity faces institutional marginalization or prohibition in most public contexts.

### 0–10 (Floor – Absolute enforced progressivism) — Compressed zone
Traditions are treated as incompatible with system requirements. Institutional rules prohibit or severely restrict traditional practices and transmission, regardless of historical continuity, customs, or inherited structures. This allows observable variation within different progressivist systems (different mechanisms of reform, restriction, or replacement while retaining zero tolerance for unmodified traditional continuity).

- **6–10** = Extreme enforced progressivism: tradition treated as incompatible with institutional rules; widespread dismantling policies, innovation mandates, and requirements for alignment with progressive frameworks dominate most core domains with severe sanctions (legal, financial, professional, or institutional) for unmodified traditional practices.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute enforced progressivism: mandatory progressive transformation and alignment requirements enforced across nearly all domains with only marginal, explicitly justified exemptions under heavy monitoring and oversight.
- **0** = Absolute enforced progressivism: zero tolerance for any unmodified traditional continuity whatsoever; all norms, institutions, and historical transmission mechanisms are required to be reformed, restricted, or replaced in accordance with progressive frameworks, with total institutional prohibition and monitoring of deviation.

**End of Cultural 5A: Tradition vs Progress**

# Cultural 5B: Radicalism (Pace and Intensity of Change)

## Core Definition

This guideline measures the observable pace and intensity with which a system pursues cultural, institutional, and social change. 

It tracks the degree to which change occurs through rapid, revolutionary, disruptive, total, or accelerated transformations that break with existing structures and impose new orders with minimal delay (radicalism) versus incremental, evolutionary, cautious, tested, or restorative modifications that maintain stability and continuity (gradualism). 

Scoring is based exclusively on empirical reality (observable timelines of policy implementation, legislative processes, institutional responses to reform proposals, enforcement mechanisms for change speed, education curricula framing of historical change, media narratives about progress tempo, public rituals, cultural output, and daily norms) — never on claims, justifications, official rhetoric, or moral framing.

---

## Scoring Bands

### High-End Guideline (90–100) — Compressed zone (Maximum Radicalism)
Constant, immediate, and total change is the required operating mode across all domains, with zero tolerance for deliberation, delay, pilot testing, or gradual implementation. This allows observable variation within different radical systems (different mechanisms of acceleration and enforcement while retaining total rejection of any incremental or stability-preserving processes).

- **100** = Total, explicit, and universal requirement of constant immediate/instant change in every domain with no exceptions, delays, or gradual processes permitted.
- **95–99** = Near-total radicalism; only the most marginal, temporary, and narrowly defined pauses or reviews are permitted before further immediate change is enforced.
- **90–94** = Extreme radicalism: constant immediate transformation dominates law, education, institutions, and culture with maximum enforcement against any form of delay, deliberation, or incremental sequencing.

### 80–89 (High – Strong institutional requirement for rapid and total change)
Rapid, disruptive, and comprehensive change is the dominant and actively enforced mode across most domains. Any processes that slow implementation are systematically overridden or eliminated.

- Law and institutions are structured to enable and require near-immediate implementation of major reforms with minimal or no consultation, pilot programs, or review periods.
- Education curricula and institutional training emphasize the necessity of rapid, total transformation and frame any delay or caution as unacceptable.
- Media and public discourse present urgency, norm-breaking, and immediate overhaul as the only legitimate responses; gradual or deliberative approaches are marginalised or sanctioned.
- Policy and institutional practice show near-constant initiation and implementation of transformative changes with little time between major initiatives.

### 70–79 (Mid–High – Clear institutional preference for accelerated change with limited tolerance for process)
Accelerated and disruptive change is the primary mode, though some limited procedural steps may still occur under pressure or in secondary areas.

- Most major reforms are implemented on compressed timelines; institutional mechanisms designed to slow or sequence change are routinely bypassed or weakened.
- Education and media narratives strongly favor urgency and transformative action over deliberation or evidence-based sequencing.
- Occasional requirements for consultation or review exist but are narrow in scope and frequently overridden when they conflict with speed.
- Observable policy output shows high volume of rapid, wide-reaching changes with minimal intervals between initiatives.

### 60–69 (Mid – Significant acceleration is routine; some residual procedural constraints remain)
Rapid change occurs as standard practice in many core domains, while some institutional or procedural checks still operate in limited areas.

- Fast-track procedures, emergency powers, or direct executive/administrative action are routinely used for major policy changes.
- Institutional resistance to rapid implementation is present but frequently ineffective or selectively applied.
- Education curricula and media content present rapid transformation as normal and necessary, while gradual approaches receive less emphasis.
- Statistical and observable patterns show frequent large-scale changes implemented on short timelines across multiple sectors.

### 51–59 (Mid–Lower – Accelerated change is common and increasingly dominant)
Rapid or disruptive methods are used regularly for significant reforms, though some domains still retain more measured processes.

- Pilot programmes and extended review processes are frequently shortened, bypassed, or applied only selectively.
- Legislative and institutional processes show increasing use of accelerated procedures for high-priority changes.
- Media and education narratives increasingly frame urgency and rapid action as necessary and positive.
- Institutional governance demonstrates growing tolerance for compressed timelines and reduced consultation on major initiatives.

### 41–50 (Low–Mid – Multiple domains operate under accelerated change regimes)
Rapid and disruptive approaches are established as routine in several key domains, while gradualist mechanisms persist in others.

- Major societal changes are regularly implemented on noticeably compressed timelines with limited public or institutional consultation.
- Institutional mechanisms designed to slow or sequence change (consultation periods, impact assessments, review boards) are selectively weakened or overridden in priority areas.
- Cultural output and education content present rapid transformation as normal and frame extended deliberation as inefficient.
- Observable patterns show a clear mix: accelerated change dominant in some core domains alongside more measured processes in others.

### 31–40 (Low – Accelerated and disruptive change is widespread)
Rapid, total, or revolutionary-style changes occur regularly across many domains, with gradualist processes becoming secondary or marginal.

- Shock reforms, large-scale overhauls, or rapid resets are implemented as standard practice in multiple sectors.
- Legislative and institutional processes are frequently redesigned or operated to prioritize speed over extended deliberation.
- Media, education, and public discourse present rapid change as the expected norm and treat extended review or caution as exceptional.
- Cultural rituals and daily institutional practice accommodate and facilitate high volumes of disruptive or accelerated initiatives.

### 21–30 (Low – Radical/accelerated methods are dominant; gradualist processes are constrained)
Accelerated and disruptive change constitutes the primary mode across most domains; processes that require significant time or deliberation are actively limited or overridden.

- Most major policy and institutional initiatives proceed through rapid implementation channels; deliberative or pilot-based approaches are routinely deprioritized or bypassed.
- Institutional resistance to fast change is weak or systematically circumvented through procedural changes or political pressure.
- Education, media, and institutional communications emphasize urgency and immediate action; extended deliberation is presented as an obstacle.
- Observable implementation patterns show near-constant momentum of transformative changes with minimal intervals for evaluation or consensus.

### 11–20 (Very Low – Near-constant accelerated change; gradualist mechanisms are minimal or token)
Rapid and disruptive transformation is the near-default mode; any remaining gradualist or deliberative processes are marginal, heavily criticized, or quickly overridden.

- Almost all significant change occurs through accelerated or top-down mechanisms; traditional deliberative processes exist only in limited or symbolic forms.
- Institutional culture operates under a strong expectation of rapid implementation; requirements for extended review or sequencing are minimal or routinely waived.
- Media and education narratives treat rapid transformation as the normal and necessary state; caution or extended process receives little institutional support.
- Daily norms and public rituals reinforce the expectation of frequent, large-scale change with limited space for stability or slow adaptation.

### 0–10 (Floor – Maximum Gradualism / Pure Stagnation) — Compressed zone
No meaningful change occurs. The system maintains existing structures, norms, and practices with zero tolerance for adaptation, evolution, reform, or transformation of any kind. This allows observable variation within different highly stable or stagnant systems (different mechanisms of preserving the status quo while retaining total rejection of any change).

- **6–10** = Extreme gradualism/stagnation: existing structures and norms are preserved with near-total resistance to any modification; any proposals for change are systematically blocked, delayed indefinitely, or rendered ineffective.
- **1–5** = Near-absolute gradualism/stagnation: the system maintains existing arrangements across nearly all domains with only marginal, symbolic, or ineffective allowances for minor adjustments under heavy constraints.
- **0** = Absolute gradualism/stagnation: zero tolerance for any change whatsoever; all domains are locked in their current state with total institutional, legal, and cultural prohibition of adaptation, reform, or evolution of any kind.

**End of Cultural 5B: Radicalism (Pace and Intensity of Change)**
